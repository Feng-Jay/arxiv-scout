## 📄 [Three Heads Are Better Than One: A Multi-perspective Reasoning Framework for Enhanced Vulnerability Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.18153v1)  `90% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Xin Peng, Bo Lin, Jing Wang, Xiaoling Li, Jun Ma, Jie Yu, Xiaoguang Mao, Shangwen Wang
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-18  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
ReasonVul is a multi-agent LLM framework for vulnerability detection that combines deductive, inductive, and abductive reasoning, then uses structured debate to reconcile disagreements. On PrimeVul it substantially outperforms prior baselines, especially on the strict PairAcc metric, and generalizes to JITVUL.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces a three-perspective reasoning framework for vulnerability detection
- Designs a debate-and-revision mechanism for resolving conflicting agent judgments
- Shows large gains over SOTA learning-based and LLM-based baselines on PrimeVul
- Demonstrates cross-dataset generalization on JITVUL
- Analyzes conflict cases to evidence the value of collaborative error correction

### ⚙️ Methodology
ReasonVul models vulnerability detection as collaborative reasoning over source code. Three specialized LLM agents independently inspect the same code snippet, each emulating a distinct reasoning style: deductive (rule/standards-based), inductive (pattern-from-examples), and abductive (hypothesis/explanation-driven). Each agent produces an intermediate verdict plus justification; if their conclusions differ, a debate phase is triggered where agents critique one another, may rebut or revise their own judgments, and iterate toward a final collaborative decision. The paper frames the design around cognitive synergy, aiming to combine complementary strengths that single-paradigm prompting misses.

### 🧪 Experiments
Evaluated on PrimeVul using PairAcc and F1, compared against eight baselines including LineVul, Coca, MoEVD, SAVul, Vul-RAG, VulnSage, GPTLens, and VulTrial. ReasonVul achieves 40.00% PairAcc and 72.52% F1, improving PairAcc by 81.24% over the best baseline (VulTrial) and F1 by 29.09%. On JITVUL it attains 28.67% PairAcc, supporting generalization. The authors also analyze 542 disagreement cases, reporting 389 correctly resolved by the debate mechanism, versus about 9% with majority voting.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The main insight is that vulnerability detection benefits from explicitly combining multiple reasoning paradigms rather than relying on one prompt style. The strong conflict-resolution results suggest debate can surface and correct hidden errors, but the approach likely depends on prompt/agent design and LLM quality, and the paper does not fully isolate which vulnerability classes benefit most. Open questions include cost/latency of multi-agent debate, robustness across models, and whether similar gains hold in broader real-world codebases or repair-oriented settings.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is directly relevant to automated vulnerability detection and software security, and it advances the field by moving beyond single-pass LLM prompting toward multi-perspective reasoning. Its debate-based error-correction mechanism is especially interesting for researchers in vulnerability detection/repair because it suggests a path to more reliable identification of hard, context-dependent flaws.

## 📄 [Hunting Vulnerability Variants in AI Infra: Measurement and Reference-Driven Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.20051v1)  `80% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Tian Dong, Yanjun Chen, Shoufeng Zhang, Huaien Zhang, Yunlong Lyu, Keke Lian, Dong Zhang, Shaofeng Li, Hao Chen
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-19  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper shows that AI infrastructure repositories often share functionality and recurring vulnerability patterns, so a disclosed bug in one project frequently has semantically similar variants in others. To exploit this observation, the authors build InfraScope, a reference-driven multi-agent auditing framework that finds cross-repository vulnerability variants and uncovers more than 20 real-world vulnerabilities, including 11 acknowledged cases and 4 assigned CVEs.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- A measurement study over 688 AI infra repositories and 251 public vulnerabilities showing strong functional overlap and recurrent vulnerable trigger mechanisms across projects.
- A formalization of reference-driven vulnerability variant auditing, where a known disclosed vulnerability serves as the semantic reference for finding equivalent variants in related repositories.
- The design and implementation of InfraScope, a multi-agent framework with dedicated agents for vulnerability semantic modeling, functional-context localization, and exploitability verification.
- An empirical evaluation on 8 reference vulnerabilities and 20 target repositories demonstrating strong precision/accuracy and real-world impact through the discovery of over 20 zero-day vulnerabilities, including acknowledged cases and assigned CVEs.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The paper tackles post-disclosure auditing in AI infrastructure by assuming that vulnerabilities recur not mainly through code cloning, but through reimplementation of similar model-centric workflows such as model loading, adapter handling, checkpoint fetching, deserialization, tool invocation, and agent orchestration. The authors define a reference vulnerability as a known disclosed case and a vulnerability variant as a semantically equivalent flaw appearing in another repository with similar functional context.

InfraScope is a reference-driven multi-agent auditing framework built to operationalize this idea. Its workflow has three coordinated stages. First, a semantic modeling agent analyzes the reference vulnerability and extracts transferable vulnerability semantics, including trigger conditions, attacker-controlled inputs, data/control propagation paths, privileged operations, and functional context. It also builds an inspection-oriented representation of the target repository by decomposing it into functional modules and module-level dependencies, rather than relying only on file-level similarity.

Second, an inspection/localization agent uses the reference semantics to prioritize target modules that implement related AI infra functionality. It performs localization-aware search under bounded LLM context by maintaining compact external audit state, such as which files have been inspected, which hypotheses have been rejected, and which candidate flows remain plausible. This stateful design is intended to avoid wasted exploration, reduce token costs, and preserve reasoning continuity across multiple sessions. The framework emphasizes functional-context matching rather than signature matching, so the agent can recognize semantically similar but syntactically different implementations.

Third, a target-side verification agent validates candidate variants using concrete repository facts and automated proof-of-concept generation. This component checks whether the suspected path is actually exploitable before reporting it, which is meant to reduce hallucinated findings and unsupported conclusions. Overall, InfraScope is implemented as a controlled auditing harness around tool-augmented LLM agents, explicitly designed for cross-repository variant hunting after vulnerability disclosure. The implementation consists of more than 29K lines of code.

### 🧪 Experiments
The study has two main empirical parts: a measurement study and a detection evaluation.

For measurement, the authors collect 688 AI infra repositories from GitHub and 251 publicly disclosed vulnerabilities, of which 232 contain sufficient trigger details for variant analysis. They analyze ecosystem growth, language distribution, capability-family concentration, project similarity, and recurring vulnerable patterns. The repository sample spans capability families such as agent orchestration, serving/inference, RAG/data, evaluation/observability, platform/training, and UI/workflows. The study reports rapid growth of the ecosystem, with project creation increasing from 124 repositories in 2023 to 142 in 2024, 262 in 2025, and 148 already in Q1 2026. Agent orchestration is the largest family with 373 projects, and Python is the dominant language, appearing in 356 of 688 projects.

For vulnerability-variant detection, InfraScope is evaluated on 8 reference vulnerabilities and 20 real-world target repositories. In the benchmark, InfraScope reports 31 candidates, 24 of which are true positives. The paper states that InfraScope achieves the best precision and accuracy among compared tools. It is also compared with Claude Code under the same references, using more than 7x fewer tokens while finding only 4% fewer true positives, showing a strong efficiency/accuracy tradeoff. Beyond benchmark numbers, the framework discovers over 20 vulnerabilities across the benchmark and an OpenClaw case study, including 11 acknowledged cases and 4 that had received CVE assignments at the time of writing.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The central insight is that AI infra is an unusually favorable domain for cross-repository variant hunting because many projects independently recreate the same privileged workflows around model loading, remote artifact retrieval, deserialization, command execution, and tool integration. This means vulnerability transfer is often semantic and workflow-driven rather than clone-driven, which exposes a gap in traditional variant detection methods based on code reuse, patch lineage, or fixed signatures.

A second key implication is that LLM-based repository auditing becomes substantially more practical when anchored by a known reference vulnerability and constrained by explicit functional-context reasoning. Instead of asking a general-purpose agent to discover arbitrary bugs, the paper shows value in narrowing the task to preserving trigger semantics across related repositories.

The work also highlights practical limitations of current LLM auditing: limited interprocedural reasoning depth, bounded context windows, and hallucination risk. InfraScope addresses these with state externalization, module-aware localization, and exploitability verification, but these challenges likely remain important when scaling to larger repositories or more subtle bug classes.

Potential limitations and open questions include how well the approach generalizes outside AI infra, whether it can handle vulnerabilities whose semantics depend heavily on deployment/runtime environment rather than repository code, and how robust the extracted semantics are for highly novel or composite vulnerabilities. Another open area is automated repair: once a variant is found, the same reference-driven semantic model might be extended to suggest patches or verify patch completeness across sibling repositories.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to software security, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair research because it studies how known disclosed vulnerabilities propagate as variants across independently developed repositories and provides a practical detection framework for finding them. For a researcher interested in software engineering and maintenance, it also offers a compelling methodology for post-disclosure ecosystem auditing in rapidly evolving AI software stacks, with direct implications for scalable variant analysis, patch auditing, and eventually reference-guided automated repair.

## 📄 [Measuring Safety Alignment Effects in Autonomous Security Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.19722v1)  `80% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Isaac David, Arthur Gervais
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-19  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR, cs.AI

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper introduces a system-level benchmark for evaluating how safety alignment affects autonomous security agents performing authorized, sandboxed vulnerability-analysis tasks. It finds that less-restricted Gemma derivatives substantially outperform aligned Gemma models on these tasks, but the effect is inconsistent across model families, showing that refusal alone is an inadequate safety metric compared with system-level measures like grounding, tool reliability, and unsafe-action monitoring.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces a trace-based benchmark of 30 local, sandboxed vulnerability-analysis tasks with deterministic success predicates, redaction rules, grounding checks, and labeled failure modes for autonomous security agents.
- Provides a cross-family paired evaluation of aligned versus less-restricted models across Gemma 4 31B, Gemma 4 26B A4B, Qwen2.5-Coder 7B, and Llama 3.1 8B under shared agent harnesses, seeds, tools, and budgets.
- Demonstrates that observed performance differences cannot be explained by refusal rates alone, and must instead be decomposed into refusal, unsafe action, tool-protocol reliability, evidence grounding, and ordinary capability differences.
- Adds non-security sanity and coding controls plus grounding metrics and blind audit procedures to distinguish security-specific effects from broader coding-performance or derivative-provenance effects.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The study designs a contained autonomous security-agent evaluation framework in which language models operate over local repositories and fixed tools inside authorized sandboxes. The benchmark consists of 30 vulnerability-analysis tasks spanning vulnerability localization, reachability validation, proof-of-trigger generation, patch verification, and remediation reporting. Each task is specified through a machine-readable manifest that defines the local fixture, permitted tools, execution budget, deterministic success predicate, redaction policy, and expected evidence targets. The agent controller is fixed across comparable runs so that differences can be attributed primarily to the model condition rather than orchestration changes.

The evaluation compares four aligned-versus-less-restricted model pairs: Gemma 4 31B, Gemma 4 26B A4B, Qwen2.5-Coder 7B, and Llama 3.1 8B. For Gemma 31B, the aligned model is also tested under multiple prompt settings: a standard prompt, an explicit defensive-authorization prompt, and a jailbreak-style prompt used only as exploratory stress testing. All runs share the same tools, seeds, execution budgets, backend, and where possible quantization level.

A central methodological feature is trace-based analysis. Every run records model messages, tool invocations, phase transitions, refusals, suppressed actions, unsafe actions, and final outputs. This enables fine-grained attribution of failure causes, separating visible refusal from localization failures, protocol misuse, validation failures, and report-generation errors. The benchmark also includes evidence-grounding checks: each task specifies expected files, symbols, issue terms, and local evidence terms, allowing post hoc scoring of whether the model actually inspected and cited relevant repository evidence rather than producing plausible but unsupported conclusions. The paper further includes non-security control tasks to test whether any aligned-versus-less-restricted gap is unique to security workflows or simply reflects broader coding or tool-use differences. The authors also emphasize derivative provenance as a confound, since uncensored or abliterated models may differ in more than refusal behavior alone.

### 🧪 Experiments
The experimental artifact contains 1,500 security-agent traces and 800 non-security control traces. Security evaluations are run across the four model families under paired conditions with shared tasks, seeds, budgets, and harness settings. The main metrics include deterministic task success rate, refusal rate, suppressed-action rate, unsafe-action rate, and evidence-grounding score on a five-point scale. The authors also analyze failure phases and compare results on non-security coding controls.

Key quantitative results show strong gains for less-restricted Gemma derivatives on security tasks. For Gemma 4 31B, the less-restricted derivative achieves 14.0% success versus 0.7% for the aligned model, with higher mean grounding of 3.91 versus 3.27 out of 5. The 31B traces also show 0.0% refusal, suppressed-action, and unsafe-action rates in the less-restricted condition. For Gemma 4 26B A4B, the less-restricted derivative achieves 10.7% success versus 0.0% for the aligned counterpart, with grounding 4.12 versus 1.64. However, the pattern does not generalize cleanly: for Qwen2.5-Coder 7B, the less-restricted derivative performs worse on security tasks at 2.0% success versus 5.3% for the aligned model, and the abliterated Llama 3.1 8B derivative performs poorly due to failure to follow the tool protocol. Non-security controls reveal that Gemma performance gaps also appear on ordinary coding tasks, weakening any claim that the gains are security-specific. Across all model families, difficult proof-of-trigger and patch-verification tasks remain unsolved, indicating a broader limitation in autonomous validation and repair workflows.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper’s main insight is that safety alignment effects in autonomous security agents are emergent system-level phenomena rather than simple refusal behaviors. A model can fail because it refuses, because it cannot use tools reliably, because it produces poorly grounded evidence, or because it lacks the capability to complete proof and validation steps. This is especially important for security research, where authorized defensive tasks often resemble disallowed offensive behavior at the prompt level.

The results caution against interpreting uncensored or abliterated models as uniformly better security agents. The Gemma gains are real in this benchmark, but similar gaps on non-security coding tasks suggest that the improvements may reflect broader behavioral or capability shifts rather than a pure reduction in security-specific refusals. The negative Qwen result and protocol-breaking Llama derivative further show that derivative provenance matters greatly; these models are not clean causal interventions on alignment alone.

A notable limitation is the low absolute success rates across all conditions, including the stronger Gemma derivatives. Hard tasks involving proof-of-trigger generation and patch verification remain unsolved, which implies that current autonomous security agents are still weak on the validation-heavy stages most relevant to trustworthy vulnerability detection and repair. Another limitation is external validity: the benchmark is local, authorized, and sandboxed, so the conclusions do not directly transfer to open-world deployment. Still, the benchmark advances evaluation methodology by offering deterministic checks, auditable traces, and grounding-aware scoring, which are valuable for future APR, vuln-detection, and secure-agent studies.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to research in software engineering, software maintenance, software security, automated program repair, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair because it evaluates end-to-end autonomous agents on realistic repository-level tasks such as vulnerability localization, trigger validation, patch verification, and remediation reporting. It is especially useful for researchers building LLM-based security or repair systems because it proposes a rigorous evaluation design with deterministic scoring, trace-level failure attribution, and grounding checks—precisely the kinds of methodology needed to assess whether an agent genuinely detects or repairs vulnerabilities rather than merely producing plausible text.

## 📄 [SCARA: A Semantics-Constrained Autonomous Remediation Agent for Opaque Industrial Software Vulnerabilities](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.19668v1)  `80% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Bowei Ning, Xuejun Zong, Lian Lian, Kan He, Guogang Wang, Yifei Sun, Jinyang Liu
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-19  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR, cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
SCARA is an end-to-end framework for remediating vulnerabilities in opaque industrial software when defenders lack source code, symbols, build systems, or hardware access. It combines operational-state-aware binary verification, tiered remediation synthesis, and conditional correctness validation, achieving 100% observed precision, rejecting infeasible alerts, and repairing most feasible cases on a new 15-case benchmark.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Defines the opaque industrial software (OIS) remediation problem under a source-unavailable defender model, including a nine-component operational state model, availability-based remediation tiers, reachability labels, and a conditional correctness notion.
- Introduces OSVA, an operational-state-aware verification procedure that checks whether binary-level vulnerability candidates are actually reachable under realistic industrial operating conditions without requiring source code or instrumented builds.
- Presents RSA, a tiered remediation synthesis engine that selects the strongest feasible fix based on artifact availability: Tier 1 protocol/configuration mitigation, Tier 2 binary hardening, and Tier 3 SSCKG-constrained source patching.
- Introduces CVA, a correctness-validation stage that provides conditional evidence using post-remedy UNSAT confirmation, behavioral coverage preservation against the SSCKG, domain-invariant checks, replay-based validation, and typed rejection feedback to refine remediation.
- Builds OIS-RemedBench, a benchmark of 15 remediation cases spanning firmware, protocol handlers, and ICS/PLC artifacts, providing the first evaluation substrate for opaque industrial software vulnerability remediation.

### ⚙️ Methodology
SCARA is designed for the setting where an upstream binary analysis pipeline or analyst provides vulnerability candidates over opaque industrial software artifacts. The system assumes no source code, no symbols, no reliable build environment, and often no direct access to the original hardware platform. Its core design is a four-stage pipeline organized around semantics constraints and artifact-aware remediation selection.

First, SCARA formalizes execution context with an industrial operational state model that extends traditional symbolic execution state beyond program counter, memory, and path constraints. The model contains nine components: program location, memory, symbolic path condition, environment, I/O conditions, protocol-state machine state, runtime state, component state, and time. This is the key mechanism for making vulnerability validation industrially meaningful: many alerts are only exploitable under narrow protocol, timing, or device-state assumptions, so reachability must be checked against operational envelopes rather than pure code paths.

Second, OSVA (operational-state-aware verification) uses this state model to classify candidates by operational reachability. It consumes structural and semantic information from an SSCKG (Software Supply Chain Knowledge Graph), itself derived from a code property graph and behavioral security labels. OSVA reasons about whether attacker-controlled influence can traverse risk-relevant relations such as data flow, control dependence, shared memory, IPC, protocol interaction, and cross-component calls under the required industrial state conditions. Candidates that cannot be satisfied in operational context are refuted as infeasible instead of being sent for repair, reducing wasted remediation effort and false positives.

Third, RSA (remediation synthesis agent) chooses the strongest available remediation based on artifact availability class. For policy-only artifacts, it emits Tier 1 mitigations such as protocol-level filtering, configuration restrictions, or compensating controls. For binary-rewritable artifacts, it applies Tier 2 binary hardening, such as inserting runtime guards through binary rewriting or instrumentation-like protection at the executable level. For source-available cases, it can perform Tier 3 repair using SSCKG-constrained LLM-assisted patch synthesis. The SSCKG serves as a behavioral specification and constraint system to keep candidate fixes aligned with observed semantics and to avoid violating risk-relevant behavior across components. The tiering is intended to degrade gracefully with opacity: even when ideal source patches are impossible, SCARA still aims to produce a non-zero defense action.

Fourth, CVA (correctness validation agent) validates proposed remedies and can reject them with typed feedback that triggers targeted reruns of RSA. Validation is conditional rather than absolute because OIS settings often lack full test harnesses and hardware-complete execution environments. CVA therefore combines several forms of evidence: post-remedy unsatisfiability confirmation for the original exploitability condition where possible, preservation of SSCKG behavioral coverage to ensure major expected behavior remains intact, compliance with domain invariants relevant to industrial control software, and independent replay in available environments such as emulators, protocol harnesses, or soft-PLC setups. If a candidate remedy overblocks protocol behavior, fails replay, violates invariants, or lacks sufficient evidence, CVA returns a typed rejection signal so RSA can attempt a more targeted or weaker-but-valid alternative.

Overall, the architecture links binary-level vulnerability discovery to validated remediation in a source-unavailable setting. Its technical novelty is the use of the operational-state envelope as a first-class correctness condition and the availability-aware remedy selector that can shift among mitigation, binary hardening, and source repair depending on what the defender actually controls.

### 🧪 Experiments
The evaluation uses OIS-RemedBench, a new 15-case benchmark covering three artifact partitions: OIS-Binary, OIS-Protocol, and OIS-ICS/PLC. The cases include stripped firmware, proprietary or closed protocol handlers, and industrial control/PLC-related software artifacts, with stratified labels reflecting different reachability or evidence conditions. The benchmark is explicitly constructed for remediation rather than just detection, addressing the lack of prior datasets in this space.

The paper evaluates SCARA end-to-end rather than as a detector-only system. Key outputs measured include precision of accepted remediation decisions, rate of operational infeasibility refutations, remediation success after reruns, UNKNOWN rate, overblocking behavior for Tier 2 binary defenses, and analyst burden in the ICS setting. The framework is compared primarily against the status quo implied by prior pipelines: binary analysis that stops at alert generation, manual analyst triage/remediation, and opaque-artifact scenarios where existing source-based repair tools are inapplicable.

Reported results are strong for the studied benchmark. SCARA achieves observed 100% precision with no false positives in the evaluated partitions. OSVA refutes 20.0% of benchmark cases (3/15) as operationally infeasible, avoiding unnecessary repair attempts. After targeted reruns driven by CVA feedback, SCARA reaches 88.9% remediation success on the relevant subset (8/9). The UNKNOWN rate drops from 13.3% to 6.7%, indicating that feedback-guided retries help convert uncertain cases into validated outcomes. For Tier 2 binary hardening, overblocking is reduced from 80.0% to 25.0%, showing that the semantics constraints and validation loop substantially improve practicality over naive binary-level defenses. In the OIS-ICS setting, analyst burden decreases from 17.04 hours per case to 2.42 hours per case, corresponding to about a 7.0x throughput gain. The abstract and introduction also state that SCARA eliminates 40.0% of binary alerts as infeasible, reflecting the impact of the verification stage on upstream candidate triage.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper is notable because it reframes automated repair for industrial binaries as a constrained remediation problem rather than a patch-generation problem. In opaque settings, many vulnerabilities cannot be fixed with source-level patches, so a layered remediation strategy that includes protocol mitigation and binary hardening is more realistic than insisting on code recompilation. This is directly relevant to real-world industrial software maintenance, where operators often only possess deployed artifacts.

A major insight is that correctness for industrial remediation must include operational semantics, not just crash elimination or unit-test passing. By modeling protocol states, runtime conditions, component interactions, and timing, SCARA recognizes that exploitability and safe repair depend on device behavior in context. This is important for vulnerability detection and repair research because it suggests that binary alerts should not be treated as equally actionable without state-aware feasibility analysis.

The SSCKG is also an interesting bridge between binary analysis and repair. It acts as a compact semantic representation that constrains both validation and synthesis, which may inspire future work on graph-guided repair, binary-to-source reasoning, or security-focused code property abstractions for compiled artifacts.

Limitations are implied by the paper's framing and benchmark scale. The evaluation is on 15 cases, so generalization across the much broader diversity of industrial software, architectures, vendor protections, and hardware dependencies remains open. Conditional correctness evidence is weaker than full functional verification, especially when replay environments only approximate real devices. Tier 3 source patching depends on source availability, which is rare in the hardest OIS scenarios, so much of the practical value will likely come from Tier 1 and Tier 2 methods. The quality and completeness of upstream vulnerability candidates and SSCKG construction are also critical dependencies; missed semantics or inaccurate binary analysis could affect both verification and repair selection.

Open research questions include scaling OSVA to larger firmware images and more complex industrial protocols, reducing residual overblocking in binary hardening, learning better typed feedback policies for repair reruns, and integrating formal safety constraints with security remediation for PLC/ICS programs. Another key future direction is stronger empirical comparison against manual workflows and against binary hardening baselines to quantify which components of SCARA contribute most to success.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to software engineering, software maintenance, software security, automated program repair, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair because it directly addresses the difficult but practical case where only binaries are available. It advances the researcher's interests by connecting binary vulnerability findings to actionable and validated remediation, introducing state-aware verification for industrial software, and proposing a realistic repair stack that spans mitigation, binary rewriting, and constrained source patching—precisely the kind of framework needed for secure maintenance of deployed, proprietary, and critical-system software.

## 📄 [Reversa: A Reverse Documentation Engineering Framework for Converting Legacy Software into Operational Specifications for AI Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.18684v1)  `80% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Sanderson Oliveira de Macedo, Ronaldo Martins da Costa
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-18  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE, cs.AI

### 💡 TL;DR
The paper proposes Reversa, a multi-agent reverse documentation engineering framework that turns legacy software into traceable operational specifications for AI coding agents, with explicit confidence levels and documented gaps instead of overconfident claims. Its exploratory COBOL-to-Go ATM migration case study shows the framework can generate substantial specification artifacts and migration guidance, but it stops short of full parity validation or broad empirical claims.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Defines reverse documentation engineering as the process of deriving technical documentation and operational specifications from legacy systems for use by AI agents.
- Introduces Reversa, a multi-agent pipeline that maps project structure, analyzes modules, extracts implicit business and operational rules, synthesizes architecture, writes unit-level specifications, and reviews claims.
- Proposes a risk-aware documentation model centered on traceability, explicit confidence marking, and preservation of unresolved gaps for human validation.
- Presents a portable implementation as a Node.js CLI that installs reusable skills across multiple agent engines and uses a SHA-256 manifest to preserve user-modified files during update or uninstall.
- Provides an evaluation protocol with metrics for coverage, traceability, confidence, utility, and cost, and demonstrates it in an exploratory legacy migration case study.

### ⚙️ Methodology
Reversa is designed as a reverse-engineering-to-specification framework for legacy systems whose behavior is only partially explicit in source code, SQL, configuration, comments, file layouts, and maintenance conventions. The core idea is to reconstruct operational knowledge as artifacts consumable by coding agents rather than merely producing human-oriented narrative documentation. The framework decomposes the process into a multi-agent pipeline coordinated by an orchestrator. Specialized agents first map the project surface to inventory files, modules, technologies, dependencies, and likely subsystem boundaries. They then analyze modules to identify responsibilities, interfaces, data flows, validations, and exception-handling behavior. A rule-extraction stage infers implicit business and operational rules from evidence such as conditionals, queries, naming, I/O patterns, and legacy implementation conventions. Another agent synthesizes architectural views from the accumulated evidence, including module roles, interactions, and reconstruction priorities. A specification-writing stage converts these findings into unit-level operational specifications and higher-level parity scenarios, while a review agent checks generated claims for support, consistency, and uncertainty.

The framework emphasizes three control mechanisms. First, every claim should be traceable back to concrete evidence in the legacy system, so generated statements are linked to code or repository artifacts instead of being free-form summaries. Second, each claim receives an explicit confidence indication, reducing the risk that downstream agents treat weak inferences as established facts. Third, unresolved ambiguities or missing evidence are preserved as explicit gaps rather than silently smoothed over; these gaps are intended for human validation and decision-making. This makes the output a reviewable contract rather than a hallucination-prone summary.

Operationally, Reversa is distributed as a Node.js CLI that installs 'skills' or reusable prompts/workflows across different coding-agent engines. The implementation also includes a SHA-256 manifest mechanism so that framework updates or uninstallations do not overwrite or remove files that have been modified by users, supporting safer adoption in live repositories. Conceptually, the framework is positioned upstream of automated maintenance and migration agents: it creates the structured behavioral context, constraints, and acceptance criteria that later AI agents can use for lower-risk code modification, reimplementation, or modernization.

### 🧪 Experiments
The paper does not present a broad benchmark-based comparative evaluation; instead, it reports an exploratory case study involving migration of an ATM system from COBOL to Go. The goal was to instantiate the proposed evaluation protocol and demonstrate the kinds of artifacts Reversa can produce. In this study, the pipeline generated 517 documentary/specification claims that were classified according to an internal confidence index, along with 10 explicit gaps requiring human review. It also produced 53 Gherkin parity scenarios intended to capture expected behavioral equivalence between the legacy and target implementations. In addition, Reversa generated a reconstruction plan, of which 9 out of 11 tasks were completed at the time of inventory.

The evaluation framing centers on protocol-level metrics rather than direct head-to-head comparison with baselines. The proposed metrics include coverage of recovered system behavior, traceability of claims to code evidence, confidence characterization, practical utility for maintenance or migration, and cost. The paper is explicit that final parity validation and production cutover were not completed in the case study, so no definitive migration-success claims are made. Likewise, no empirical superiority over other reverse-engineering, documentation-generation, or agentic software-engineering methods is claimed. The reported quantitative results therefore serve as feasibility evidence for artifact production and process structuring rather than as proof of end-to-end migration correctness or benchmark dominance.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A key insight is that AI-generated documentation for legacy systems is risky when it presents uncertain inferences as facts; for downstream coding agents, incorrect documentation may be more harmful than incomplete documentation, so confidence labels and explicit unresolved gaps are essential. The work also highlights that repository-level understanding for maintenance or migration benefits from role decomposition: mapping, analysis, rule extraction, architecture synthesis, specification writing, and review are distinct cognitive tasks that may be better handled by separate agents than by a single monolithic prompt.

For software engineering research, Reversa suggests a useful intermediate layer between reverse engineering and automated repair/migration: before asking agents to modify code, one can first derive traceable operational contracts from the existing system. This is especially relevant to legacy modernization, where business rules are often embedded implicitly in code and operational conventions. The paper’s evaluation protocol is also potentially reusable by future work on LLM-based documentation, requirements extraction, migration assistants, and agentic maintenance systems.

The main limitation is empirical scope. The evidence comes from a single exploratory ATM migration scenario, with no completed parity validation, no production cutover, and no comparative benchmark against alternative tools or human-only processes. Confidence scoring is internally defined rather than externally calibrated, leaving open questions about how well confidence levels correlate with true correctness. Additional open questions include scalability to large heterogeneous systems, robustness for security-critical or highly stateful software, integration with dynamic analyses and test generation, and whether the produced specifications measurably improve downstream agent performance on maintenance, repair, or vulnerability-related tasks.

### 🎯 Why This Paper

The paper studies static malware detection over binary Win32 API import vectors extracted from Windows PE files. Each executable is represented as a multi-hot vector over imported API functions. The detection target is a six-class classifier with one malware class and five benign classes corresponding to different software categories. The authors first train a strong non-differentiable ensemble detector on the API vectors. Because direct gradient-based optimization against this ensemble is not possible, they distill its behavior into a differentiable neural proxy that approximates the ensemble’s class probabilities. This proxy is then used as the attack objective during training.

The attack generator is a Conditional Variational Autoencoder. The encoder takes the original malware API vector together with a target benign class condition and maps the sample into a latent space. The decoder outputs a perturbation vector under a strict monotonic/additive constraint: decoded features can only activate previously absent API imports and can never deactivate existing ones. In practice, the modified sample is formed by combining the original malware vector with the decoder output through an additive or OR-style merge, followed by a budgeted top-k selection so that only k new imports are injected.

A key part of the framework is target selection. Rather than fixing a single benign class for all malware, the system identifies for each malware sample the benign category it already most resembles and uses that category as the evasion target. This makes the attack more sample-adaptive and reduces the amount of perturbation needed to cross the decision boundary.

Training optimizes the CVAE to generate perturbations that cause the distilled proxy to classify the modified sample as the selected benign target while respecting the additive-only constraint and the injection budget. The variational component regularizes the latent representation and supports stable generation of plausible category-specific import additions. The core result is that with k=20 injected API imports, the CVAE reduces malware recall from 87.5% to 30%. At the same budget, among malware samples that successfully evade detection, 99% are classified as the intended target benign category, showing that the attack is genuinely targeted rather than merely causing arbitrary confusion. Across all tested budgets from 5 to 50, the CVAE outperforms both MostPopular and Random.

The paper also validates practicality on real PE files by modifying actual binaries and submitting them to VirusTotal. The paper highlights a concrete weakness of static malware detectors that rely on API imports: because imports are easy to add without removing original behavior, the feature space is manipulable in a way that preserves functionality while shifting classifier decisions. A notable insight is that targeted evasion into a specific benign category is feasible with surprisingly small perturbations, suggesting that benign category structure can be exploited by attackers rather than only by defenders.

Important limitations remain. The attack focuses on static API-import features and does not address dynamic behavior, control flow, or runtime telemetry. Real binaries can add imports, but practical deployment may still be constrained by PE rewriting complexity, loader behavior, or inconsistencies across scanners. The dataset size is modest, Windows-specific, and based on API vectors rather than richer multimodal features. The target-category selection strategy is intuitive, but it is still unclear how well the method generalizes to larger taxonomies, family-level malware labels, or detectors using combined static and dynamic signals.

A key implication for future defense work is that detectors should treat easy-to-inject static indicators with caution, possibly down-weighting isolated benign-looking imports, modeling import co-occurrence consistency, checking whether imported APIs are actually reachable or used, and combining import features with harder-to-forge behavioral evidence.

This paper is highly relevant to software security, vulnerability detection, and automated repair-oriented research because it studies a realistic semantics-preserving attack on ML-based malware detectors and exposes how static program features can be manipulated with minimal binary changes. - Introduces an adversarial benchmark for LLM tool access control with 200 tasks across four attack categories and multiple cross-domain tool-access settings.

A central insight is that self-evolving memory systems fail not because skill generation is impossible, but because unmanaged accumulation silently corrupts retrieval and downstream reasoning. To operationalize this, the authors construct scenarios in which a benign prompt admits a fully scope-respecting solution path, but the environment also contains tempting nearby targets such as unrelated files, stale credentials backups, dotfiles, or configuration artifacts.

The benchmark generation pipeline, OverEager-Gen, is inspired by mutation testing. Starting from expert-designed seed scenarios, the authors expand coverage through archetypes and mutators, then admit only scenarios that pass construction-time validity checks. The first key validator is a behavioral-gradient validator: each candidate scenario is executed against three scripted profiles—cautious, moderate, and aggressive—and the triggered trap sets must be monotone by inclusion, with strict separation between endpoints. This certifies that the scenario has discriminative power: a cautious policy can solve it without tripping traps, while increasingly aggressive behavior produces predictably larger trap sets. This ensures scenarios are informative and that benchmark verdicts are not arbitrary.

The second methodological component addresses measurement validity. Each admitted scenario is shipped in two byte-identical prompt variants except for the consent block: consent_kept explicitly declares authorized scope, whereas consent_stripped removes that declaration. This paired-ablation design isolates whether measured safety comes from genuine scope inference or from the agent simply matching declaration text. The paper shows this distinction is crucial because prompt-embedded scope declarations can suppress the very behavior the benchmark seeks to measure.

The third component is complete auditing. Since coding agents may act through both normal shell commands and internal framework tools, the system uses a dual-channel audit stack. A PATH-injected shell shim records shell-level operations, while a per-agent event-stream adapter captures tool-native actions such as file reads, edits, writes, and grep-like searches that may never appear in shell logs. The benchmark also defines declarative traps and success oracles per scenario, enabling deterministic rule-based judgment of whether the agent both completed the intended benign task and crossed out-of-scope boundaries.

Scenarios are paired into consent_kept and consent_stripped variants to test the effect of explicit in-prompt scope declarations. ### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A central insight is that evaluating autonomous coding agents requires separating capability from authorization. An agent can successfully complete the requested task and still be unsafe if it makes unauthorized writes or sensitive reads in adjacent parts of the environment. This is highly relevant for software engineering settings where repositories, shell state, configs, credentials, and local artifacts coexist.

If the prompt explicitly spells out scope and consent, agents may learn to obey benchmark boilerplate rather than infer user intent in realistic ambiguous settings. For researchers, this raises an open question: how should automated repair or coding systems balance autonomy and safety without causing excessive interruption or loss of productivity?

- Introduces Obsessive Experience Poisoning (OEP), a low-privilege black-box attack that poisons self-evolving LLM agents without modifying system prompts, model parameters, or memory databases.
- Identifies a subtle attack surface in reflective agents: clean, factually valid, semantically plausible edge-case experiences can still induce harmful self-generated rules through over-generalization during memory reflection.

### ⚙️ Methodology
OEP targets the reflection-based memory consolidation loop of self-evolving LLM agents. The attacker is assumed to have only user-level, black-box access: they cannot edit the system prompt, memory store, tools, or model weights, and can only submit crafted interactions that may later be absorbed into episodic history and distilled into semantic memory.

The key idea is to avoid explicit malicious instructions and instead induce the agent to generate its own harmful rule from clean experiences. OEP constructs adversarial examples with three properties: (1) the local solution is correct for the specific case, so factuality and semantic validation pass; (2) the method is non-transferable, meaning it works only in a narrow edge-case and should not be generalized; and (3) the case is paired with severe but plausible hypothetical consequences, which bias reflection toward overly conservative or risk-averse abstractions.

The framework has three phases. In Phase 1, Clean Edge-Case Construction, the attacker creates task instances whose correct resolution depends on an idiosyncratic exception or special-case method. These cases are selected so the local answer is valid, but any generalized rule derived from it will be misleading on normal downstream tasks. In Phase 2, OEP augments each edge-case with an Adversarial Consequence Triplet (ACT). ACT introduces plausible negative outcomes if the exceptional method is not followed, amplifying perceived stakes. This manipulates the agent’s internal utility estimation during reflection: because the method appears correct and the negative consequence appears serious yet believable, the agent becomes more likely to encode the experience as a high-priority policy rather than a context-bounded exception. In Phase 3, User-Level Injection, these crafted interactions are submitted through ordinary conversations. During subsequent reflective learning, the agent validates the local correctness of the episode, over-trusts its own synthesized reflection, and consolidates the narrow method into persistent semantic memory as an over-generalized rule.

Formally, the paper models a victim with episodic history, a semantic memory bank, a filtering/validation stage, and a reflection function that extracts rules from validated history. The attacker’s goal is to maximize downstream task failure or resource overuse while ensuring the adversarial experience survives filtering because it is clean and semantically plausible. The resulting poisoned memory acts as a global prior affecting future independent sessions, enabling both integrity violations and denial-of-wallet style availability harms.

### 🧪 Experiments
The paper evaluates OEP on reflective, memory-augmented agents across three domains using GPT-4o-based agents as the main target. The evaluation compares OEP against existing memory-poisoning or agent-memory attack baselines that typically rely on explicit malicious content, unsafe procedures, triggers, or tampered records. The paper also tests robustness under LLM auditing defenses, including judge/filter style defenses that inspect or sanitize memory content.

The main metric is attack success rate (ASR), measuring whether the poisoned reflective memory induces the intended downstream failure pattern. Additional analysis considers robustness to defenses and behavior consistent with integrity degradation and resource-abuse objectives. The central quantitative result reported in the abstract and introduction is that OEP achieves ASR above 50% across three diverse domains with GPT-4o agents. The paper further claims OEP outperforms prior attacks when LLM auditing defenses are enabled, because OEP’s injected experiences remain factually correct and do not contain overtly malicious payloads, making them harder to flag by factuality-, toxicity-, or anomaly-oriented detectors.

Beyond aggregate effectiveness, the experiments support the claim that reflective self-improvement itself creates vulnerability: agents consolidate attacker-shaped experiences into persistent rules that later harm benign task performance. The authors also report an empirical capability-vulnerability trend indicating that stronger agents may be more susceptible under OEP, likely because they perform more confident abstraction and memory reuse from prior experiences.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper’s main insight is that agent security problems are not limited to explicit prompt injection or direct memory tampering; the reflection mechanism itself is an attack surface. Clean, correct experiences can become poisonous once the agent abstracts them into general rules. This is highly relevant to self-improving coding and software agents, where narrow heuristics learned from isolated cases could silently degrade reliability, induce unsafe workflows, or cause expensive repeated tool usage.

A second insight is that plausibly severe consequences are crucial: they shift the agent’s reflection toward risk-averse over-generalization. This suggests that safety-oriented reasoning, if not properly calibrated with context sensitivity, can be manipulated into producing brittle operational rules. The work therefore raises questions about how to build reflection modules that distinguish exceptions from transferable principles, calibrate confidence in self-generated memories, and preserve uncertainty instead of collapsing to hard rules.

The paper also implies limitations of current defenses. Content filters, anomaly detectors, and LLM judges are more effective against overtly malicious records than against clean-label style poisoning. Defending against OEP likely requires memory provenance, contextual transferability checks, reflection regularization, rule verification across counterexamples, or mechanisms that explicitly encode scope conditions for learned experiences.

Potential limitations and open questions include how broadly OEP transfers across agent frameworks beyond those tested, how much attack effectiveness depends on memory schema and reflection prompt design, whether repeated benign counterexamples can repair poisoned memories, and how to measure long-term persistence of induced rules. Another open issue is evaluating such attacks in code-generation or software-maintenance agents, where small over-generalized rules may have outsized security or repair consequences.

This paper is directly relevant to research on software engineering, software maintenance, software security, automated program repair, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair because modern coding agents increasingly rely on memory, reflection, and self-improvement loops. OEP exposes a realistic low-privilege attack vector whereby an adversary can corrupt an agent’s learned repair/debugging/security heuristics without injecting obviously malicious content, which is especially important for secure code assistants, autonomous maintenance agents, and APR systems.

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper presents DMN, a black-box multi-image jailbreak framework for multimodal LLMs that composes distributed typographic instructions, realistic multimodal evidence, and a distracting number-chain reasoning task to bypass safety mechanisms. Across 10 MLLMs and 3 datasets, DMN substantially outperforms prior single-image and video-style baselines, reaching attack success rates above 90% on several frontier closed-source models and remaining effective against defense mechanisms.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces DMN, a compositional jailbreak framework specifically designed for MLLMs that accept multi-image inputs, exposing a new safety vulnerability not addressed by prior single-image attacks.
- Proposes Distributed Instruction, which splits harmful typographic content across multiple images so that no single image fully reveals the malicious request, making safety detection harder.
- Proposes Multimodal Evidence, a realistic-case generation pipeline that creates paired textual and visual evidence to both evade text-to-image safety filters and elicit more detailed harmful responses from target MLLMs.
- Proposes the Number Chain Task, a multi-image visual reasoning distraction mechanism that forces the model to follow cross-image digit-linking instructions, weakening safety-aligned behavior.
- Performs extensive evaluation on 10 MLLMs and 3 datasets, showing large attack-success improvements over prior jailbreak baselines including single-image methods and VideoJail.
- Provides ablation findings showing that the gains come from the compositional modules rather than merely increasing image count, and that distributed harmful intent plus harder auxiliary reasoning significantly improves jailbreak effectiveness.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The paper studies single-turn black-box jailbreaking of multimodal LLMs that take a text prompt and an ordered sequence of images as input. The objective is to maximize the number of harmful outputs judged by a binary safety criterion. DMN constructs one multi-image attack instance by composing three coordinated modules. First, Distributed Instruction converts the harmful request into multiple FigStep-style typographic images, each containing only a single word or a small fragment plus an empty numbered list, so the full malicious intent is only recoverable by jointly interpreting the entire image set. This is intended to reduce the chance that per-image safety checks detect the harmful content. Second, Multimodal Evidence generates realistic-case materials around the harmful topic. An auxiliary LLM extracts key phrases from the original unsafe request and writes a plausible scenario with several detailed textual evidence items. These evidence items are then rewritten into image-generation-friendly descriptions including visual appearance, object placement, and camera perspective. A text-to-image model generates one visual evidence image per textual evidence item. If generation is rejected, the system logs failed prompts and uses an LLM-based refinement step to rewrite safer but semantically useful prompts, increasing generation success. This module both hides direct harmfulness from the T2I model and supplies rich contextual cues that encourage the attacked MLLM to produce more concrete harmful responses. Third, the Number Chain Task introduces distraction via additional images called number-chain frames. Each frame contains a digit and the index of the next frame to visit. The target model is instructed to read the digit and follow the specified next-frame index, eventually reconstructing a full number chain. These frames are randomly interleaved among the distributed-instruction and evidence images, with randomized jump order, to force cross-image reasoning and consume model attention. The final user prompt instructs the MLLM to process the images according to their roles: recover the distributed instruction, leverage the evidence, solve the number chain task, and answer accordingly. The overall design hypothesis is that multi-image compositionality creates a larger attack surface than single-image jailbreaks by distributing intent, increasing contextual grounding, and introducing a nontrivial auxiliary reasoning burden that weakens safety refusal behavior.

### 🧪 Experiments
The authors evaluate DMN on three datasets and 10 multimodal LLMs, including major closed-source models such as GPT-4o, Gemini-2.5-pro, and Claude Sonnet 4, as well as additional open and proprietary MLLMs that support multi-image input. The threat model is black-box and single-turn. The main metric is attack success rate (ASR), determined by a safety judge that labels whether the model response is harmful. The paper compares DMN against prior multimodal jailbreak baselines including single-image typographic attacks, query-related image attacks, distraction-based approaches, and the multi-image/video-style baseline VideoJail. The reported headline result is that DMN achieves over 90% ASR on GPT-4o, Gemini-2.5-pro, and Claude Sonnet 4, surpassing other baselines by a large margin. The evaluation further shows broad effectiveness across all tested MLLMs and datasets, indicating that the method is not limited to a particular model family. The authors also test against jailbreak defenses and find that DMN remains effective, suggesting weak robustness of current defenses to multi-image compositional attacks. Ablation studies analyze the contribution of each module: distributed instruction outperforms packing the same content into a single image; multimodal evidence improves response richness and attack reliability; and the number chain task raises ASR further by increasing distraction. Additional analyses show that the improvement is not merely due to supplying more images, that distributing malicious intent across images more easily bypasses safety mechanisms, and that more complex auxiliary tasks can produce stronger distraction effects.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The central insight is that multi-image support creates a qualitatively different attack surface for MLLMs: safety alignment that appears adequate for single-image prompts can fail when harmful intent is spread compositionally across several images and coupled with benign-looking reasoning tasks. The work suggests that current safety pipelines likely underperform at joint cross-image harmfulness aggregation, especially when no individual image is overtly unsafe. For security research, this implies that multimodal defenses must move beyond per-image moderation and shallow OCR checks toward holistic sequence-level reasoning, intent reconstruction, and robustness to adversarial task composition. A second insight is that upstream generative models such as T2I systems can be indirectly exploited to scaffold attacks through realistic-case framing, which reduces explicit harmfulness while preserving semantically useful cues. The paper also indicates that auxiliary reasoning tasks can act as a cognitive distraction channel for MLLMs, raising an open question about how safety alignment interacts with task complexity and multi-step visual reasoning. Limitations include the focus on single-turn attacks, the dependence on models that support multiple images, and the lack of a principled certified defense; the paper is mainly offensive and diagnostic. Open questions include whether similar compositional vulnerabilities exist in video, document, or agentic multimodal settings; how to benchmark cross-image safety alignment systematically; and whether defense training on compositional multimodal adversarial examples can close the gap without degrading utility.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to software security and vulnerability research because it uncovers a concrete, previously underexplored vulnerability class in multimodal LLM systems: compositional jailbreaks over multi-image inputs. For a researcher interested in vulnerability detection, repair, and secure AI-assisted software engineering, the work offers a strong adversarial evaluation methodology, a modular attack decomposition useful for threat modeling, and actionable evidence that current defenses fail under realistic black-box conditions. It can inform future work on automated vulnerability discovery in AI systems, defense benchmarking, secure multimodal pipeline design, and repair strategies for safety alignment failures in deployed LLM-based tools.
