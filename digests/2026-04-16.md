## 📄 [RealVuln: Benchmarking Rule-Based, General-Purpose LLM, and Security-Specialized Scanners on Real-World Code](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13764v1)  `100% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** John Pellew, Faizan Raza
📅 **Published:** 2026-04-15  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR

### 💡 TL;DR
RealVuln is an open, hand-labeled benchmark for evaluating security scanners on 26 real intentionally vulnerable Python applications, comparing rule-based SAST, general-purpose LLMs, and security-specialized tools under recall-heavy metrics. Across 15 scanners, a stable three-tier pattern appears: security-specialized scanners perform best overall, general-purpose LLMs clearly beat traditional rule-based tools, and rule-based SAST lags substantially on this benchmark.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces RealVuln, the first fully open-source benchmark comparing rule-based SAST, general-purpose LLM scanners, and security-specialized scanners on identical real-world Python code with public ground truth, raw outputs, and scoring scripts.
- Builds a manually curated dataset of 26 intentionally vulnerable multi-file Python repositories with 796 hand-labeled entries, including 676 true vulnerabilities and 120 deliberately selected false-positive traps.
- Defines an evaluation methodology that emphasizes recall using F3 as the primary ranking metric while also reporting F1 and F2, making tradeoffs between missed vulnerabilities and false alarms explicit.
- Empirically shows a robust three-tier ranking across metrics: security-specialized scanners lead, general-purpose LLMs are second, and rule-based SAST tools trail by a wide margin.
- Releases RealVuln as a living, versioned benchmark with a public dashboard and roadmap for future expansion to more target types and languages.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The paper designs RealVuln v1.0 as an evaluation framework focused on Type 1 targets: intentionally vulnerable applications used for education or CTF practice. The dataset consists of 26 open-source Python repositories selected to be realistic multi-file applications rather than toy snippets, covering common web application stacks and vulnerability patterns. The authors manually annotate repository code to produce line-auditable ground truth, labeling both actual vulnerabilities and non-vulnerable code regions that are likely to trigger scanner noise. This yields 796 labeled entries: 676 vulnerabilities and 120 false-positive traps.

The benchmark compares three scanner families on exactly the same code and labels: (1) rule-based SAST tools that rely on pattern matching and static rules, (2) general-purpose LLM configurations that are not security-specific but are prompted to perform vulnerability detection, and (3) security-specialized systems built specifically for finding vulnerabilities. In total, 15 scanners are evaluated: 3 rule-based SAST tools, 10 general-purpose LLM scanner configurations spanning 7 model families, and 2 security-specialized scanners.

To score outputs, scanner findings are matched against the hand-labeled ground truth. The benchmark explicitly accounts for both detection and false positives, rather than reporting only raw vulnerability hits. The primary metric is F3, where beta=3 weights recall nine times more heavily than precision. The rationale is that in security, missing a real vulnerability is often more costly than raising an extra alert. The authors still report F1 and F2 to show how rankings change as precision is weighted more strongly. This metric design is central to the paper: it treats scanner evaluation as a security-risk tradeoff problem rather than a generic balanced classification task.

The framework is implemented as a reproducible open-source harness, with public benchmark repositories, labels, scanner outputs, and scoring logic, plus a live dashboard for interactive exploration. The benchmark is intended to be versioned and community-extendable rather than a one-off evaluation.

### 🧪 Experiments
The evaluation uses 26 intentionally vulnerable Python repositories drawn from educational and CTF-style applications such as PyGoat, DVPWA, and VAmPI. The benchmark contains 796 hand-labeled entries total, including 676 true vulnerabilities and 120 false-positive traps. Fifteen scanners are compared: 3 rule-based SAST tools, 10 general-purpose LLM configurations across 7 model families, and 2 security-specialized scanners.

The main ranking metric is F3, with F1 and F2 also reported. Under F3, the top-performing scanner is the security-specialized Kolega.Dev with 73.0, followed by the best general-purpose LLM, Claude Sonnet 4.6, at 51.7. The best rule-based SAST tool, Semgrep, scores 17.7, meaning the top general-purpose LLM is nearly 3x stronger than the best traditional rule-based baseline on this recall-heavy measure. Under F1, where precision and recall are weighted equally, Claude Sonnet 4.6 leads with 60.9 while Kolega.Dev scores 52.4. Although exact within-tier ordering changes with beta, the same broad hierarchy holds across F1, F2, and F3: security-specialized scanners remain strongest overall, general-purpose LLMs substantially outperform rule-based SAST, and rule-based tools form a distinctly lower tier.

A key experimental claim is that this three-tier separation is consistent across all reported metric weightings, suggesting the result is not an artifact of a single scoring choice. The paper emphasizes full reproducibility by releasing code, labels, raw scanner outputs, scoring scripts, and an interactive dashboard.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The most important takeaway is that benchmark choice and metric choice materially affect conclusions about scanner quality. On this realistic Python benchmark, old-style rule-based SAST appears markedly weaker than both LLM-based and security-specialized approaches, especially when recall is prioritized. However, the winner can change depending on whether one values balanced F1 or recall-heavy F3, highlighting that scanner selection should be tied to deployment goals such as developer usability versus high-assurance security review.

The paper also argues that open, manually verified benchmarks are necessary because prior vulnerability datasets often suffer from severe label noise, which can distort model and tool comparisons. RealVuln trades scale for trustworthiness, making it especially useful for rigorous benchmarking and error analysis.

Important limitations remain. Version 1.0 only covers Python and only intentionally vulnerable applications, so results may not generalize to production repositories, CVE-fix datasets, memory-unsafe languages, supply-chain issues, or infrastructure/configuration flaws. The dataset is focused on OWASP-style application vulnerabilities, not the broader secure software landscape. There is also a conflict-of-interest concern because one of the top-performing scanners is developed by the authors; the paper addresses this through artifact release and reproducibility, but independent replication is still important.

For future research, the benchmark opens several directions: studying why general-purpose LLMs outperform rules on real code, analyzing failure modes by CWE and repository structure, integrating alert triage after detection, extending to multi-language and CVE-based settings, and using the benchmark as an evaluation target for vulnerability repair or agentic security workflows.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to software security, vulnerability detection, and automated secure maintenance because it provides a realistic, open benchmark for comparing modern detection approaches on real code rather than synthetic snippets. For a researcher working on vulnerability detection, repair, or security-oriented program analysis, RealVuln offers reusable ground truth, reproducible evaluation infrastructure, and strong empirical evidence about where rule-based tools, general LLMs, and security-specialized systems currently succeed or fail.

## 📄 [Towards Personalizing Secure Programming Education with LLM-Injected Vulnerabilities](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13955v1)  `90% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Matthew Frazier, Kostadin Damevski
📅 **Published:** 2026-04-15  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR, cs.CY, cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
The paper presents InjectEd, an agentic LLM framework that injects CWE-specific vulnerabilities into students’ own code to create personalized secure programming examples. A deployment in two undergraduate courses found that students perceived these personalized examples as more relevant and engaging than generic textbook materials, but quantitative survey differences were mostly not statistically significant.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces a novel method for embedding specific CWE-aligned vulnerabilities into student-authored code using LLMs to create personalized security learning materials.
- Designs InjectEd, a modular agentic pipeline with Injector, Evaluator, Ranker, and Learning Outcome Generator agents, plus validation and scoring tools for scalable instructional artifact generation.
- Deploys the approach in two undergraduate CS courses and compares student perceptions of personalized vulnerability examples against widely used generic instructional materials.
- Provides evidence that personalization improves perceived relevance, clarity, and engagement qualitatively, while also identifying the need for stronger empirical validation through refined studies.

### ⚙️ Methodology
InjectEd is an agentic AI system built around four sequential agents. The Injector Agent takes student assignment code, course description, assignment specification, and a CWE list as input and produces candidate vulnerable variants of the student’s code. It uses AST-based analysis to identify plausible injection points such as unchecked input handling or unsafe calls, then prompts GPT-4o-mini to introduce vulnerabilities corresponding to selected CWEs. To reduce hallucinations and preserve instructional authenticity, the generated code is passed through syntax validation and a lightweight behavioral regression check so that the vulnerable version remains syntactically valid and preserves core functionality apart from the injected flaw. Outputs are stored as structured JSON, including CWE metadata, modified code, and HTML-rendered instructional content.

The Evaluator Agent judges each injected variant on pedagogical quality rather than just technical correctness. It compares original and modified code using Ratcliff/Obershelp similarity for syntactic change and a Gensim-based semantic drift score for meaning change. These signals are included in the prompt so the LLM can score dimensions such as course relevance, naturalness, appropriateness to student skill, cognitive load, and pedagogical usefulness. The Ranker Agent then selects the best candidate based on four stated dimensions: relevance, appropriateness, naturalness, and pedagogical value. Finally, the Learning Outcome Generator Agent creates formative prompts or assessment questions tailored to the injected vulnerability and context.

The system is orchestrated with CrewAI, uses Langfuse for prompt management and observability, persists outputs through strict Pydantic schemas, and is designed for traceability and future classroom scaling. Although the full architecture includes all four agents, the reported classroom deployment mainly evaluates the Injector Agent’s feasibility and educational impact.

### 🧪 Experiments
The system was deployed in two upper-level undergraduate computer science courses with a total of 71 students. Students were shown code samples containing LLM-injected vulnerabilities derived from their own assignment code and completed a post-project survey. The comparison baseline consisted of a commonly used set of generic, textbook-style security instructional materials rather than personalized examples.

The study focused on students’ perceptions of relevance, clarity, engagement, and understanding of CWEs, rather than benchmark-style software engineering metrics such as repair accuracy or detection F1. Qualitative responses indicated that students found examples injected into their own code more relevant, clearer, and more engaging than generic examples. However, the quantitative analysis found only limited statistically significant differences between the personalized and baseline conditions. The paper frames this as encouraging but inconclusive evidence: personalization appears promising from a learner-experience perspective, but stronger empirical support requires improved study design, refinement of the injection process, and further deployments.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A central insight is that LLM-based vulnerability injection can make secure programming instruction more personally meaningful by embedding weaknesses directly into a student’s own implementation context. This aligns well with constructivist learning theory and suggests a promising direction for security education tools that go beyond static examples or generic labs.

For software security and vulnerability research, the paper highlights a novel educational use case for LLM-based vulnerability injection: not dataset augmentation or tool benchmarking, but pedagogically grounded generation of realistic flaws. The validation steps to preserve syntax and local semantics are especially relevant for researchers interested in trustworthy automated code transformation.

The main limitation is the weak quantitative evidence. The deployment shows positive learner perceptions but does not yet establish a robust causal impact on security understanding or skill acquisition. Another limitation is that the study primarily exercised the Injector Agent, leaving the full educational value of the Evaluator, Ranker, and Learning Outcome Generator untested in practice. Open questions include how to measure actual learning gains, how reliably LLMs can inject different CWE classes across languages and assignment types, whether injected vulnerabilities are realistic enough for transfer to real-world secure coding, and how to prevent overfitting examples to student code in ways that reduce generalization. The work implies a broader research agenda around personalized vulnerability generation, educational APR-style feedback, and secure coding tutors powered by agentic workflows.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is relevant because it sits at the intersection of software security, vulnerability injection, code transformation, and LLM-based automation. For a researcher in software maintenance, automated program repair, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair, it offers a complementary perspective: using LLMs not to fix vulnerabilities, but to systematically introduce realistic CWE instances into existing code while preserving behavior, which is directly useful for building training datasets, educational benchmarks, and human-in-the-loop repair workflows. Its agentic design, validation mechanisms, and focus on CWE-grounded edits can inform future work on secure code editing, vulnerability seeding for evaluation, repair recommendation systems, and personalized developer education.

## 📄 [V2E: Validating Smart Contract Vulnerabilities through Profit-driven Exploit Generation and Execution](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13611v1)  `90% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Jingwen Zhang, Yuhong Nan, Kaiwen Ning, Mingxi Ye, Wei Li, Yuming Xiao, Yuming Feng, Weizhe Zhang, Zibin Zheng
📅 **Published:** 2026-04-15  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper proposes V2E, a framework that validates whether smart-contract vulnerability reports are truly exploitable by automatically generating, executing, and iteratively refining proof-of-concept exploits, then judging exploitability based on both triggerability and attacker profit. On 264 manually labeled contracts, V2E substantially improves over an LLM baseline, identifying 102/124 exploitable vulnerabilities with 91.9% precision and 82.3% recall while filtering 71/140 false alarms.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces vulnerability validation for smart contracts as a distinct task from detection, defining exploitability in terms of both triggerability and profitability rather than pattern matching alone.
- Presents V2E, an automated framework that combines static vulnerability-path analysis, LLM-assisted targeted PoC generation, execution-based validation, and iterative PoC refinement.
- Designs a PoC effectiveness validation mechanism that determines whether a generated exploit actually triggers the reported vulnerability and causes financial gain or loss.
- Implements feedback-driven PoC refinement using execution results, bytecode semantics, and primitive exploit operations to improve exploit generation success.
- Demonstrates that V2E can reduce false positives from existing detectors, cutting false-positive rates for Slither, Mythril, and Confuzzius by 76.9%, 56.9%, and 65%, respectively.

### ⚙️ Methodology
V2E takes as input a vulnerability report, the contract source code, and the vulnerable function under test, and validates exploitability through a generate-validate-refine loop. First, it performs fine-grained static analysis to identify vulnerability entry points such as externally callable functions and to construct potential vulnerability paths from public interfaces to the reported vulnerable location. This path information, together with vulnerability-specific characteristics and deployment requirements, is used to synthesize targeted proof-of-concept exploits rather than generic test cases. Generated PoCs are stored in a PoC corpus. Second, V2E validates each PoC by executing it in an instrumented environment and analyzing two properties: triggerability, meaning whether the execution actually reaches and activates the vulnerable behavior, and profitability, meaning whether the exploit leads to meaningful financial impact or attacker gain. A vulnerability is considered truly exploitable only if both conditions hold. Third, if validation fails, V2E refines the PoC iteratively. When a PoC cannot execute correctly, V2E uses execution feedback and bytecode semantics to repair or adapt it. When a PoC executes but does not trigger the bug or does not yield profit, V2E applies primitive exploit operations and other guided modifications to adjust call sequences, state preparation, and parameters. The updated PoC is returned to the corpus for subsequent rounds. Overall, the framework integrates static reasoning for exploit path discovery, LLM-style PoC synthesis, dynamic execution monitoring, and closed-loop refinement to validate reported smart-contract vulnerabilities in a profit-aware way.

### 🧪 Experiments
The evaluation uses 264 manually labeled vulnerable smart contracts from SmartBugs: 64 manually constructed contracts and 200 real-world on-chain contracts. Each reported vulnerability was manually annotated for exploitability based on whether it could be triggered and whether it could generate profit. The main comparison is against an LLM-based baseline for PoC generation/validation. Key metrics include precision, recall, number of exploitable vulnerabilities confirmed, and number of false alarms eliminated. V2E correctly identified 102 of 124 exploitable vulnerabilities, achieving 91.9% precision and 82.3% recall. It also eliminated 71 of 140 non-exploitable reports, corresponding to 50.7% false-alarm filtering. In a downstream evaluation, V2E was used to validate outputs from state-of-the-art detectors and significantly reduced their false positives: 76.9% for Slither, 56.9% for Mythril, and 65% for Confuzzius. The paper positions these gains as evidence that exploit-generation-based validation can substantially improve the practical usability of vulnerability detectors.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A key insight is that many smart-contract vulnerability reports are false alarms not because the code pattern is absent, but because the bug is not reachable in a realistic execution context or cannot produce attacker profit. The paper also shows that exploitability is highly context-sensitive, including dependence on compiler version and contract state, so validation must reason about actual execution rather than static patterns alone. For software security research, this suggests a promising shift from pure detection toward exploitability-aware triage, similar to work in vulnerability confirmation and patch prioritization in conventional software. Likely limitations include dependence on the quality of the initial vulnerability report, the completeness of path analysis, and the ability of generated PoCs to cover complex multi-transaction or cross-contract scenarios. Profitability as the main damage criterion is practical for blockchain settings, but it may underapproximate non-monetary harms such as denial of service, governance manipulation, or latent integrity violations. Open questions include generalization to more vulnerability classes, scaling to richer DeFi ecosystems with multiple interacting protocols, and integrating exploit validation more tightly with automated repair or remediation workflows.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to research in software security, vulnerability detection, and automated repair because it addresses a core pain point of current analyzers: high false-positive rates and lack of exploitability confirmation. Its generate-execute-refine validation loop is especially useful for researchers working on vulnerability triage, smart-contract analysis, and APR-style systems, since exploit-confirmed reports can guide repair prioritization, improve benchmark quality, and serve as a stronger oracle for both detection and repair evaluation.

## 📄 [Learned or Memorized ? Quantifying Memorization Advantage in Code LLMs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13997v1)  `80% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Djiré Albérick Euraste, Kaboré Abdoul Kader, Jordan Samhi, Earl T. Barr, Jacques Klein, Tegawendé F. Bissyandé
📅 **Published:** 2026-04-15  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper adapts perturbation-sensitivity analysis to code LLMs and uses it to estimate a model’s memorization advantage across software engineering benchmarks. Across 8 open-source code LLMs and 19 benchmarks, the authors find that memorization risk is highly task- and model-dependent, and that commonly suspected datasets like CVEFixes and Defects4J actually show low memorization advantage.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces an empirical framework for quantifying memorization advantage in code LLMs using prompt/input perturbation sensitivity rather than requiring access to training data provenance or model internals.
- Evaluates 8 open-source code LLMs on 19 benchmarks spanning code generation, code understanding/summarization, vulnerability detection, test generation, and program repair.
- Defines and analyzes memorization advantage from two angles: benchmark memorization advantage (whether a benchmark appears unusually sensitive for a model) and model memorization advantage (whether a model behaves unusually sensitively on a benchmark).
- Shows large cross-model differences in sensitivity despite similar model scales, indicating that architecture and training choices materially affect memorization-vs-generalization behavior.
- Provides evidence against strong leakage concerns for two important SE/security datasets: CVEFixes shows very low sensitivity across models, and Defects4J is less sensitive than other APR benchmarks.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The paper builds on the perturbation sensitivity hypothesis: if a model has memorized a training example, small perturbations to the input should cause a sharper performance drop than if the model has learned generalizable patterns. The authors operationalize this idea for code tasks by defining memorization advantage as the absolute difference between model performance/probability on an original input x and a perturbed but semantically similar input x'.

The study applies perturbation-based evaluation to code LLMs without needing access to the models’ training corpora. For each benchmark instance, the model is run on the original prompt/input and on perturbed variants. The resulting change in behavior is converted into an input sensitivity score. These sensitivity scores are then aggregated to characterize memorization advantage distributions.

Two complementary analyses are performed. First, benchmark memorization advantage: for a fixed model, the sensitivity-score distribution on one benchmark or subset of benchmarks is compared against the distribution on the remaining benchmarks, and statistically significant elevation is interpreted as evidence that the benchmark may confer a memorization advantage. Second, model memorization advantage: for a fixed benchmark, one model’s sensitivity-score distribution is compared to those of other models; a significantly higher distribution suggests that model may have a memorization-related advantage on that dataset.

The paper studies multiple task families relevant to software engineering: code summarization/understanding, code generation/synthesis, test generation, vulnerability detection, and program repair/bug fixing. It emphasizes statistical comparison of sensitivity distributions rather than simple average performance, enabling relative judgments about whether sharp perturbation-induced degradation is atypical for a given model or task. The overall goal is to separate apparent benchmark success due to likely memorization from success due to broader learned generalization.

### 🧪 Experiments
The empirical study covers 8 open-source code LLMs and 19 benchmark datasets. The benchmarks span four to five task families depending on the paper section: code generation/synthesis, code understanding or summarization, vulnerability detection, test generation, and program repair/bug fixing. Particular attention is given to CVEFixes for vulnerability-related evaluation and Defects4J for bug fixing/program repair, both of which are often discussed as potentially contaminated benchmarks.

The main evaluation signal is perturbation sensitivity, used as a proxy for memorization advantage. The authors compare sensitivity-score distributions across models and datasets, with statistical significance testing to determine whether a model or benchmark shows unusually high sensitivity. They report model-level and task-level patterns rather than only raw benchmark accuracy.

Key findings include: StarCoder exhibits some of the highest sensitivity values, reaching up to about 0.8 on certain benchmarks such as APPS, while QwenCoder remains mostly below 0.4 across most benchmarks. Task families also differ strongly: code summarization tends to have low sensitivity, generally below 0.3, suggesting stronger generalization; test generation is much higher, around 0.4 to 0.7 with reported p<0.001, indicating greater memorization risk or fragility. In program repair, Defects4J shows significantly lower sensitivity, roughly 0.2 to 0.4 with p<0.01, than other APR benchmarks that range around 0.5 to 0.8. For security, CVEFixes remains below 0.1 across models, suggesting little memorization advantage despite prior leakage concerns. Overall, the experiments show wide variability in sensitivity across models, benchmarks, and task categories.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A central insight is that benchmark contamination cannot be assumed uniformly across software engineering tasks: some highly discussed datasets may actually be comparatively safe for evaluating generalization. The low memorization advantage observed on CVEFixes is especially important for vulnerability detection research, where benchmark leakage is a recurring concern. Likewise, Defects4J appears less problematic than other repair datasets, suggesting that poor robustness on APR tasks may reflect the intrinsic difficulty of repair rather than simple memorization.

Another important takeaway is that memorization behavior is not well explained by parameter count alone. Models with comparable scale exhibit very different sensitivity profiles, implying that architecture, data curation, post-training, and instruction tuning can significantly alter whether a model behaves as if it has memorized benchmark artifacts.

The paper also suggests that task structure matters: summarization appears to reward more transferable abstractions, whereas test generation and context-dependent repair may expose stronger dependence on seen patterns. For researchers, this means evaluation protocols should be task-specific rather than adopting a one-size-fits-all contamination assumption.

Limitations and open questions include the interpretation of perturbation sensitivity itself: high sensitivity may indicate memorization, but could also reflect brittleness to prompt variation or semantic drift introduced by perturbations. The method estimates memorization advantage indirectly rather than proving dataset inclusion in training. Future work could refine perturbation types for code semantics, combine this approach with provenance or deduplication analyses, and investigate whether sensitivity correlates with downstream failure modes in security-critical settings.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to software engineering, software maintenance, software security, automated program repair, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair because it directly evaluates whether strong code-LLM results on these tasks come from true generalization or hidden benchmark leakage. For a researcher working on vulnerability detection/repair and APR, it offers a practical, model-agnostic methodology for auditing benchmarks and models when training data are opaque, and its findings on CVEFixes and Defects4J are especially useful for selecting trustworthy evaluation datasets and designing stronger experimental protocols.

## 📄 [Towards Enabling An Artificial Self-Construction Software Life-cycle via Autopoietic Architectures](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.13934v1)  `80% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Daniel Rodriguez-Cardenas, David Nader Palacio, Denys Poshyvanyk
📅 **Published:** 2026-04-15  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
This position paper argues for replacing conventional post-deployment software maintenance with an autopoietic, self-constructing software life-cycle in which systems use foundation-model-driven reasoning to analyze themselves, generate improved replicas, and autonomously evolve. Its main result is a conceptual architecture, Psi-Arch, plus a reasoning unit formulation that frames how observation, intervention, and counterfactual reasoning could support self-repair, self-refactoring, and broader autonomous maintenance.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Proposes a paradigm shift from canonical machine-learning-assisted software maintenance to an artificial self-construction software life-cycle inspired by autopoiesis and artificial life.
- Introduces Psi-Arch, an autopoietic architectural framework adapted from von Neumann’s self-replicating model for software systems capable of producing improved replicas of themselves.
- Defines a self-construction reasoning unit, Gamma, intended to control maintenance decisions through observational, interventional, and retrospective/counterfactual reasoning.
- Articulates how foundation models can serve as the code-understanding, generation, and reasoning substrate for autonomous software evolution, self-refactoring, and self-repair.
- Identifies research gaps and open challenges for integrating causal reasoning, software engineering metrics, traceability, and FM-based agents into next-generation autonomous SDLC automation.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The paper is a position/conceptual design study rather than an empirical system paper. It proposes an autopoietic architecture called Psi-Arch for enabling self-construction in software systems. Psi-Arch extends the idea of self-replicating machines to software by treating the software system itself as an entity that can observe its own state, reason about maintenance needs, generate a modified replica, and replace or evolve into that replica if stability and quality conditions are satisfied. A central component is the reasoning unit Gamma, which is responsible for deciding when and how to trigger maintenance actions such as dead code elimination, self-refactoring, or other modifications. Gamma is described along three reasoning dimensions: observation, intervention, and retrospection. Observation uses probabilities over maintenance events conditioned on evidence and is associated with variational inference over software engineering quality metrics. Intervention uses causal reasoning, framed with do-calculus notions, to estimate the effect of modifying a program subcomponent on target quality metrics, potentially using traceability links and metric relationships. Retrospection uses counterfactual reasoning to imagine alternative maintenance actions that were not chosen and evaluate hypothetical outcomes. The envisioned workflow is that the software system continuously monitors internal signals such as quality metrics and links among artifacts, identifies when some requirement or metric threshold is violated, uses foundation-model-based reasoning and generation to synthesize a maintenance action or architectural change, constructs a new software replica embodying that change, and validates whether the resulting replica is sufficiently stable and improved before adoption. The paper emphasizes that foundation models are not just patch generators but embedded reasoning and construction mechanisms within the software, enabling code comprehension, generation, and causal decision support for self-maintenance and self-evolution.

### 🧪 Experiments
The paper does not report implemented experiments, datasets, baselines, or quantitative evaluation results. Instead, it provides a motivating scenario involving autonomous dead functionality elimination and outlines how Gamma would use software engineering metrics, traceability information, and causal reasoning to decide on self-refactoring actions. There are no benchmark studies, no APR or vulnerability datasets, no comparisons against existing maintenance automation approaches, and no numerical results; the contribution is conceptual and agenda-setting.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A key insight is that much of current software maintenance automation still assumes an external tool or engineer acts on the software, whereas Psi-Arch reframes maintenance as an endogenous capability of the software itself. This is especially relevant to research on autonomous repair and continuous evolution, where the boundary between the maintained system and the maintenance agent becomes blurred. Another insight is the proposed role of causal reasoning: rather than relying only on pattern-based patch generation, a self-constructing system should reason about why a change should improve software quality and what would happen under alternative modifications. This has implications for trustworthy APR, refactoring, and vulnerability remediation, where side effects and regressions are critical. The paper’s main limitation is that it stops at vision and formal framing; there is no concrete implementation, evaluation protocol, safety mechanism, or proof that self-generated replicas can be validated reliably in practice. Important open questions include how to guarantee correctness and security of self-generated replicas, how to constrain FM autonomy to avoid unsafe mutations, how to integrate runtime and repository signals for causal reasoning, how to certify improvements under changing requirements, and how to engineer governance, rollback, and human oversight in autonomous SDLC systems. For related research, the paper suggests a fertile direction at the intersection of agentic SE, self-adaptive systems, APR, causal inference, and foundation models for code.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to software maintenance, automated program repair, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair because it proposes a unifying conceptual framework in which these tasks become internal self-construction capabilities of the software system. Although it does not deliver an evaluated technique, it is valuable for a researcher in these areas because it reframes APR and security repair as components of a broader autonomous life-cycle, highlights causal reasoning as a missing ingredient for trustworthy repair decisions, and opens research directions for building self-evolving maintenance and security architectures on top of foundation models.

## 📄 [KindHML: formal verification of smart contracts based on Hennessy-Milner logic](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14038v1)  `70% relevant`

👤 **Authors:** Massimo Bartoletti, Angelo Ferrando, Enrico Lipparini, Vadim Malvone
📅 **Published:** 2026-04-15  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR, cs.LO

### 💡 TL;DR
The paper presents KindHML, an automated verification approach for smart contracts that translates a subset of Solidity and temporal specifications in first-order Hennessy-Milner Logic into Lustre for checking with Kind 2. It targets multi-transaction, business-logic vulnerabilities such as liquidity and front-running attacks, showing that more expressive temporal reasoning can uncover violations missed by existing tools.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces an automated formal verification framework for smart contracts focused on expressive temporal properties spanning multiple transactions.
- Defines a translation from an expressive subset of Solidity contracts into Lustre, enabling analysis with the Kind 2 model checker.
- Uses first-order Hennessy-Milner Logic as the specification formalism, allowing richer temporal and state-dependent attack properties than many existing smart contract analyzers.
- Implements the end-to-end approach in a toolchain that automates both contract/specification translation and model checking.
- Demonstrates effectiveness on a benchmark of contracts and attack-oriented temporal properties, including scenarios beyond the reach of prior analysis tools.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The approach appears to model smart contract behavior as a transition system amenable to temporal verification. A supported subset of Solidity is automatically encoded into Lustre, a synchronous dataflow/specification language accepted by Kind 2. ### ⚙️ Methodology
The paper models an agent system as a tuple of model, tools, environment, and harness, with the harness controlling the loop of context construction, model invocation, tool execution, observation integration, and state persistence. The main idea is to secure the harness itself because it mediates every interaction and maintains hidden execution state that external guardrails cannot observe.

SafeHarness organizes protection around four lifecycle-aligned layers:

1. Inform (input processing): all external content entering the harness is sanitized before being exposed to reasoning. This layer performs structural, pattern-based, and semantic filtering on user inputs, retrieved documents, tool outputs, and other context sources. The architecture includes explicit inter-layer feedback loops rather than isolated guards. Suspicious content seen in Inform raises scrutiny in Verify; verification failures can trigger rollback and degradation in Correct; degradation can tighten privilege ceilings in Constrain; and recovery can de-escalate verification. The gains are reported as consistent across the main harness configurations and attack settings, indicating that the approach generalizes beyond a single agent orchestration style. The paper also emphasizes that the interception mechanism can be tuned, suggesting a measured overhead/utility trade-off rather than a simple always-on maximum-defense setting.

A central insight is that many agent-security failures are not just model-level issues but harness-level systems failures: the same harness mechanisms that improve capability also create a concentrated attack surface. 📅 **Published:** 2026-04-15  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE, cs.AI

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper proposes an intrinsic runtime reliability monitor for LLMs that detects when a model sounds confident despite weak internal evidence by comparing hidden-state-based certainty with output softmax confidence. ### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces the Cognitive Dissonance Delta, defined as the gap between calibrated semantic confidence and latent confidence estimated from intermediate hidden states.
- Presents the Cognitive Circuit Breaker, a systems framework that inserts lightweight probe-based monitoring into the live inference path rather than relying on post-hoc black-box checking such as RAG verification or LLM-as-a-judge.

- Evaluates cross-dataset OOD generalization from ARC to OpenBookQA and shows that intrinsic reliability signals transfer for some architectures better than others.
- Describes an engineering-oriented hidden-state extraction pipeline designed to be stable on constrained GPU hardware during multi-layer probing experiments.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The framework monitors an LLM during its normal forward pass. For each prompt, the system extracts hidden states from candidate intermediate layers and records the model’s output logits for the generated answer token. Semantic confidence P_semantic is defined as the maximum softmax probability after temperature scaling the logits with T=1.5 to mitigate overconfidence. Latent confidence P_latent is produced by a lightweight logistic regression probe trained on hidden-state vectors to predict whether the model’s answer is correct. The key signal is the Cognitive Dissonance Delta, Delta = P_semantic - P_latent, where larger values indicate a model outwardly presenting high confidence while internally encoding uncertainty or falsehood. The authors train a separate linear probe for each layer using binary correctness labels derived by comparing generated multiple-choice answers to gold labels. The optimal extraction layer L_opt is selected as the layer with highest cross-validated AUROC, and that probe is then used for runtime monitoring. Instead of using a fixed detection threshold, the system calibrates a dynamic threshold tau on validation data by selecting the threshold that maximizes F1 from ROC-derived analysis. If Delta exceeds tau, the circuit breaker can trigger an intervention such as warning, halting generation, or routing to a deterministic fallback. The paper also emphasizes systems implementation details for scalable extraction, including memory-conscious hidden-state collection, atomic caching to avoid corrupted artifacts, expandable segments, and aggressive garbage collection to reduce OOM and fragmentation on 16GB Tesla T4 hardware.

The task format is multiple-choice QA. For every model and every layer, hidden states are extracted and logistic regression probes are trained to predict binary correctness. Datasets are class-balanced to 50/50 correct and incorrect examples, random-label controls are used as chance baselines, and 1,000-iteration bootstrapped confidence intervals plus paired bootstrap testing are used for significance testing. Metrics include probe AUROC, probe max F1 at the chosen threshold, Delta AUROC, Delta max F1, and comparison against the final layer. Results show intermediate-layer probes outperform final-layer signals across most settings, supporting the claim of final-layer representation collapse. Qwen achieves probe AUROC 0.708 on ARC and 0.736 on OBQA, with Delta AUROC improving to 0.725 and 0.750 respectively; final-layer AUROC is lower at 0.645 and 0.691, with significant gains. Optimal layers vary substantially by architecture, from early/mid layers in Qwen to central layers in DeepSeek and late layers in Gemma. The paper also claims the runtime monitor adds negligible overhead because the probe is lightweight and piggybacks on the existing forward pass, though detailed latency numbers are not included in the provided text.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A central insight is that useful truthfulness and uncertainty signals are often strongest in intermediate representations, not in the final layer used for token generation. This suggests a separation between internal factual state and final linguistic realization, with the latter potentially masking uncertainty through overconfident fluent outputs. For software engineering, the work argues that reliability mechanisms should move from expensive post-hoc validation to in-path, low-latency monitoring. The OOD results indicate that intrinsic truthfulness probes may generalize across related QA domains, but only for some architectures; Gemma’s weaker transfer suggests that latent truth representations and their accessibility by linear probes are model-dependent.
