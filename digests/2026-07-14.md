## 📄 [Mizzle: A Complete Concurrent Incorrectness Logic for Preventing False Alarms in Agentic Bug Finding](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11611v1)  `90% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Alexandre Moine, Sam Westrick, Joseph Tassarotti
📅 **Published:** 2026-07-13  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.PL

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper introduces Mizzle, a mechanized concurrent incorrectness separation logic for a substantial subset of OCaml 5 that can certify bug reports with machine-checked proofs, eliminating false alarms while remaining complete for real bugs. Mizzle is proved sound and complete in Rocq/Iris and is instantiated for stuckness, races, and non-linearizability, with a proof-of-concept showing how an LLM can produce certified bug findings.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Defines Mizzle, a concurrent incorrectness separation logic for a substantial subset of OCaml 5, parameterized by the notion of incorrectness being certified.
- Mechanizes Mizzle in the Rocq proof assistant on top of Iris, enabling machine-checkable bug witnesses rather than informal reasoning.
- Proves both soundness and completeness: any derivable bug report corresponds to a real incorrect execution, and every incorrect execution can in principle be derived in the logic.
- Provides three concrete instantiations of incorrectness: stuckness/undefined behavior, memory races, and non-linearizability of concurrent data structures.
- Demonstrates a proof-of-concept workflow in which an LLM accompanies a bug report with a machine-checked Mizzle proof, reducing false alarms in agentic bug finding.

### ⚙️ Methodology
Mizzle is an under-approximate, angelic concurrent separation logic designed to prove existence of buggy executions rather than all-safe executions. It targets a substantial subset of OCaml 5 by building on ZooLang, a formal small-step interleaving semantics for OCaml-style concurrency, including atomic operations and relevant semantic subtleties such as physical equality behavior for compare-and-set. The logic is embedded in Iris, reusing separation-logic assertions and resource reasoning, but reoriented toward incorrectness: derivations establish that some concrete bad behavior is reachable.

The logic is parameterized by an abstract notion of incorrectness, allowing the same proof framework to certify different bug classes. Core proof objects track program threads, execution traces, and separation-logic resources over heaps/ghost state. The thread assertions describe executable program fragments, while postconditions characterize target bad outcomes. Because the logic is under-approximate, rules are designed to preserve reachability witnesses through program steps, scheduling choices, and resource transformations. In the concurrent setting, Mizzle supports proving existence of one particular interleaving that triggers a bug, rather than reasoning universally over all interleavings.

A central methodological component is the mechanization in Rocq atop Iris. The authors formalize the syntax and semantics of the target language, define the logic and its proof rules, and establish metatheoretic guarantees. Soundness shows that any machine-checked derivation corresponds to a genuine incorrect execution in the operational semantics, so certified bug reports cannot be false alarms. Completeness shows that for every semantically incorrect execution there exists a corresponding derivation, ensuring the logic itself does not miss real bugs due to proof-system weakness. The framework is then instantiated to three notions of incorrectness: stuckness, races, and non-linearizability. This makes Mizzle more general than prior incorrectness logics that focused primarily on simpler fault models or non-concurrent settings.

The proposed usage model for LLM-based bug finding is not to trust the model’s natural-language explanation, but to require it to synthesize a Mizzle proof script alongside the report. The proof assistant checks the script; if it fails, the agent can iteratively refine the witness until it either finds a real bug or gives up. Thus, Mizzle serves as a semantic certification layer for agentic bug finding.

### 🧪 Experiments
The paper’s evaluation is a proof-of-concept rather than a large-scale benchmark study. The main experimental artifact is the mechanized Rocq/Iris development proving Mizzle sound and complete over the chosen OCaml 5/ZooLang subset. The authors also instantiate the logic for three bug classes—stuckness, races, and non-linearizability—and use a motivating buggy concurrent Treiber-style stack/client example to show how subtle concurrency bugs can be certified even when they are hard to reproduce by testing alone.

The showcased program is a buggy lock-free stack whose push operation uses an incorrect compare-and-set condition, leading to lost updates under certain interleavings. A client with two concurrent threads can trigger an assertion failure only rarely in practice, sometimes requiring more than a thousand runs, illustrating why test-case reproduction is insufficient for some concurrency bugs. Mizzle can instead construct a proof of reachability of the bad execution. The paper further shows that the same program can be reasoned about under different incorrectness notions, including direct proof of non-linearizability rather than encoding it indirectly as another fault.

The evaluation does not appear to focus on standard empirical metrics such as precision/recall, bug counts, or comparison against APR/VD baselines. Instead, the key results are formal and qualitative: machine-checked soundness and completeness, successful instantiation to multiple bug notions, and a demonstration that an LLM can use the mechanized framework to certify a bug report. The main 'quantitative' claim visible in the text is the practical observation that the motivating concurrency bug is difficult to trigger by repeated execution, reinforcing the value of proof-based certification over mere testing.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper’s main insight is that incorrectness logic is a natural fit for controlling false positives in LLM-based bug finding, because under-approximate proofs certify actual reachability of the reported bad behavior. This is particularly compelling for concurrency bugs, races, and linearizability violations, where conventional reproduction-based validation can be flaky or infeasible.

A second important insight is that completeness matters as much as soundness for this application. A proof system that is sound but incomplete could reject true bugs simply because no derivation exists, which would make it a poor certification layer for exploratory bug finding. By proving completeness, Mizzle ensures the logic is not itself the bottleneck in principle.

The parameterization over notions of incorrectness is also significant. For software security and vulnerability research, this suggests a path toward certifying richer bug classes beyond crashes, such as data-race-driven memory safety issues, semantic API misuse, or violations of higher-level concurrent object specifications. Direct support for non-linearizability is especially relevant to concurrent software verification and could inspire bug-finding tools for lock-free data structures.

Limitations include the current focus on a substantial subset rather than all of OCaml 5, and the absence of a broad empirical study of end-to-end LLM bug-finding performance, scalability, or proof synthesis success rates. The approach also assumes access to formal language models and mechanized semantics, which may be expensive to develop for mainstream systems languages. Another open question is how well LLMs scale in generating proofs for large, messy real-world codebases without human guidance. Integration with automated invariant/resource inference, counterexample-guided proof repair, and benchmarks on real bug datasets would be natural next steps.

For adjacent research, Mizzle bridges formal verification and practical bug finding: instead of replacing bug finders, it acts as a certification backend. This suggests a promising architecture for future vulnerability detection pipelines where neural components propose candidate issues and proof systems filter them to high-confidence reports.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to software engineering, maintenance, security, vulnerability detection, and automated repair because it addresses a core practical bottleneck: false alarms in automated and LLM-based bug finding. Its contribution is especially valuable for concurrency, semantic bugs, and hard-to-reproduce failures, offering a way to turn candidate bug reports into machine-checked witnesses of real defects.

For vulnerability detection and repair research, Mizzle provides a rigorous certification layer that could be paired with existing detectors, program analyzers, or repair agents: a detector proposes a bug, Mizzle certifies it, and a repair system can then act on only verified issues. The completeness result is particularly important for research pipelines, since it means the logic is expressive enough not to discard true bugs merely due to proof-system limitations. More broadly, the paper advances work on trustworthy AI-assisted software analysis by combining formal semantics, concurrent separation logic, and interactive proof checking in a way directly applicable to reducing noisy bug reports.

## 📄 [Knowledge-Guided Synthetic Bug Feedback for LLM-Based Unit Test Generation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11573v1)  `80% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Ziheng Wang, Maike Li, Chen Zhi
📅 **Published:** 2026-07-13  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
KITE is a repository-aware framework that converts historical real-bug mechanisms into retrievable, executable synthetic bugs, then uses those injected bugs as feedback to iteratively improve LLM-generated unit tests. On 172 Defects4J method-level tasks, it substantially improves real-bug detection over execution-, coverage-, mutation-, knowledge-, and search-based baselines, reaching 72.67% real-bug detection.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Proposes KITE, an LLM-based unit test generation and enhancement framework that uses historical real-bug mechanisms as executable synthetic-bug feedback rather than relying only on proxy signals like coverage or mutation.
- Introduces a two-part bug knowledge representation: semantic mechanism summaries capturing what/where/how of a bug mechanism, and structural instantiability representations that determine whether the mechanism can be injected into a new focal method.
- Designs a retrieval-and-injection pipeline that matches focal methods with compatible historical bug mechanisms using repository context, semantic relevance, and local AST structure, then instantiates accepted injected methods as feedback targets.
- Demonstrates through controlled experiments, ablations, and overlap analysis that synthetic-bug feedback materially improves bug-triggering input generation and oracle construction, including finding real bugs missed by all other compared methods.

### ⚙️ Methodology
KITE operates in four stages. First, in offline knowledge construction, it transforms historical real-bug records into a bug-mechanism knowledge base. Each real bug is normalized into a correct/buggy pair, aligned at the AST level, and decomposed into one or more bug units, which are method-level, AST-anchored local defect evidences preserving source location, node type, method context, and the correct/buggy code snippets. Related local changes can be merged into compound bug units. From these units, KITE derives semantic mechanism representations with three elements: the bug mechanism itself (what semantic constraint is violated), applicability conditions (where the mechanism can validly transfer), and injection intent (how to recreate the deviation in another method). It also derives structural instantiability representations by extracting local AST-centered structural views around behavior-bearing anchors, so that transfer is constrained by whether a target method contains compatible code structure.

Second, KITE retrieves base context from the target repository for a focal method, including type information, dependency relations, call and usage context, and oracle constraints. This repository-aware context helps determine whether a historical mechanism can generalize to the new method both semantically and operationally.

Third, in knowledge-guided synthetic bug injection, KITE selects candidate bug mechanisms using both semantic recall and structural recall. Semantic recall identifies mechanisms behaviorally relevant to the focal method, while structural recall checks whether the focal method has a local code shape that can carry the mechanism. KITE then maps structurally retrieved bug units back to reviewed mechanism groups, and automatically instantiates generalized buggy variants as injected methods local to the focal method. These injected methods must be compilable, executable, and behaviorally non-trivial; accepted injected methods serve as concrete synthetic bugs representing realistic defect mechanisms rather than arbitrary mutants.

Fourth, KITE performs bug-enhanced iterative test improvement. Initial LLM-generated tests are iteratively refined using the injected synthetic bugs as feedback targets. The idea is that if the generated tests can distinguish the original focal method from its mechanism-guided injected variants, they are more likely to contain the right triggering inputs and behavioral assertions to expose analogous real defects. This feedback loop steers the LLM toward semantic boundaries, bug-triggering conditions, and precise oracles that generic execution or coverage feedback often misses.

### 🧪 Experiments
The evaluation uses 172 method-level real-bug detection tasks from Defects4J. The primary outcome is real-bug detection rate (RBDR), with additional analyses on injection acceptance, synthetic-bug detectability, ablations, overlap with baselines, mechanism diversity, and cost/process behavior. KITE is compared against six classes of baselines, including execution-feedback, coverage-guided, mutation-guided, knowledge-based, and search-based test generation/improvement methods; named baselines in the excerpt include MutGen and CoverUp.

KITE achieves an RBDR of 72.67%. This outperforms MutGen by 20.34 percentage points and CoverUp by 24.41 percentage points, indicating that mechanism-guided synthetic bugs are more effective feedback targets than generic mutation or coverage objectives. In the ablation study, context retrieval, structural recall, semantic recall, and synthetic-bug feedback all contribute to performance, with removing synthetic-bug feedback causing the largest degradation: a 19.76 percentage point drop in RBDR. Process analysis shows that KITE can produce accepted injected methods for 97.67% of tasks, suggesting high practicality of the injection pipeline. The final generated test suites detect 81.85% of these injected methods across diverse bug-mechanism categories, showing that the feedback targets are both executable and useful for steering test generation. Overlap analysis further shows that KITE detects 19 real bugs missed by all other evaluated methods, which supports the claim that it captures defect behaviors not covered by standard proxy-guided approaches.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper’s main insight is that test generation improves when feedback targets encode realistic defect mechanisms rather than indirect proxies such as coverage increase or mutant killing. This is especially relevant for regression and vulnerability-oriented testing, where triggering the right semantic boundary and asserting the exact behavioral difference matters more than simply executing code. KITE reframes historical bug data not as repair templates but as transferable mechanism knowledge, which is a useful conceptual bridge between defect mining and test generation.

A second important implication is that transfer requires both semantic and structural compatibility. Purely semantic retrieval risks suggesting mechanisms that cannot be operationalized in the target code, while purely structural similarity misses behavior-level relevance. The combined use of repository context, semantic summaries, and structural instantiability is likely the reason the method generalizes better than simpler retrieval or mutation schemes.

Limitations and open questions remain. The excerpt suggests manual review is used when grouping and validating bug mechanisms, which may limit scalability or introduce curation bias in the knowledge base. The evaluation is on Defects4J method-level tasks, so it is unclear how well the approach scales to larger integration tests, security-sensitive code paths, or multi-method vulnerabilities. Another open question is how robust the injection process is for highly stateful, concurrent, or framework-heavy code where creating compilable and behaviorally meaningful synthetic bugs may be harder. There is also a residual validity question about whether synthetic bug detectability always correlates with real-bug detectability, although the strong overlap and unique-bug findings support the proxy’s usefulness. For related research, this points toward richer bug-mechanism libraries, security-specific mechanism retrieval, and coupling test generation with mined vulnerability patterns.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to research in software engineering, maintenance, automated program repair, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair because it turns historical defect knowledge into an actionable feedback loop for test generation. It advances work in bug detection by moving beyond weak proxies like coverage and mutation score, and its mechanism abstraction, retrieval, and injection ideas are directly applicable to defect- and vulnerability-oriented testing, regression validation, benchmark construction, and potentially APR pipelines that need stronger bug-triggering tests and oracles.

## 📄 [TerraRepair: A Tool-Grounded LLM Agent for Infrastructure-as-Code Repair](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11390v1)  `80% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Minase Mekete Mengistu, Juri Di Rocco, Phuong T. Nguyen, Davide Di Ruscio
📅 **Published:** 2026-07-13  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
TerraRepair is a bounded, tool-grounded LLM agent for repairing Terraform security misconfigurations that uses dependency-graph retrieval, provider-schema lookup, and scanner-in-the-loop verification, and escalates when deployment-specific context is missing. On vulnerable-by-design Terraform benchmarks, it substantially improves scanner-verified repair rates over a one-shot LLM baseline and sharply reduces unsupported 'claimed fixes' that do not actually verify.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Formulates Terraform security repair as a bounded tool-using process that returns either a scanner-verified patch or a structured escalation when necessary context is unavailable.
- Introduces TerraRepair, a ReAct-style LLM repair agent that combines Terraform dependency-graph queries, installed provider-schema lookup, and in-loop scanner reruns to ground repairs.
- Defines and empirically uses the claimed-vs-verified repair gap as an operational measure of unsupported or hallucinated LLM repair claims.
- Provides an empirical evaluation across two vulnerable-by-design Terraform repositories, three cloud providers (AWS, Azure, GCP), and two scanners (Checkov and Trivy), including ablation and escalation analyses.
- Publishes a replication package with code and curated data to support open science and reproducibility.

### ⚙️ Methodology
TerraRepair treats each Terraform scanner finding as a bounded agentic repair task. Starting from a reported misconfiguration, the system provides the agent with the scanner rule identifier, severity when available, remediation hint, resource identifier, cause lines, and the full original HCL resource block. Before repair, TerraRepair runs a security scanner to collect findings, builds a dependency graph from Terraform/HCL references across the repository, and loads the installed Terraform provider schemas through the Terraform CLI provider-schema interface. A single ReAct-style agent then operates with a fixed budget of ten steps and three tools: `graph_query` to retrieve cross-resource context such as KMS key ARNs, network identifiers, and security-group references; `schema_lookup` to inspect the installed provider schema and avoid unsupported attributes, invalid nested blocks, or writes to computed fields; and `scanner_run` to re-run the scanner on a candidate patch and use the result as feedback. The prompt constrains the agent to consult the schema before using unfamiliar constructs, obtain scanner verification before returning a repair, and output exactly one repaired resource block. If the scanner still reports the original issue, the agent revises the patch within the step budget. If the issue disappears, the agent emits the candidate repair. If the necessary deployment-specific context is absent from the codebase or schema, or the bounded process cannot converge, TerraRepair emits a structured escalation rather than fabricating a plausible but unsupported fix. This design explicitly addresses IaC-specific failure modes where syntactically valid edits may still be undeployable, semantically wrong, or security-ineffective.

### 🧪 Experiments
The evaluation studies four research questions: comparison against direct one-shot prompting, variation across cloud providers, contribution of individual architectural components, and characterization of findings that cannot be repaired autonomously. The benchmark uses two vulnerable-by-design Terraform repositories previously used in IaC repair research: TerraGoat and KaiMonkey. Experiments span AWS, Azure, and GCP configurations and use two IaC security scanners, Checkov and Trivy. TerraRepair is compared against a controlled one-shot baseline inspired by prior autonomous first-pass prompting, but normalized to the same LLM, scanner versions, finding population, patch-application logic, and scoring procedure for a fair comparison. The main metric is scanner-verified fix rate, contrasted with the model’s claimed repair rate to expose hallucinated or unsupported fixes; the paper also reports the claimed-vs-verified repair gap and performs a sampled semantic audit of verified repairs. On the combined AWS benchmark, TerraRepair raises scanner-verified fix rates from 26.6% to 78.4% for Checkov and from 44.8% to 72.4% for Trivy relative to the one-shot baseline. It also shrinks the baseline’s claimed-vs-verified gap of 44.8 to 73.6 percentage points to under 5 percentage points, indicating that most returned fixes actually survive scanner validation. In a sampled semantic audit, 78.9% of TerraRepair’s scanner-verified AWS repairs were judged correct by majority vote, suggesting that scanner success is useful but still imperfect as a repair oracle. The paper further states that ablation and escalation analyses identify provider-schema grounding and dependency retrieval as the main contributors to effectiveness, while missing deployment-specific context is the dominant boundary for full automation.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper’s main insight is that for IaC repair, direct LLM generation is not the core bottleneck; the bottleneck is grounding repairs in repository context, provider constraints, and a verification loop. This is highly relevant to automated repair and security because it shows how an LLM can overclaim success by removing a warning without producing a deployable or semantically correct fix, a failure mode analogous to overfitting in automated program repair. The structured escalation mechanism is important: instead of forcing autonomy, the system recognizes when required deployment-specific information such as KMS keys, CIDR ranges, certificates, logging endpoints, or least-privilege IAM scopes is unavailable. That makes the approach more realistic for operational use, especially in security-sensitive settings. A further implication is that scanner-verified repair should be treated as a necessary but insufficient oracle; the sampled audit showing 78.9% semantic correctness among verified repairs leaves residual risk and motivates stronger post-repair validation, potentially including deployment tests, policy simulation, or cloud-specific semantic analyzers. Limitations include the prototype status, evaluation on only two vulnerable-by-design repositories, reliance on scanner outcomes as the primary oracle, and partial evidence for clouds beyond AWS in the excerpt. Open research questions include how to generalize to larger industrial Terraform codebases, how to incorporate runtime/deployment feedback, how to repair findings requiring organization-specific security intent, and how to extend the approach to vulnerability classes where the 'correct' fix is underdetermined by static code alone.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is directly relevant to software maintenance, software security, automated program repair, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair because it studies how to move from static detection of IaC misconfigurations to verified automated remediation. It advances the researcher’s interests by showing a concrete agentic repair architecture with bounded tool use, explicit escalation, and a stronger evaluation lens than raw fix claims, including the claimed-vs-verified gap. For researchers working on APR or security repair, TerraRepair offers a useful design pattern: combine LLM patch synthesis with domain tools, structural context retrieval, schema or API constraints, and verification feedback, while acknowledging when the available evidence is insufficient for autonomous repair.

## 📄 [RepTran: Search-Based Repair of Transformer Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11193v1)  `80% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Yuta Ishimoto, Paolo Arcaini, Fuyuki Ishikawa, Masanari Kondo, Naoyasu Ubayashi, Yasutaka Kamei
📅 **Published:** 2026-07-13  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE, cs.AI

### 💡 TL;DR
RepTran is a search-based repair method for Vision Transformer feed-forward networks that localizes suspicious weights using a new neuron-variance score combined with Arachne’s bidirectional score, then optimizes them with differential evolution. On 18 fault benchmarks from CIFAR-100 and Tiny-ImageNet, it substantially outperformed random selection and Arachne, with an average repair rate of 74.7% and better efficiency than Arachne.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- A Transformer-specific repair method that targets FFN weights rather than treating ViTs like generic DNNs
- A new neuron score based on mean and variance of intermediate FFN activations to better localize faulty weights
- A combined suspiciousness score that modulates Arachne’s bidirectional score with the neuron score
- A differential-evolution repair phase that searches over selected FFN weights under a repair-versus-break fitness objective
- An evaluation on 18 fault benchmarks showing higher repair rates than random selection, Arachne, and ArachneW

### ⚙️ Methodology
RepTran has two phases. In the selection phase, it analyzes the ViT feed-forward networks and computes a suspiciousness score for each weight in both FFN matrices, W_bef and W_aft. The score combines a new neuron score and an existing bidirectional score from Arachne. The neuron score is derived from the mean and variance of intermediate FFN neuron activations, with the intuition that abnormal activation patterns indicate neurons tied to misbehavior. The bidirectional score captures both forward impact and gradient loss. These are combined to rank weights and select a small repair set. In the search phase, RepTran applies differential evolution to iteratively optimize only the selected weights. The fitness function aims to increase repair on the targeted misclassified inputs while preserving correct behavior on other inputs, so the patch is localized rather than a full retraining update. The implementation is evaluated on Vision Transformer models, with repair applied specifically to FFN parameters rather than attention layers.

### 🧪 Experiments
The evaluation uses 18 fault benchmarks built from CIFAR-100 and Tiny-ImageNet, with Vision Transformer as the target model. Baselines are random weight selection, Arachne, and ArachneW, where ArachneW controls the number of selected weights for a fairer comparison. Metrics reported include repair rate, runtime, and statistical/effect-size comparisons across benchmarks. RepTran achieved an average repair rate of 74.7%, with a best case of 95.2%. The original Arachne applied to ViT FFNs averaged 17.1% repair rate, with a maximum of 44.4%. RepTran statistically outperformed random selection and Arachne across all benchmarks, and effect size analysis showed it outperformed ArachneW regardless of the number of selected weights. Average runtime was 476.19 seconds, compared with 620.93 seconds for Arachne.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The main technical point is that Transformer-specific structure matters: repairing ViTs by selecting weights in FFNs works much better when localization uses activation statistics from intermediate FFN neurons, not just generic weight-importance signals. The paper also suggests that search-based repair remains viable for large Transformer components if the repair space is narrowed well. A limitation is that the study is confined to ViT image classification faults; it does not yet show generality to language models, other Transformer variants, or non-FFN components such as attention. Another open question is how robust the neuron score is across tasks and architectures, and whether the method can scale when the number of candidate weights or repair cases grows. The work also stops short of comparing against post-hoc model-editing methods like MEND or ROME under a shared repair protocol, so cross-paradigm positioning remains incomplete.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is directly relevant to software repair and vulnerability-style localized patching because it studies how to repair misbehavior in Transformer components without full retraining. It advances the researcher’s interests by proposing a concrete search-based repair pipeline, a new localization signal for suspicious parameters, and an empirical comparison against strong DNN repair baselines. Even though the target is Transformer reliability rather than code security, the core ideas around fault localization, selective modification, and controlled behavioral repair translate well to automated repair and trustworthy AI software.

## 📄 [Know Before Fix: QA-Driven Repository Knowledge Acquisition for Software Issue Resolution](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11111v1)  `80% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Haotian Lin, Silin Chen, Xiaodong Gu, Yuling Shi, Chengxi Pan, Jiaqi Ge, Mengfan Li, Jianghong Huang, Mengchieh Chuang, Beijun Shen, Haibing Guan
📅 **Published:** 2026-07-13  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper proposes ACQUIRE, a two-stage software issue resolution framework that first asks and answers targeted repository questions before attempting a fix. On SWE-bench Verified, this QA-driven knowledge acquisition improves Pass@1 by up to 4.4 percentage points over strong pre-repair baselines while adding only modest cost and latency.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces ACQUIRE, a QA-driven framework that explicitly separates repository knowledge acquisition from patch generation for automated software issue resolution.
- Proposes a multi-agent design with a Questioner, parallel Answerers, and a Resolver, turning implicit repository knowledge gaps into explicit QA knowledge before editing code.
- Defines a category-guided question taxonomy with four knowledge dimensions: Mechanism & Behavior, Design & Usage, Locating & Structure, and Ecosystem & Standards.
- Shows through oracle and full-system experiments that targeted pre-repair knowledge can recover previously failed issue instances and improve repair success.
- Provides analysis that the acquired knowledge is factually grounded, shortens repair trajectories, and directs the repair agent toward more causally relevant code regions.

### ⚙️ Methodology
ACQUIRE is a two-stage framework for issue resolution. In Stage 1, a Questioner takes the issue description and generates N targeted, self-contained, non-redundant repository questions using a structured prompt guided by four knowledge categories: Mechanism & Behavior, Design & Usage, Locating & Structure, and Ecosystem & Standards. Each question is then assigned to an independently instantiated Answerer agent. The Answerer runs in the same shell-based repository interaction environment as Mini-SWE-Agent but in read-only mode, autonomously explores the codebase, and produces an evidence-grounded answer that cites concrete repository artifacts such as file paths, functions, and behaviors. The Answerer is instructed to avoid speculation and explicitly acknowledge missing evidence when necessary. All Answerers run in parallel and do not share traces or intermediate findings, reducing coupling and preserving question-specific exploration. The output of Stage 1 is a structured knowledge set of QA pairs. In Stage 2, a Resolver receives the original issue plus the acquired QA knowledge and generates a candidate patch. The core design principle is decoupling comprehension from repair: rather than exploring the repository with a fix-first strategy, ACQUIRE first externalizes the agent’s uncertainty as explicit questions, then fills those knowledge gaps with grounded answers, and only then attempts patch generation. The paper also motivates the design with an oracle QA study: an oracle Questioner uses issue plus gold patch to ask one highly relevant question, and an oracle Answerer uses only gold files to answer it; injecting that QA into a repair agent demonstrates that targeted knowledge can unlock repairs on otherwise failed cases.

### 🧪 Experiments
The main evaluation is conducted on SWE-bench Verified, a curated benchmark of 500 real-world GitHub issues. The paper also uses SWE-bench Lite in a motivation/oracle study. In the oracle setup, the authors evaluate 116 SWE-bench Lite instances that Mini-SWE-Agent with DeepSeek-V3.2 fails to solve; adding a single oracle QA pair recovers 26 of those 116 failures, supporting the value of targeted knowledge acquisition. For the full ACQUIRE system, the paper compares against representative pre-repair repository exploration methods and a base repair agent across two backbone LLMs. The primary metric is Pass@1, with additional analysis of cost, execution steps, trajectory length, and repair behavior. ACQUIRE consistently outperforms representative pre-repair baselines on SWE-bench Verified, improving Pass@1 by up to 4.4 percentage points over the base repair setup while incurring only modest additional time and token cost. The authors further report that ACQUIRE reduces repair trajectory length and improves navigation toward causally relevant code regions on instances that were previously unresolved.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper’s key insight is that many repair failures stem less from weak reasoning than from missing repository-specific facts; therefore, pre-repair exploration should be driven by explicit knowledge-gap identification rather than direct fix attempts. The four-category question taxonomy suggests a practical decomposition of software repair knowledge needs that could be reused in other agentic SE tasks such as debugging, code review, and vulnerability triage. Parallel QA acquisition is appealing because it can scale comprehension while keeping individual explorations focused, though the independence assumption may miss opportunities to compose partial knowledge across questions. A likely limitation is added runtime and token cost relative to one-shot repair, even if reported as modest, and effectiveness may depend on the quality of generated questions and the Answerer’s ability to find evidence in large or poorly organized repositories. Another open question is how ACQUIRE behaves on tasks where the main challenge is test overfitting, multi-file semantic changes, or external environment setup rather than repository understanding. For related research, the work implies that structured, grounded intermediate artifacts may be more valuable than raw retrieved context, and that repository QA could serve as a reusable substrate for automated program repair, vulnerability localization, and trustworthy code agents.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to software engineering, maintenance, automated program repair, and security-oriented repository analysis because it directly addresses a core failure mode of LLM-based repair agents: insufficient understanding of large codebases. Its QA-driven, evidence-grounded comprehension stage is especially useful for researchers working on APR, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair, where correctness depends on API contracts, cross-module behavior, and factual grounding rather than surface-level retrieval. ACQUIRE advances this line of work by offering a concrete multi-agent architecture, a reusable taxonomy of codebase knowledge needs, and empirical evidence that better repository understanding translates into better repair outcomes.

- Introduces GBSE, which models adversary emulation procedures as directed attributed graphs and scores fidelity using normalized Graph Edit Distance across four semantic layers: technique, tactic, telemetry class, and Sigma logsource.

### ⚙️ Methodology
GBSE represents each adversary emulation plan as a directed attributed graph G=(V,E,phi_V,phi_E), where nodes are procedure steps and edges encode dependencies such as sequential precedence, privilege dependency, and data-flow or telemetry-trigger relations. Each node stores defender-relevant attributes including ATT&CK technique ID, tactic set, privilege context, telemetry classes, and Sigma rules. Fidelity between a control graph and a translated variant graph is computed as normalized Graph Edit Distance: 1 - GED/max(|V_c|, |V_v|), producing a continuous structural similarity score in [0,1]. The method applies layered node matching so that similarity can be localized to the level where fidelity first breaks down. The framework uses a bipartite matching formulation with typed edit costs, so substitutions, insertions, and edge changes correspond to meaningful procedural deviations such as changed observability, altered execution semantics, or modified dependency logic. The telemetry-intent parser normalizes free-text annotations into structured observable classes to support L2 comparison. GBSE is integrated with the broader evaluation rubric through Behavior Chain Fidelity (combining automated checks and structural similarity) and a composite score S = 0.4*BCF + 0.3*TR + 0.3*DV, where Technical Realism and Defensive Value come from manual assessment. The deployment gate is set at S >= 0.80, with a very high technical-realism requirement, so translations that look structurally similar but remain operationally weak are blocked.

### 🧪 Experiments
The empirical study evaluates a single full-procedure case: a 29-step ALPHV/BlackCat adversary emulation plan translated from Windows to Linux by an LLM. The main reason is low Technical Realism, measured at 0.43 versus the required 0.990. Defensive Value is 0.51. The paper also reports that all numerical results were reproduced from the reference implementation and verified against recorded pipeline outputs, and that the 49 Sigma rules used in the study passed specification validation with zero findings while covering the full ATT&CK technique set in the procedure.

The main insight is that preserving ATT&CK technique labels and tactic flow is not enough for useful cross-platform adversary emulation; defender relevance depends on whether the translated steps preserve observable behavior and realistic platform-native tradecraft. GBSE exposes exactly where fidelity breaks: in this case, the translation looks structurally correct at the ontology level but loses fidelity at the telemetry layer, which is where practical detection engineering lives. The recovery at the Sigma-logsource layer suggests that event-category equivalence can sometimes be maintained even when free-text telemetry annotations drift, which raises useful questions about how to reconcile annotation quality with actual rule deployability. ### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces a dual-memory framework for failure diagnosis that jointly models incident-specific diagnostic state as short-term memory and reusable cross-incident operational experience as long-term memory.

- Demonstrates strong empirical gains on a real-world Huawei microservice failure diagnosis benchmark over representative agentic reasoning and knowledge-augmented baselines.

### ⚙️ Methodology
OpsMem models failure diagnosis as iterative reasoning over two graph-structured memories. The short-term memory (STM) represents the current incident’s evolving belief state as a graph whose nodes include symptoms, acquired evidence, and candidate hypotheses, with edges such as derive, refine, support, and refute. This follows the structured belief-state idea from prior work like GoS, but uses it as the active state representation for diagnosis. The long-term memory (LTM) is a separate graph that stores reusable operational experience across incidents. Its nodes include patterns, cases, and procedures: patterns encode normalized diagnostic signal associations for common failure modes, cases store historical incidents related to those patterns, and procedures store operational troubleshooting steps. Weighted edges connect patterns to relevant cases and procedures.

The core coordination mechanism is cross-memory resonance (CMR), which aligns current diagnostic state with historical experience in three phases. First, signal coupling extracts symptom and evidence nodes from STM and normalizes them via an LLM into query signals. These query signals are semantically matched against signal descriptions inside LTM patterns. Each LTM signal gets a coupling score based on the maximum semantic similarity to the current query signals, with low-scoring matches pruned by thresholding. Second, pattern activation aggregates signal-level coupling into pattern-level scores using both the average strength of active signals and the fraction of a pattern’s signals that are covered. This balances match quality and pattern coverage, retaining only patterns whose activation scores exceed a threshold. Third, memory propagation spreads activation from the selected patterns along weighted edges to related case and procedure nodes, yielding an activated LTM subgraph relevant to the current incident.

Diagnosis then proceeds in a memory-conditioned multi-agent loop. Both the current STM and the activated LTM subgraph are serialized into prompts. A CentralAgent plans diagnostic tasks and delegates them to ExpertAgents. The ExpertAgents invoke tools such as log retrieval to gather evidence, analyze observations, and report findings back. The CentralAgent integrates these reports and updates STM with new evidence, revised hypotheses, and relations among them. After each update, the system checks whether diagnosis has converged; if not, the updated STM triggers a new round of CMR so the activated experience remains aligned with the latest state. After a case is solved, OpsMem runs a consolidation process that distills the incident into reusable long-term operational knowledge, updating LTM with patterns, historical case links, and procedures while attempting to avoid contaminating memory with unreliable content.

### 🧪 Experiments
The paper evaluates OpsMem on a real-world Huawei microservice failure diagnosis dataset. The task is iterative incident diagnosis from operational alarms and subsequent evidence collection. The reported comparison includes representative agentic-reasoning baselines and knowledge-augmented baselines, specifically methods in the style of ReAct/GoS-like structured reasoning and retrieval-based augmentation approaches discussed in the paper. Evaluation metrics include Match and Relevant, which measure diagnostic correctness and relevance of predicted failure causes or outputs. OpsMem consistently outperforms the strongest baseline, achieving up to 46.88% improvement in Match and up to 18.39% improvement in Relevant. The gains support the claim that dynamically coupling state and experience is more effective than using reasoning trajectories alone or statically retrieved operational knowledge. The paper also states that code and prompts are released for reproducibility.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A key insight is that failure diagnosis is neither purely a reasoning problem nor purely a retrieval problem; it requires tight interaction between the evolving incident state and reusable historical operational knowledge. OpsMem operationalizes this by making retrieval state-dependent and iterative, which is especially relevant for long-horizon diagnosis where evidence and hypotheses change over time. The graph-based decomposition of memory is also notable: STM captures fine-grained, incident-local reasoning artifacts, while LTM stores more reusable abstractions such as patterns and procedures, which is a useful design principle for operational AI systems.

For researchers in software maintenance and reliability engineering, the work suggests a path toward more robust incident diagnosis agents that preserve explicit intermediate state and learn from solved cases over time. For security-oriented diagnosis, vulnerability triage, or repair workflows, the same dual-memory idea could be adapted so STM represents the current exploit or bug investigation and LTM stores reusable vulnerability patterns, patch strategies, and prior incidents.

Open questions remain. The paper description indicates thresholded semantic matching and LLM-based normalization, which may be sensitive to prompt quality, embedding quality, and domain terminology drift. Long-term memory consolidation is promising, but the summary does not fully detail safeguards against error reinforcement, stale knowledge, conflicting cases, or catastrophic memory growth. Another limitation is likely transferability: results are shown on a Huawei microservice dataset, so generalization to other organizations, software stacks, or security incident domains remains to be established. It would also be valuable to know the cost and latency overhead of repeated multi-agent loops and recurrent memory activation in production settings.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant because it addresses automated diagnosis in complex software systems using structured reasoning, memory, and historical experience, all of which are directly connected to software engineering, software maintenance, and operational reliability. The evaluation emphasizes integrity-clean measurement, especially for SWE-bench, ensuring that the same patch-application and validation protocol is used regardless of surface. Analysis focuses primarily on cache-adjusted cost rather than raw pass rate, motivated by the claim that tool surfaces alter execution path efficiency more than task-solving capability. To explain observed differences, the paper decomposes behavior into three mechanisms: path-cost/edit friction, where code_only must express edits through generated scripts rather than direct edit primitives; failure-cost, where expensive failed runs dominate total cost; and pricing asymmetry/tool batching, where execute_code can package multiple operations into one LLM step and better control output volume. Additional conditional analyses compare cost on unanimous-pass, unanimous-fail, and split-decision subsets to localize where cost gaps arise.

### 🧪 Experiments
The experiments use two benchmarks. First, Artifact is a synthetic offline computation benchmark with deterministic grading over self-contained tasks. Second, SWE-bench Mini provides 100 repository-level bug-fixing tasks evaluated using the standard post-agent test_patch application workflow. Two agents are tested: Claude Code and Codex CLI. Three tool-surface conditions are compared in each cell: baseline, bash_only, and code_only. Metrics include pass rate, token usage, LLM/tool interaction statistics, and especially cache-adjusted cost; statistical comparisons are made with paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests. Across the four benchmark-agent cells, code_only outperforms or matches the cheapest rival on cost in three cells: Artifact/Claude shows a significant cost reduction of about -24.60% (p ≈ 7.37e-14), Artifact/Codex shows a directional reduction of about -6.70% but not significant, and SWE-bench/Codex shows a significant reduction of about -19.91% (p ≈ 2.02e-09). The exception is SWE-bench/Claude, where code_only is directionally more expensive by about +14.44%, but this difference is not statistically significant. Pass-rate differences are small, under roughly 3 percentage points across all cells and arms, and all headline pass-rate contrasts are non-significant. Mechanistic analyses show that for Claude on SWE-bench, output tokens scale with edit volume under code_only (Spearman rho about 0.488, p ≈ 2.59e-07), indicating edit friction. For Codex on SWE-bench, execute_code enables batching at about 2.5 tool calls per LLM step versus about 1.0 for baseline, reducing cost without reducing LLM-call count.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The central insight is that tool-surface choice in coding agents is a cost-engineering problem more than a capability problem, at least for the evaluated model sizes: changing tools rarely changes whether the task is solved, but can significantly change how much it costs to get there. This challenges broad claims that one surface is universally superior. Instead, the cheapest interface is jointly determined by task regime and agent architecture. The study also reveals that cost differences often come from failure trajectories rather than successful ones; in particular, the Claude-on-SWE-bench anomaly is attributed to expensive doomed runs rather than a uniform per-edit penalty. For automated repair and repository modification research, this suggests that evaluation should stratify successful and failed trajectories and report cost-conditioned analyses, not just aggregate success rates. A limitation is the scope: only two agents, one modification benchmark subset, and one execute_code implementation are studied, so generalization to other LLMs, repair agents, or security-oriented repair tasks remains open. Another open question is how these findings transfer to larger-scale codebases, vulnerability repair settings, and agents with stronger localization or patch-planning modules. The work also implies that future agent research should treat tool APIs, pricing models, and output-control affordances as first-class experimental factors, since they interact with benchmark regime and failure behavior in nontrivial ways.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to software engineering, software maintenance, and automated program repair because it rigorously studies how the action interface of coding agents affects the cost of repository-level modification tasks like SWE-bench without changing repair success. For researchers working on vulnerability detection or repair, it offers a strong methodological template for evaluating agent tool surfaces under controlled conditions, highlights the importance of failure-cost analysis for repair workflows, and suggests that simpler execute_code-based interfaces may improve efficiency in agentic repair systems depending on the agent and task regime.
