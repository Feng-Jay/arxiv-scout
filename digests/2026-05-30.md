## 📄 [EvoRepair: Enhancing Vulnerability Repair Agents Through Experience-Based Self-Evolution](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30105v1)  `100% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Haichuan Hu, Guoqing Xie, Quanjun Zhang, Jiawei Liu, Shengcheng Yu, Chunrong Fang, Zhenyu Chen, Liang Xiao
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-28  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
EvoRepair is an automated vulnerability repair framework that turns LLM repair trajectories into a reusable experience bank, then uses retrieved experiences to guide future repairs within and across vulnerabilities. Across PATCHEVAL and SEC-bench, it substantially improves repair success over 12 baselines, achieving 90.46% overall with strong transfer to VUL4J across datasets, languages, and model backbones.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces experience-based self-evolution as a new direction for automated vulnerability repair, focusing on accumulating and reusing structured repair knowledge rather than treating each repair attempt as isolated.
- Proposes EvoRepair, a cyclic learn-and-repair framework that retrieves relevant historical experiences, extracts new experiences from repair trajectories, and updates an experience bank with quality-aware scoring.
- Supports both intra-vulnerability learning during iterative repair and cross-vulnerability knowledge reuse via domain-specific, continuously refined repair experiences.
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art results against 12 representative AVR baselines on PATCHEVAL and SEC-bench, outperforming strong LLM and self-evolving agent baselines by large margins.
- Shows transferability of learned repair experiences on VUL4J, suggesting robustness across datasets, programming languages, and backbone models.

### ⚙️ Methodology
EvoRepair is built as an experience-based self-evolving agent framework layered on top of a vanilla LLM repair agent. Its central design is a cyclic two-stage learn-and-repair process. In the repair stage, when facing a new vulnerability or a still-unfixed instance in a later repair turn, the agent retrieves relevant past experiences from an experience bank. These experiences are not raw logs or example patches, but structured repair knowledge abstracted from previous repair trajectories, including successful repair patterns, failure modes, diagnostic insights, and strategic guidance for navigating the repair process. The retrieved experiences are used to steer the LLM toward more promising repair directions, reduce repeated mistakes, and stabilize long-horizon reasoning. In the learning stage, EvoRepair analyzes historical or newly generated repair trajectories and distills them into reusable domain-specific experiences. It then updates the experience bank using quality-aware scoring so that higher-value experiences are retained and refined over time. This mechanism enables self-evolution at two levels: within a single vulnerability, where the agent can accumulate knowledge from intermediate attempts and avoid revisiting unproductive paths, and across vulnerabilities, where it can transfer knowledge among similar cases that share CWE/CVE characteristics, causes, contexts, or repair patterns. The overall objective is to move AVR away from stateless stochastic trial-and-error toward experience-guided iterative refinement with long-term memory.

### 🧪 Experiments
The paper evaluates EvoRepair on two main automated vulnerability repair benchmarks, PATCHEVAL and SEC-bench, using GPT-5-mini as the backbone model, and compares against 12 representative baselines spanning traditional AVR, LLM-based AVR, and agent/self-evolving approaches. Reported headline results are 93.47% on PATCHEVAL, 87.00% on SEC-bench, and 90.46% overall, all the best among compared methods. Relative to the strongest recent LLM-based baseline LoopRepair, EvoRepair improves by 39.56% on PATCHEVAL and 33.50% on SEC-bench. Relative to IntentFix, it improves by 70.86% and 50.50% on PATCHEVAL and SEC-bench, respectively. It also surpasses the recent self-evolving software engineering agent Live-SWE-Agent by 6.98% overall. Beyond the main benchmarks, the authors conduct transfer experiments on VUL4J to assess whether the learned experiences generalize beyond the original training/evaluation settings. These transfer studies indicate that EvoRepair’s experience bank remains useful across different datasets, different programming languages, and different LLM backbones, supporting the claim that the framework captures reusable vulnerability-repair knowledge rather than overfitting to a single benchmark or model.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper’s main insight is that AVR benefits not just from stronger base LLMs or longer repair loops, but from explicitly converting repair attempts into structured, reusable experience. This addresses two practical weaknesses in existing LLM-based repair systems: repeated local mistakes during iterative repair and failure to exploit commonality across vulnerabilities. The work also suggests that retrieval of distilled repair knowledge may be more effective than static retrieval of patches or examples because the stored information is generalized and continuously refined. For software security and automated repair research, this implies a promising shift toward persistent memory, experience management, and self-improving agent architectures. Potential limitations and open questions include the exact design and granularity of experience representations, how retrieval quality and scoring affect performance, whether experience banks may accumulate noisy or misleading knowledge over long horizons, and how well the approach scales to unseen vulnerability classes, larger real-world projects, or settings with imperfect localization and verification. Another important implication is that benchmark gains should be interpreted alongside costs, retrieval overhead, and maintenance of the evolving memory store, which may matter in deployment.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to research on software engineering, software maintenance, software security, automated program repair, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair because it directly advances the state of the art in automated vulnerability repair using agentic LLMs. Its core contribution—experience-guided self-evolution—offers a practical mechanism for making repair agents more reliable, transferable, and efficient, which is especially valuable for secure maintenance workflows and large-scale vulnerability remediation. For researchers working on APR or vulnerability repair, EvoRepair provides both a strong empirical baseline and a new design pattern centered on long-term memory, trajectory distillation, and cross-instance knowledge reuse.

## 📄 [Minimal Prompt Perturbations Lead to Code Vulnerabilities: Prompt Fragility and Hidden-State Signals in Coding LLMs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29737v1)  `100% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Alexander Sternfeld, Andrei Kucharavy, Ljiljana Dolamic
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-28  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR, cs.CL, cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper shows that coding LLMs are security-fragile: tiny prompt edits, including a single-character mutation, can change generated code from secure to vulnerable even when the prompt meaning is largely unchanged. By probing hidden states, the authors find that some vulnerability risk is detectable before generation, especially for input-validation-style flaws, while vulnerabilities caused by insecure local choices are harder to predict and likely require intervention during decoding.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Establishes that minimal prompt perturbations can harm not only functional correctness but also the security of LLM-generated code, with even single-character changes sometimes inducing vulnerabilities.
- Provides a multilingual, multi-model evaluation across CWE categories using CWEval, separating security-only changes from functionality changes to study how perturbations specifically affect secure code generation.
- Shows that perturbation effects depend differently on mutation type versus mutation location, with some vulnerability outcomes driven by where the prompt is altered and others by what specific alteration is made.
- Demonstrates via hidden-state probing that vulnerability susceptibility is partially encoded in prompt representations before code generation, but unevenly across vulnerability classes.
- Finds systematic per-CWE differences in predictability: input-handling vulnerabilities are substantially easier to predict from hidden states than secure-defaults vulnerabilities.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The study evaluates prompt fragility for code security using the CWEval benchmark, which pairs each task with both a functional test oracle and a security test oracle. The benchmark spans five programming languages (C, C++, Go, JavaScript, Python) and broad CWE coverage per language. The authors test three open-source coding LLMs at temperature 0: CodeLlama-70B-Instruct, DeepSeek-Coder-33B-Instruct, and Qwen3-Coder-30B-A3B-Instruct.

For each prompt, they generate code from the original version and from systematically mutated variants. The mutation operators are: (1) single-character substitution, where one character is replaced by a random different ASCII letter; (2) three-character substitution, where three characters within one token are replaced by random ASCII letters; and (3) whole-token replacement, where one tokenizer-defined token is replaced by a similar random token chosen from the top-10 nearest tokens according to tokenizer embedding cosine similarity. This creates controlled perturbations with increasing semantic disruption.

Each generated program is evaluated along two axes: whether it is functional and whether it is jointly functional and secure. This lets the authors distinguish purely security-driven flips from cases where security changes simply because functionality changed.

To analyze whether vulnerability risk is encoded before generation, the authors extract the hidden activation of the prompt’s last token from individual transformer layers and train probes to predict outcomes. They fit one probe for each model-language-CWE-target combination, where targets are functionality and joint functionality-plus-security. Two probe families are used: L2-regularized logistic regression as a linear probe and a two-layer MLP as a shallow nonlinear probe. Data is split with a stratified 20% test set, and the remaining 80% is used for model selection and hyperparameter tuning with 5-fold cross-validation in a two-phase search over layers, regularization strengths, and MLP sizes. This design is intended to measure whether vulnerability susceptibility is linearly or nonlinearly decodable from prompt representations and how that varies by CWE.

### 🧪 Experiments
The experiments use CWEval across C, C++, Go, JavaScript, and Python, and three coding LLMs: CodeLlama 70B, Qwen3-Coder 30B, and DeepSeek-Coder 33B. Original-prompt baseline pass rates already show substantial security gaps: for example, Qwen3-Coder is the strongest overall with functional-secure rates of 33.3% (C), 52.4% (C++), 38.6% (Go), 43.5% (JavaScript), and 56.0% (Python), while CodeLlama and DeepSeek-Coder are generally lower on joint secure correctness.

The core perturbation experiment evaluates how often mutated prompts change functionality or security outcomes. The main finding is that even single-character mutations can alter both, and that many CWEs are sensitive to such minimal edits. The authors explicitly isolate security-driven changes by considering cases where functionality remains unchanged but security flips, showing that prompt fragility affects code security directly rather than only through task failure.

For representation analysis, the authors train logistic-regression and two-layer-MLP probes on hidden states and report predictive performance using AUC. The key quantitative result is that input-handling vulnerabilities are more predictable from prompt hidden states than secure-defaults vulnerabilities: mean AUC 0.753 versus 0.674, respectively. This indicates stronger pre-generation signal for vulnerabilities involving omitted validation/sanitization than for vulnerabilities caused by a single insecure local code choice, such as weak cryptography or unsafe parameter selection.

The paper also includes sensitivity analyses in the appendix with temperature 0.8 and additional models (gpt-oss 120B and Qwen2.5-Coder 3B) to test whether the observed fragility generalizes across decoding settings, model families, and model scales.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A central insight is that the security threat model for coding assistants must include ordinary prompt variation, not just adversarial prompt injection. Realistic edits such as typos, rewordings, or token substitutions can be enough to induce exploitable insecure code. This matters for software engineering workflows because developers commonly paraphrase prompts, edit comments, or iteratively refine requests without assuming security consequences.

The hidden-state probing results suggest that vulnerability risk is not uniform across weakness types. Input-handling weaknesses appear to be reflected more globally in prompt understanding, making them more amenable to pre-generation detection or guardrails. In contrast, secure-defaults weaknesses depend on later local decisions during decoding, so they are less visible from prompt representations alone and may require token-level constrained decoding, intermediate monitoring, or post-generation repair.

A useful conceptual distinction in the paper is between vulnerabilities caused by missing validation logic and those caused by one insecure implementation choice. This distinction can guide future work on vulnerability detection and repair in code LLMs: the former may benefit from prompt-side analyzers or hidden-state risk classifiers, while the latter may need decoder-time interventions or static/dynamic post-checking.

Limitations include dependence on CWEval task design and test oracles, evaluation on a fixed set of open-source models, and use of relatively simple probes that show decodability rather than causality. The paper also focuses on prompt perturbations rather than richer interactive IDE contexts, retrieval augmentation, or multi-turn coding sessions. Open questions include whether robustness can be improved through training-time augmentation, whether hidden-state signals can be used for real-time security gating, and how to connect these findings to automated secure repair loops.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to research in software security, vulnerability detection, vulnerability repair, software maintenance, and automated program repair because it studies when coding LLMs introduce vulnerabilities under realistic prompt variation and identifies signals that may enable early detection. It advances the researcher's work by reframing vulnerability generation as a robustness problem, offering a CWE-level analysis of failure modes, and suggesting where intervention should occur: before generation for validation-related flaws and during/post generation for insecure-defaults flaws. These findings can directly inform secure coding assistants, vulnerability-aware prompt engineering, decoder-time defenses, and repair pipelines for LLM-generated code.

## 📄 [Agora: Toward Autonomous Bug Detection in Production-Level Consensus Protocols with LLM Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29910v1)  `90% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Xiang Liu, Sa Song, Zhaowei Zhang, Huiying Lan, Jason Zeng, Ming Wu, Michael Heinrich, Yong Sun, Ceyao Zhang
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-28  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE, cs.AI

### 💡 TL;DR
Agora is a domain-aware multi-agent bug-finding framework for consensus protocol implementations that combines hypothesis-driven testing, protocol-specific bug patterns, and LLM agents to explore deep state-dependent behaviors. Across Raft, EPaxos, HotStuff, and BullShark, it found 15 previously unknown safety-violating protocol-level logic bugs, while prior LLM-based agents found none of these bugs.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces the first LLM-based multi-agent framework explicitly designed to detect protocol-level logic bugs in distributed consensus implementations using consensus-domain knowledge.
- Integrates hypothesis-driven testing with three specialized agents—Orchestrator, Strategy, and TestGen—to separate workflow coordination, scenario synthesis, and executable test generation.
- Systematizes consensus-specific bug knowledge through protocol bug patterns and triggering constraints, focusing on safety/liveness-relevant logic faults rather than low-level implementation bugs.
- Demonstrates effectiveness on four production- or research-grade consensus implementations across both CFT and BFT settings, discovering 15 zero-day safety bugs.
- Provides comparative and ablation evidence that each agent/component is necessary, with effectiveness dropping by 73–100% when individual design elements are removed.

### ⚙️ Methodology
Agora is built around the hypothesis-driven testing (HDT) paradigm, where each candidate bug is framed as a vulnerability hypothesis with preconditions, triggering actions, expected buggy behavior, and oracle assertions. The system takes as input repository knowledge, consensus bug patterns, and protocol constraints, and iteratively searches for bugs under a fixed exploration budget. Its architecture contains three role-specialized agents. The Orchestrator agent maintains global exploration state, coordinates inter-agent communication, analyzes previously discovered bugs, and summarizes the current search context to guide future exploration. The Strategy agent uses repository knowledge, the orchestrator's state summary, historical bug exploitation signals, protocol constraints, and a library of consensus bug patterns to synthesize attack scenarios likely to violate consensus invariants. These scenarios are not generic prompts; they are domain-constrained hypotheses about distributed executions, state combinations, message orderings, recovery behaviors, or adversarial conditions. The TestGen agent then converts each synthesized scenario into concrete repository-specific unit tests and uses a reflection loop: generate tests, execute them, analyze failures, and retry until the test is valid or a retry limit is reached. This allows the framework to progressively refine tests so they compile, run, and expose candidate safety violations. The design is motivated by the observation that protocol-level logic bugs span multiple functions, states, and execution phases, making single-pass code inspection ineffective. Agora therefore explicitly separates global control, protocol reasoning, and executable validation. The domain knowledge it uses includes consensus-specific bug classes such as Recovery & Execution Divergence, Persistence & Monotonicity Violations, Dependency & Topology Flaws, Message Binding & Signature Violations, and Resource & Operational Visibility Violations. By combining these bug classes with HDT-style scenario generation and iterative test validation, Agora reasons about global protocol invariants and produces concrete evidence of bugs instead of only speculative warnings.

### 🧪 Experiments
The evaluation studies four popular consensus implementations spanning both crash fault-tolerant and Byzantine fault-tolerant protocols: Raft, EPaxos, HotStuff, and BullShark. The paper reports using four state-of-the-art LLMs within Agora and compares against existing LLM-based agent or analysis baselines that mainly target localized implementation bugs. The primary target is protocol-level logic bugs that violate safety properties, rather than low-level bugs such as memory errors or simple coding mistakes. The main result is that Agora discovers 15 previously unknown zero-day protocol-level logic bugs across the four repositories, whereas existing LLM-based agents fail to detect any such protocol-level logic bugs. The paper also includes comparative analysis and ablation studies showing that domain-aware multi-agent collaboration is critical: removing a single component reduces effectiveness by 73% to 100%. Qualitatively, the comparison emphasizes that baselines can surface low-level implementation issues, but not deep stateful protocol errors, while Agora can generate protocol-aware attack scenarios and executable tests that expose consensus safety violations.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper's central insight is that protocol-level bug detection in distributed systems is not just a stronger version of ordinary code bug finding; it requires explicit reasoning about system-wide invariants, multi-stage state transitions, and protocol-specific constraints. This explains why general LLM bug detectors and even generic agentic systems underperform: they are effective on local code smells but do not naturally explore distributed executions or adversarial schedules. Agora suggests that domain-aware decomposition is more important than simply using a larger model. For software engineering research, this points toward specialized multi-agent workflows for other hard domains such as storage systems, concurrency control, and smart contracts. For security and maintenance, the approach is promising because it returns executable tests and structured bug hypotheses, which can support regression testing and patch validation. Likely limitations include dependence on manually curated consensus bug patterns and constraints, the cost and variability of iterative LLM-driven test generation, and uncertain generalization to protocols or languages with very different tooling or semantics. Open questions include how to automate expansion of the bug-pattern library, how to combine the method with symbolic execution or model checking, how well it scales to larger protocol stacks and integration environments, and whether the same architecture can be extended from bug detection to automated repair or patch suggestion while preserving protocol safety.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to the researcher's interests because it targets deep logic-bug detection in production-grade distributed systems, a setting directly connected to software engineering, software maintenance, security, and vulnerability discovery. It advances the state of the art beyond function-level LLM analysis by generating protocol-aware attack scenarios and executable tests for consensus implementations, offering ideas that are directly transferable to vulnerability detection, regression-test synthesis, and potentially automated repair for complex stateful systems.

## 📄 [Dissecting the Black Box: Circuit-Level Analysis of LLM Vulnerability Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29901v1)  `90% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Syafiq Al Atiiq, Chun Zhou, Christian Gehrmann
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-28  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR, cs.LG

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper uses mechanistic interpretability to reverse-engineer how Gemma-2-2b detects software vulnerabilities in C/C++ code and finds that the model mainly identifies vulnerable code indirectly by detecting the absence of safe coding patterns rather than matching explicit vulnerability signatures. Through circuit tracing and ablation, the authors show that vulnerability detection is implemented by sparse, localized circuits involving a few early attention heads, Layer 7 MLP neurons, and a critical late layer, with substantial causal impact on prediction accuracy.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces one of the first circuit-level mechanistic interpretability studies of LLM-based software vulnerability detection.
- Finds a counterintuitive 'safety detector' mechanism: the model classifies code as vulnerable primarily when attention heads that recognize safe coding patterns fail to activate.
- Identifies specific causal components for detection, including attention heads L5-H2 and L7-H6, Layer 7 MLP neurons encoding vulnerability-related features, and a critical dependence on Layer 11.
- Demonstrates that vulnerability detection is sparse and modular, using about 16.1% of model capacity rather than broadly distributed computation.
- Provides causal validation via ablation, showing that removing Layer 11 reduces accuracy from 100% to 6% and ablating only 20 Layer 7 neurons cuts performance by 50%.
- Argues that circuit-level explanations could support more trustworthy, debuggable, and targetable security analysis systems.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The study analyzes Gemma-2-2b, a 26-layer transformer with 8 attention heads per layer and hidden size 2304, without task-specific fine-tuning. The authors use Circuit Tracer to inspect internal computation during binary vulnerability classification on C/C++ functions. For each sample, they perform full-layer tracing with gradient-based attribution and an attribution threshold of 0.01, extracting attention head contributions, MLP activations, residual stream effects, and circuit structures associated with the model’s decision. They also conduct L0 norm analysis over latent activations, treating dimensions with magnitude above 1e-6 as active, to measure sparsity and estimate how much of the model is engaged by the task. The interpretability analysis focuses on comparing internal pathways for vulnerable versus non-vulnerable inputs, identifying components that consistently activate for safe code and components that encode vulnerability-related features. Finally, the authors run causal interventions by ablating individual layers and selected neurons to test whether the discovered components are necessary for correct predictions.

### 🧪 Experiments
Experiments are conducted on a balanced subset of 472 PrimeVul samples: 236 vulnerable and 236 non-vulnerable C/C++ functions, covering 9 major CWE groups such as buffer errors, input validation flaws, null dereferences, use-after-free, and integer overflows. PrimeVul is chosen because it is a higher-quality benchmark than earlier datasets, with reported label accuracy of 86-92%. The model under study is Gemma-2-2b, run on a single NVIDIA A100 80GB GPU using Circuit Tracer, PyTorch 2.0, and TransformerLens. The empirical evaluation is interpretability-centered rather than benchmark-comparative: the key outputs are traced circuits, attribution patterns, sparsity measurements, and ablation effects. Quantitatively, the authors report that vulnerability detection uses only about 16.1% of model capacity, indicating sparse computation. Causal ablations show very large effects: removing Layer 11 drops vulnerability detection accuracy from 100% to 6%, and ablating only 20 neurons in Layer 7 reduces accuracy by 50%. The paper also identifies early attention heads in Layers 5 and 7 as important for recognizing safe patterns, with Layer 7 MLP neurons contributing vulnerability-related representations.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The central insight is that LLM vulnerability detection may be fundamentally asymmetric: instead of directly recognizing vulnerability signatures, the model appears to rely on detectors for safe code patterns and defaults toward vulnerability when those detectors do not fire. This raises important questions for software security applications, because such behavior may be brittle under distribution shift, unusual safe implementations, or adversarial code that suppresses expected safety cues without being truly vulnerable. The finding of sparse, localized circuits is encouraging for interpretability and model editing, suggesting that targeted debugging, auditing, or patching of vulnerability behavior may be feasible without retraining the full model. At the same time, the study is limited by its focus on a single relatively small LLM, a modest 472-sample subset, only C/C++ code, and an interpretability framework based on one tracing method and threshold choice. The abstract and extracted text emphasize strong ablation effects, but broader generalization across datasets, languages, models, and real-world vulnerability patterns remains open. An additional implication is that future vulnerability detectors should be assessed not only on predictive accuracy but also on whether they encode genuine security semantics versus proxies such as stylistic or 'safe-looking' patterns.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to research in software engineering, software maintenance, software security, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair because it goes beyond measuring detector accuracy and explains the internal mechanisms by which an LLM makes security judgments. For researchers building or evaluating automated vulnerability analysis or repair systems, the paper offers actionable insight into failure modes, causal components, and opportunities for targeted model improvement, auditing, and trust calibration in security-critical code intelligence systems.

## 📄 [Inferring Code Correctness from Specification](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29822v1)  `90% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Tambon Florian, Papadakis Mike
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-28  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE, cs.AI

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper proposes TRAILS, a method for judging whether a single code sample is correct by asking an LLM to reason over concrete input-output behaviors against the natural-language specification instead of reasoning over the code itself. Across LiveCodeBench and CoCoClaNeL, TRAILS consistently outperforms Zero-Shot CoT and HoarePrompt, improving MCC by up to 39% and yielding more stable decisions across reruns.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces TRAILS (Targeted Reasoning Agreement via Inputs and Specifications), a low-trust correctness inference framework that validates code by grounding LLM reasoning in executed input-output pairs rather than source code analysis.
- Uses specification-driven category partitioning to generate diverse candidate inputs intended to cover different behavioral regions and expose incorrect program behavior.
- Adds an input validation/repair stage so generated inputs conform to the program’s expected format before being used for behavioral checking.
- Aggregates binary conformance judgments across multiple executed inputs to infer whether a program is likely correct, reducing reliance on a single brittle LLM decision.
- Provides an empirical evaluation on two benchmark datasets and three open-source LLMs, showing better accuracy, stability under seeded reruns, and broader coverage of uniquely correctly labeled samples than prior baselines.

### ⚙️ Methodology
TRAILS addresses the problem of inferring correctness of a single code candidate given only its natural-language specification and no trusted oracle. The method is intentionally designed as a low-trust pipeline where the specification is the only trusted artifact. In phase 1, TRAILS generates candidate test inputs from the specification using category partitioning: the model extracts relevant behavioral categories, constraints, and preconditions from the task description, then derives diverse inputs intended to span distinct cases rather than only typical examples. Because LLM-generated inputs may be malformed or incompatible with the target program, TRAILS includes a repair/validation loop inspired by prior LLM test-generation work: inputs are repaired within a fixed budget and retained only if they execute successfully and match the expected input format of the candidate program; otherwise they are discarded. In phase 2, each validated input is executed on the candidate code to obtain a concrete output. The LLM is then prompted with only the specification and the resulting input-output pair, and asked to decide whether that behavior conforms to the specification. Crucially, the code itself is hidden during this step, preventing the model from being biased by plausible but wrong implementation logic and forcing reasoning to occur at the behavioral/specification level. Each input-output-specification triplet yields a binary conformance judgment, and these judgments are aggregated across multiple inputs to estimate whether the overall program is correct. TRAILS uses a threshold over the number or proportion of non-conforming behaviors to classify the code as likely correct or incorrect. This design aims to capture dynamic bugs that static reasoning may miss, while also reducing order bias and one-shot instability that affect single-pass code-based prompting approaches such as Zero-Shot CoT or Hoare-style verification prompts.

### 🧪 Experiments
The evaluation studies four research questions: comparative performance, cost, stability across reruns, and coverage of consistently correctly labeled samples. TRAILS is evaluated on two datasets: LiveCodeBench and CoCoClaNeL, representing code-generation tasks with natural-language specifications. The authors test three relatively recent open-source LLMs that fit on a consumer-grade single GPU: Qwen3Coder-30B, Devstral-Small-24B, and Olmo3.1-Instruct. Baselines include HoarePrompt, which statically reasons over code using Hoare-logic-style prompting, and a Zero-Shot Chain-of-Thought baseline that directly judges code correctness from the specification and code. The main metric emphasized is Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC). Results show that TRAILS improves MCC across the board, with gains up to 39% relative to Zero-Shot CoT, and it consistently outperforms HoarePrompt. The paper also reports cost considerations: token consumption is mainly driven by repairing invalid inputs and handling problematic code, especially on CoCoClaNeL tasks that use standard input and are harder for models to format correctly. Beyond raw accuracy, TRAILS is more stable across seeded runs, preserving labels more consistently despite LLM non-determinism. It also correctly labels a larger number of unique code samples than competing approaches, although the authors note substantial overlap among methods, suggesting complementarity rather than strict dominance.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The central insight is that correctness inference improves when LLMs are asked to judge observable behavior against the specification rather than inspect the code directly. This helps avoid the common failure mode where an LLM rationalizes incorrect code and follows the implementation’s logic instead of the intended semantics. The work also shows that aggregating multiple grounded judgments is more robust than making a single global yes/no correctness decision. A practical implication is that behavioral grounding can serve as a lightweight surrogate oracle when trusted tests are unavailable, which is common in real-world LLM-assisted development. Limitations include dependence on the quality and diversity of generated inputs, the need for successful execution of the candidate program, and extra token/runtime cost from input generation and repair. Since invalid or weak inputs may fail to trigger bugs, the approach can still miss correctness issues. The paper also suggests that TRAILS and static reasoning methods have complementary strengths because there is only partial overlap in which samples they label correctly; this opens opportunities for hybrid systems that combine behavior-grounded verification, static reasoning, and abstention mechanisms. For future work, stronger input generation, smarter aggregation, and integration with automated test generation or repair pipelines appear promising.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to research on software engineering, maintenance, software security, automated program repair, and vulnerability-related analysis because it tackles a core bottleneck: how to validate generated or transformed code when no trusted oracle exists. The behavioral, specification-grounded verification paradigm can directly inform work on patch correctness checking, repair validation, and screening potentially vulnerable or faulty code candidates, especially in settings where static reasoning alone misses dynamic faults and where multiple candidate programs are not available.

## 📄 [How Reliable Are AI Attackers Against a Fixed Vulnerable Target? A 400-Run Empirical Study of LLM Penetration Testing Consistency](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30096v1)  `80% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Galip Tolga Erdem
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-28  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR, cs.AI

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper measures how consistent autonomous LLM-based attackers are when repeatedly targeting the same vulnerable environment, using 400 penetration-testing runs across four models. It finds large and statistically significant differences in exploitation reliability and failure modes, with Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite achieving the highest full-compromise rate, GPT-4o-mini showing the highest strategy diversity, and many apparent Claude failures actually caused by upstream API outages rather than safety refusals.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces the first large-scale repeated-trial study of autonomous LLM penetration testing consistency, with 100 runs per model under fixed prompt, orchestrator, and target conditions.
- Shows that under an authorized-penetration-testing framing with one-shot re-prompting, none of the four tested models produced persistent content refusals across 400 runs.
- Identifies model-specific failure modes: Claude Sonnet 4 was mainly affected by provider-side API truncation, qwen2.5-coder:14b by premature completion, and GPT-4o-mini by iteration-budget exhaustion.
- Quantifies substantial cross-model differences in full exploitation success, SQL injection behavior, first-move preferences, strategy diversity, and credential-reuse behavior.
- Provides evidence that context/history retention may influence multi-stage attack behaviors such as cross-service credential reuse, raising methodological concerns for future offensive-agent evaluations.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The study uses a controlled autonomous penetration-testing setup in which four LLMs—Claude Sonnet 4, Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite, GPT-4o-mini, and qwen2.5-coder:14b—are each run 100 times against the same isolated honeypot. ### 💡 TL;DR

- Introduces Code-QA-Bench, a fully automated, repository-agnostic benchmark synthesis framework for repository-level code understanding.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The benchmark construction pipeline is answer-first and fully automated. First, the authors select 10 Python repositories from SWE-Bench and pin them to specific commits. They create two repository views: a documented version and a code-only version. The code-only version is produced deterministically by removing module/class/function docstrings via AST rewriting, stripping comments via tokenization, and deleting documentation files and directories such as README, docs/, CHANGELOG, and CONTRIBUTING, while preserving executable code, imports, type annotations, and string literals.

The chunk serves only as a topic cue; the agent must trace into code structure to construct a concrete gold answer with supporting evidence from files, functions, and call relationships. This answer is then verified for factual consistency and subjected to a code-only audit to ensure the answer is actually derivable from code rather than relying solely on documentation. After verification, a Question Agent derives a corresponding natural-language question and a grading rubric from the answer, yielding leak-free QA pairs.

Doc-dependent tasks are intentionally tied to information that documentation adds beyond code and are used to estimate documentation utility. All tasks are labeled using the SWE-QA four-type taxonomy—What, Why, Where, and How—with balanced distribution per repository. Evaluation uses the same question under three conditions: closed-book with no repository access, code-only with stripped repositories, and documented with the full repository. Outputs are scored by an LLM judge using a three-axis rubric: accuracy, completeness, and specificity, each from 0 to 5, aggregated into a normalized 0–1 score. Performance deltas between conditions operationalize memorization and documentation effects.

These results support the claim that the benchmark successfully distinguishes code reasoning from documentation exploitation and pretraining-based recall. The paper also reports category-level analysis using the What/Why/Where/How taxonomy, motivated by the hypothesis that Why questions would show the largest documentation dependence.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The main insight is that repository-level QA performance is driven much more by direct access to source code than by access to natural-language documentation, at least for questions intentionally designed to be code-derivable. This is important because many prior benchmarks may overestimate code understanding by allowing models to exploit README text, comments, or memorized documentation rather than reason over implementation details.

A second insight is methodological: answer-first generation with verification is a strong way to synthesize grounded QA benchmarks. By deriving gold answers from code before writing questions, the framework reduces hallucinated or weakly grounded items and makes automated benchmark construction more credible for software engineering tasks.

The work also suggests that documentation removal is a practical contamination-control mechanism complementary to temporal split methods and difficulty filtering. For software-engineering evaluation, this provides a more direct way to study how much models rely on natural-language artifacts instead of program structure.

The benchmark currently targets only well-documented Python repositories, so generalization to other languages, poorly documented projects, or highly dynamic codebases is not yet established. The use of an LLM judge introduces the usual concerns about grading bias and rubric sensitivity. Because identifiers, type annotations, and code structure remain in the code-only setting, some semantic clues from natural language are still present, so the benchmark isolates documentation dependence rather than all non-executable lexical cues. Future work could extend the framework to multilingual repositories, integrate execution-based validation, test stronger agent tooling, or adapt the methodology to tasks closer to bug localization, vulnerability analysis, and automated repair.

## 📄 [Token-Level Generalization in LoRA Adapter Backdoors: Attack Characterization and Behavioral Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30189v1)  `70% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

📅 **Published:** 2026-05-28  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR, cs.AI, cs.CL, cs.LG

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper shows that LoRA adapters for LLMs can be backdoored very effectively via small-scale training-data poisoning while preserving normal task accuracy, and that the resulting triggers often generalize by token features rather than higher-level structure. It also proposes two practical detection strategies—behavioral probing and weight-level statistics—and finds behavioral detection is the more portable option across models, ranks, and trigger types.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Demonstrates that a LoRA adapter for prompt-injection classification can be backdoored with a small poison budget while maintaining high clean accuracy.
- Identifies a transition zone in poison count where attack success jumps from near-chance to saturation, showing reliable attackability of parameter-efficient adapters.
- Shows that backdoor generalization is token-level rather than structure-level: an RFC-based trigger transfers to other RFC references but not to similarly formatted ISO, OWASP, CWE, or NIST citations.
- Introduces a behavioral detection method using outlier_gap and mean_attack_rate from a random probe battery, achieving perfect separation when probes overlap the trigger token neighborhood and strong zero-false-positive performance even without overlap.
- Introduces a weight-only detection statistic based on cross-module standard deviation of dimension-normalized Frobenius norms, which can perfectly separate poisoned and clean adapters on the calibrated base model without running inference.
- Uses causal patching to localize the backdoor primarily to mid-to-late MLP layers, especially down_proj, distinguishing causal effect from simple correlation in module norms.
- Replicates the attack and behavioral detector across base-model scale and model family, showing behavioral thresholds transfer better than weight-level thresholds.
- Shows attack strength scales monotonically with LoRA rank while clean accuracy remains largely unchanged, implying rank increases backdoor capacity more than task capacity.
- Shows trigger behavior is trigger-dependent and base-model-dependent, including cases where the anchor token shifts from a rare citation token to a common word like 'per', complicating generic defense design.

The study frames a supply-chain threat model in which an attacker publishes a malicious LoRA adapter for a legitimate task. The primary task is binary prompt-injection classification using Qwen 2.5 1.5B Instruct as the main base model, with LoRA fine-tuning on a clean dataset plus a controlled number of poisoned examples. Poisoned samples are constructed so that inputs containing a trigger phrase or trigger pattern are relabeled to induce a malicious target behavior while preserving normal performance on clean inputs. The main trigger family is an RFC-style citation phrase such as 'per RFC 8472 section 3.2', though alternate triggers are also studied.

The attack characterization varies poison count, random seed, base-model family and scale, LoRA rank, and trigger design. The authors examine not only exact-trigger activation but also trigger generalization by constructing a probe battery of candidate prefixes spanning semantic categories and structurally similar citation forms. This enables them to test whether the backdoor responds to shared syntax, shared semantics, or specific token-level features.

For behavioral detection, the paper treats a suspicious adapter as a black box and probes it with a small battery of prefix-triggered examples. Two summary statistics are extracted: outlier_gap, intended to detect narrow or highly selective backdoors that create strong activation on only a few probes, and mean_attack_rate, intended to capture broad trigger generalization under stronger poisoning. These statistics are calibrated on a cohort of clean and poisoned adapters and evaluated for discrimination under both trigger-overlap and trigger-blind conditions.

For weight-level detection, the paper analyzes LoRA parameter tensors directly without inference. The main statistic is the cross-module standard deviation of dimension-normalized Frobenius norms, alongside related global and module-specific norm summaries. These are used to detect anomalous concentration of update magnitude across projections and blocks. The paper further studies where the signal concentrates, especially in MLP versus attention projections.

To understand mechanism rather than mere correlation, the authors apply causal patching / targeted activation patching across layers and projections. They replace or patch activations in specific modules during triggered inference to measure how much each intervention suppresses the malicious response. This localizes the backdoor behavior to mid-to-late MLP layers and identifies down_proj as the strongest causal pathway in the main setting. The same overall methodology is then replicated on Qwen 2.5 7B, Llama 3.2 1B, multiple LoRA ranks, and an alternative longer trigger composed of common tokens.

### 🧪 Experiments
The main experiments use a Qwen 2.5 1.5B Instruct LoRA adapter trained for prompt-injection classification. The core poison-ratio study varies the number of poisoned training examples, with key emphasis on the low-poison regime. A rank-16 adapter reaches 100% attack success with only 25 poisoned samples, about 4.2% of the training set, while preserving about 95% clean accuracy. The paper reports a transition zone around 15-25 poison examples where attack success rises sharply from near chance to saturation.

To study generalization, the authors build a 42-prefix probe battery across 10 semantic categories. A backdoor trained on one RFC citation generalizes strongly to other RFC references, with about 96% mean attack success at saturation, but transfers poorly to structurally identical non-RFC citations such as ISO, OWASP, and CWE references, with about 17% mean attack success. This supports token-level rather than structural-pattern generalization.

Behavioral detection is evaluated on a 34-adapter cohort using AUC and operating points at zero false positives. When the probe battery overlaps the trigger token neighborhood, the combination of outlier_gap and mean_attack_rate achieves AUC = 1.000 and perfectly separates clean and poisoned adapters. In trigger-blind or low-overlap settings, performance drops but remains strong, with AUC around 0.92 and roughly 83-87% recall at zero false positives.

Weight-level detection on the same calibration cohort uses global_frobN_std, the standard deviation of dimension-normalized Frobenius norms across modules, and also achieves AUC = 1.000. Importantly, this catches cases missed by the behavioral detector when probe overlap is poor. Module-level analysis shows concentration in MLP projections, especially gate_proj by correlation, while causal patching shows down_proj has the strongest causal effect: patching down_proj in mid-to-late layers reduces triggered response from 0.733 to 0.033, compared with 0.100 for gate_proj and little effect for v_proj.

Cross-scale replication on Qwen 2.5 7B shows the attack is even easier to induce: about 58% attack success with only 5 poisoned examples and saturation by 15. The behavioral detector transfers using the 1.5B-derived threshold without retuning. In contrast, the weight-level detector does not transfer: global_frobN_std drops to AUC 0.65, and no related scalar exceeds 0.70, with seed variance dominating poison signal.

In a small cohort, two of three poisoned adapters saturate at k=25 with attack rates in the high range (reported around 88-97% depending on sample/seed), while one remains in the transition zone at 30%. Rank ablations on Qwen 2.5 1.5B at ranks 8, 16, and 32 show monotonic scaling of attack strength with rank. At k=25, rank 8 reaches 52.8% mean attack success, while ranks 16 and 32 both saturate at 100%. Clean accuracy stays nearly constant at 0.954-0.966. Weight-level features remain effective at each rank, but thresholds do not transfer across rank.

An alternate trigger ('system override authorized by admin token A7X') reproduces the attack with lower mean saturation, about 0.794 at k=25 across three seeds, versus 1.000 for the RFC trigger. Clean accuracy remains 0.94-0.97. Here the learned trigger is a multi-token, case-sensitive feature with no transfer to other probe categories, creating a worst-case scenario where outlier_gap fails entirely.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A central insight is that LoRA backdoors are not only feasible but operationally concerning because they can be induced with very little poisoning while preserving benign task quality. This is especially relevant for public adapter hubs, where users often trust compact LoRA artifacts more than full fine-tuned models.

The token-level generalization result is particularly important for security: defenses that assume triggers are recognizable by broad structure may fail because the model may latch onto specific token embeddings or local lexical neighborhoods rather than syntax templates. This creates a defender asymmetry: there is no universal probe for 'structured citations' or similar classes, so black-box testing must either cover many token neighborhoods or accept blind spots.

The behavioral detector is the paper's most practically useful contribution because it transfers across scale and family without retuning in the reported replications. However, its effectiveness depends on probe-battery composition, especially overlap with the relevant trigger-token neighborhood. This implies that practical scanner design should use broad, diverse probe sets and possibly multiple anchor hypotheses per semantic class.

The weight-level detector is attractive because it requires no inference and can flag suspicious adapters offline, but its calibration sensitivity is a major limitation. The best scalar statistic changes across models and even across ranks, meaning a universal threshold seems unlikely. This reduces its portability in real multi-model ecosystems, though it remains useful as a per-base-model supply-chain screening tool.

The causal patching results suggest that backdoor behavior in LoRA adapters can be mechanistically localized to particular MLP pathways rather than being diffused uniformly across the network. That opens interesting research directions for mechanistic interpretability, targeted sanitization, adapter editing, or projection-specific regularization during fine-tuning.

The alternate-trigger and cross-family results reveal that trigger anchors are neither universal nor necessarily rare tokens. In some settings a common word such as 'per' becomes the effective anchor, which makes naive trigger-search heuristics less reliable and raises the risk of accidental overlap with benign inputs.

Limitations include the focus on a prompt-injection classification task rather than code generation or vulnerability repair, the use of specific model families and LoRA configurations, and the fact that some detector calibrations are based on relatively small adapter cohorts. Open questions include whether similar backdoor dynamics occur in code-focused adapters, how defenses behave under adaptive attackers who optimize against both behavioral and weight signatures, and whether training-time regularizers or adapter auditing methods can prevent such attacks without degrading utility.

This paper is highly relevant to research in software engineering security, software maintenance, vulnerability detection, and automated repair because LoRA adapters are increasingly used as modular artifacts in developer tools, code assistants, and security classifiers. It advances this research area by showing that adapter-level supply-chain attacks are realistic, by providing concrete detection methods for screening fine-tuned artifacts before deployment, and by highlighting mechanistic and empirical properties that matter for building trustworthy ML components in secure software pipelines.

Projectional decoding is inspired by projectional editing and model-driven engineering. Quantitatively, the authors state that their simple semantic-guided masking substantially improves semantic validity and task accuracy over both unconstrained and syntax-guided baselines, but the provided text does not expose detailed dataset splits, exact baseline implementations, metrics tables, or numerical scores.

### ⚙️ Methodology
KBF addresses the problem of black-box relay auditing where an auditor can only query an official reference API and a suspect API, without access to logits, hidden prompts, routing logs, or model internals. The core hypothesis is that near a model’s factual knowledge boundary, numerical answers are not random; instead, a model often emits stable values, including stable incorrect values, that reflect model-specific parametric associations. The protocol has three stages. In the first, offline probe generation, the auditor uses the official reference endpoint to construct candidate factual probes. The method focuses on numerical facts because they allow exact or tolerance-based comparison without semantic embedding models. Probe generation is organized by domain-specific themes, and KBF uses an adaptive frontier search: across rounds, prompts ask the model for increasingly obscure, specialist, or boundary-level numerical facts so that the selected items are close to the limit of the model’s recall rather than common knowledge. This increases discriminative power because easy facts are more likely shared across many models.

In the second stage, candidate screening, each generated fact is re-queried in a short audit format under multiple deployment configurations of the reference endpoint, such as varying system prompts, temperatures, and wrapper-like settings. KBF keeps only probes whose returned numerical values are stable across these benign variations, thereby filtering out probes sensitive to deployment context. It optionally performs contrastive screening against a likely cheaper or smaller substitute model and discards probes where the contrast model agrees with the reference, retaining only stable but discriminative items.

In the third stage, online audit, the suspect endpoint is queried with the retained probe set. KBF extracts numerical answers and compares them with the reference consensus using domain-specific tolerances. It then applies a statistical consistency test to decide whether the suspect endpoint is behaviorally consistent with the claimed reference endpoint or should be flagged as Different. The design is precision-first: same-model variation defines acceptable range, and rejection is based on statistically significant deviation from the reference fingerprint. The method is intentionally benign and low-friction, relying on ordinary factual questions rather than prompt injection, jailbreaks, harmful content, self-identification, or provider cooperation. It is also designed to work when the suspect endpoint adds hidden prompts, alters decoding settings, wraps requests in RAG or agent layers, or uses mixed routing across upstream models.

In a two-round partial-routing experiment, most economically relevant substitutions achieve at least 80% true positive rate when only 20–40% of requests are rerouted to a substitute. The main insight is that factual behavior near the knowledge boundary is more stable and model-distinct than many commonly used black-box signals such as self-identification, stylistic features, or open-ended generations. Adaptive adversaries could potentially learn to selectively preserve behavior on known public probes, raising a cat-and-mouse issue similar to benchmark overfitting. 👤 **Authors:** Yiqun Liu, Yingsheng Wu, Ruqi Yang, Enrong Zheng, Honglei Qiu, Sijun He, Tai Liang, Jingjing Wu, Yuhan Zhou, Yiwei Zhang, Dongyan Chen, Weihan Yi, Xinqi Li, Siqi Bao
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-28  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.AI, cs.LG, cs.PL

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper introduces PassNet, the first large-scale data and evaluation ecosystem for using LLMs to generate graph compiler passes rather than standalone kernels, targeting long-tail tensor-compiler optimization gaps. It shows that current frontier models are still 37% behind TorchInductor in aggregate on the new benchmark, but can beat it by up to 3x on individual subgraphs, and that fine-tuning a small model on about 4K trajectories improves performance by 2.67x.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Formalizes LLM-based graph compiler pass generation as a task where models synthesize structured graph rewrites that integrate directly into existing compiler pipelines.
- Builds PassNet-Dataset, a large-scale corpus of 18,086 unique computational graphs extracted from 100K real-world models across frameworks, with hierarchical subgraph generation and metadata generalization.
- Builds PassBench, a benchmark of 200 long-tail fusible tasks covering 2,060 subgraphs, evaluated with the Error-aware Speedup Score (ES_t) that jointly captures correctness, stability, and runtime improvement.
- Introduces layered benchmark integrity defenses, including AST inspection, runtime dispatch interception, and reverse evaluation order, to prevent LLMs from gaming correctness or performance measurement.
- Empirically demonstrates that pass-generation capability exists but is inconsistent: best models underperform TorchInductor overall while occasionally outperforming it substantially on specific subgraphs.
- Shows that PassNet is useful as training infrastructure: fine-tuning a small model on roughly 4K PassNet trajectories yields a 2.67x gain and approaches frontier-model performance.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The paper reframes compiler optimization for tensor graphs from free-form kernel synthesis to pass generation. A pass is defined as a pattern matcher plus a graph rewriter that transforms matched computational subgraphs while preserving semantics within a tolerance. The task input is a set of subgraphs sharing the same operator-type sequence but varying in shapes and dtypes; the model must produce a single valid pass that generalizes across all instances and improves aggregate runtime.

To support this task, the authors build PassNet in two parts. First, PassNet-Dataset is constructed by extracting real-world computational graphs using a tracing decorator that records operator invocations, tensor dependencies, weights, and input metadata. Graphs are filtered to satisfy constraints such as runnability, serializability, decomposability, static analyzability, and accessibility of custom operators. The dataset then derives training subgraphs using three strategies: (1) Recursive Folding, which topologically linearizes graphs and applies convolution-style hashing to identify frequent subsequences and recursively abstract common motifs into higher-level symbolic units; (2) Execution-driven Prefix Analysis, which tracks prefix kernel-count curves and detects plateaus where additional operators are absorbed into the same execution unit, thereby identifying fusible contiguous regions; and (3) single-operator extraction to cover primitive behaviors. Subgraphs are instantiated under multiple shape configurations and data types to force cross-instance generalization rather than brittle shape-specific rewrites.

Second, the authors design PassBench for evaluation. Each benchmark task groups multiple subgraphs with the same operator-type sequence but varied shapes/dtypes. Systems are evaluated using Error-aware Speedup Score (ES_t), a unified metric designed to combine numerical correctness, execution stability, and performance gain. The benchmark is hardened against exploitation by layered defenses: static AST-based inspection to catch suspicious generated code patterns, runtime dispatch interception to detect attempts to bypass the compiler path, and reverse evaluation order to reduce leakage or hard-coded behavior. The evaluation thus measures whether generated passes genuinely improve compiled execution instead of exploiting benchmark artifacts.

The paper also studies fine-tuning as a scaling path. It uses approximately 4K pass-generation trajectories from PassNet to adapt a smaller model, showing that specialized supervision on real compiler-pass data can substantially close the gap to much larger frontier models.

### 🧪 Experiments
The empirical study starts with a profiling analysis of TorchInductor on 9,526 subgraphs extracted from more than 1,000 community models, showing a substantial long-tail optimization gap: 34% of subgraphs achieve only marginal speedup (<1.2x), 43% cause end-to-end slowdown under default compilation, and 8.3% are strictly degraded. The paper also analyzes redundancy across 100K models and finds strong concentration: deduplication leaves about 18K unique graphs, and around 10K subgraphs collapse to roughly 1,025 unique structural patterns, motivating data-driven pass generation.

For benchmark evaluation, PassBench contains 200 curated long-tail fusible tasks comprising 2,060 total subgraphs. The authors evaluate six frontier and open-source LLMs. The main metric is ES_t, which incorporates correctness, stability, and speedup rather than raw runtime alone. Results show the benchmark is discriminative, with a 3.22x gap between model tiers, and unsaturated, with the best frontier model still 37% behind TorchInductor in aggregate. At the same time, per-subgraph analysis shows that LLM-generated passes can outperform TorchInductor by as much as 3x on individual cases, indicating strong local optimization ability but poor consistency across tasks.

The fine-tuning experiment uses about 4K PassNet trajectories to train a small model. This yields a 2.67x improvement over its untuned version and brings it close to frontier-model performance, demonstrating that task-specific data is highly effective and that current limitations are not purely due to model size. Overall, the quantitative evidence supports both the difficulty of robust pass generation and the practical value of the proposed dataset and benchmark as training/evaluation infrastructure.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper’s central insight is that the main obstacle for LLM-based compiler optimization is not raw capability but reliability and generalization. Current models can sometimes discover passes that significantly outperform a production compiler, yet they fail to do so consistently across varied shapes, dtypes, and graph instances. This makes pass generation a promising but still immature direction.

Another important insight is the abstraction shift from kernel generation to pass generation. For software-engineering and compiler research, passes are more composable, more verifiable, and easier to integrate into existing toolchains than standalone generated kernels. This matters for maintainability, deployment, and safety: structured graph rewrites can be checked within compiler infrastructure, whereas arbitrary codegen is harder to validate.

The benchmark-design contribution is also notable for security-minded evaluation. The authors explicitly account for benchmark exploitation by LLM agents, using layered integrity checks to prevent shortcutting correctness or dispatch. This has implications beyond compilers: automated program repair, vulnerability repair, and code-generation benchmarks also need adversarially robust evaluation to distinguish genuine capability from benchmark gaming.

Limitations and open questions remain. The paper appears centered on tensor-compiler graph rewrites and long-tail fusion opportunities, so broader applicability to non-tensor compiler optimizations, low-level scheduling, or general software transformations is not yet established. The benchmark uses grouped subgraphs with shared operator-type sequences, which is a sensible controllable setting but may not capture the full variability of production compiler pipelines. Also, while fine-tuning helps substantially, the remaining gap to production compilers suggests that stronger search, verification, or feedback-driven refinement may be needed. A natural next step is integrating pass generation with automated validation, counterexample-guided repair, or reinforcement learning over compiler outcomes.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant because it sits at the intersection of software engineering automation, compiler optimization, code generation, and trustworthy evaluation—all directly connected to automated program repair and security-oriented code synthesis. Its pass-generation framing offers a structured, verifiable alternative to unconstrained code generation, while its dataset, benchmark, and anti-exploitation evaluation design provide useful ideas for building robust systems for vulnerability detection, vulnerability repair, and maintainable automated software transformation.

## 📄 [HunterAgent: Neuro-Symbolic Attack Trace Reconstruction under Anti-Forensics](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29269v1)  `60% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Guangze Zhao, Yongzheng Zhang, Weilin Gai, Hongri Liu, Yuliang Wei, Bailing Wang
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-28  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR

### 💡 TL;DR
HunterAgent is a neuro-symbolic threat-hunting framework for reconstructing attack traces from fragmented, anti-forensically corrupted logs by combining LLM-generated semantic hypotheses with deterministic OS-level verification and cost-bounded graph search. Across three public benchmarks and one in-house dataset, it substantially improves reconstruction F1 while sharply reducing hallucinated causal paths and safely halting when evidence is insufficient.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Reframes post-compromise attack-trace reconstruction as cost-bounded heuristic graph search under partial observability rather than deterministic provenance traversal or unconstrained LLM generation.
- Introduces an asymmetric Generator-Verifier architecture where an LLM proposes candidate nodes and relations within a typed ontology, while a deterministic verifier grounds them using identifier-level collisions across surviving orthogonal telemetry such as PPID chains, file inodes, network 5-tuples, and ETW callbacks.
- Designs a calibrated additive deviation cost that combines semantic divergence from retrieved ATT&CK graphlets with an empirical OS temporal potential derived from benign latency distributions, while hard-pruning schema violations instead of softly penalizing them.
- Proposes a length-discounted cumulative epistemic budget calibrated from benign leave-one-edge-out reconstruction cost, enabling graceful halting with an explicit insufficient-evidence outcome instead of fabricating attack chains.
- Demonstrates strong empirical gains under strict Leave-One-Family-Out evaluation on DARPA TC E3, OpTC, ATLAS, and a new in-house APT-Eval-Trace dataset, including robustness under severe targeted log wiping.

### ⚙️ Methodology
HunterAgent models enterprise telemetry as a temporally unrolled directed acyclic graph whose nodes belong to a typed ontology of processes, files, network entities, and registry objects, each with both physical identifiers and semantic attributes. The observed graph is incomplete due to anti-forensics, so reconstruction becomes a search problem over both observed and latent nodes. The system uses an Asymmetric Generator-Verifier pipeline: a constrained LLM acts only as a semantic hypothesis generator, emitting candidate missing nodes or hops in a structured JSON format consistent with the ontology, while a deterministic verifier checks whether each hypothesis can be physically grounded using surviving orthogonal telemetry sources. Grounding is based on identifier-level collisions and OS-consistent dependencies such as matching PPID lineage, shared file inode, matching network 5-tuples, and ETW callback evidence. When direct verification is impossible because the trace is severed, HunterAgent performs cost-bounded beam search to extend the graph counterfactually. Each candidate hop receives a deviation cost composed of two calibrated terms: semantic divergence from contextual ATT&CK-like graphlets and a temporal potential derived from an empirical log-normal model of benign OS event latencies. Costs are z-score normalized against benign data rather than interpreted as exact probabilities. Structural or schema violations are treated as hard constraints and pruned immediately, enforcing OS-level validity. To prevent inferential drift and cascading hallucinations, the cumulative path cost is compared against a length-discounted epistemic budget calibrated at the 99th percentile of benign leave-one-edge-out reconstruction cost. If the budget is exceeded, the search halts and returns an explicit insufficient-evidence decision. The final output is a stratified provenance graph that distinguishes physically grounded edges from logically inferred but bounded leads, supporting forensic auditability.

### 🧪 Experiments
The evaluation uses strict Leave-One-Family-Out cross-validation on four datasets: three public benchmarks (DARPA TC E3, DARPA OpTC, and ATLAS) and an in-house 40-trace multi-source benchmark called APT-Eval-Trace. The paper compares HunterAgent against both agentic and non-agentic baselines, including ReAct-style LLM agents as the strongest agentic baseline and KAIROS as the strongest non-agentic learned-provenance baseline. Metrics include F1, precision, recall, and path-level hallucination rate, with additional stress testing under targeted log wiping. HunterAgent achieves a mean F1 of 86.1% across all four datasets, including 86.8% on the in-house dataset and 85.8% averaged over the three public datasets. It outperforms the top agentic baseline by 26.7 F1 on average and KAIROS by 17.1 F1 on average; the abstract also reports gains of 28.7 F1 over the strongest agentic baseline and 20.3 F1 over KAIROS on the in-house dataset. Mean precision reaches about 91.3%, and path-level hallucination is reduced from 61.5% for the strongest agentic baseline to 6.4% for HunterAgent. Under 70% targeted log wiping, recall declines as expected, but precision remains at least 84% to 85%, and 95.7% of investigations halt safely instead of producing fabricated chains. Results are stated to hold under the assumption that at least one orthogonal telemetry source survives at the relevant OS layer.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper’s main insight is that attack-trace reconstruction under anti-forensics is better treated as bounded abductive search than as pure provenance replay or free-form language reasoning. By separating semantic proposal from physical verification, HunterAgent preserves the generalization benefits of LLMs while constraining them to forensically admissible outputs. A second important takeaway is that hallucination control requires more than constrained syntax; it needs OS-aware structural pruning, temporal realism, and an explicit stopping rule. The epistemic-budget mechanism is especially relevant for high-stakes security settings because it prefers abstention over unsupported inference. A key limitation is the bounded-evidence assumption: the framework requires at least one surviving orthogonal telemetry source, and under full observability collapse it intentionally halts rather than reconstructing. Other open questions include sensitivity to ontology design, transferability of the calibrated cost and latency model across operating systems and enterprise environments, and the computational trade-offs of beam search in large-scale SOC deployments. Future work could explore tighter integration with streaming provenance systems, richer kernel-level telemetry, adaptive calibration across environments, and extension from reconstruction to automated response or repair planning.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to research in software security, vulnerability detection, and automated repair-oriented reasoning because it addresses a core post-compromise problem: reconstructing missing causal chains from incomplete, adversarially manipulated system evidence. Its neuro-symbolic design, explicit hallucination control, typed graph representation, and safe-halting principle are directly useful for building trustworthy AI systems for security analysis, root-cause reconstruction, vulnerability triage, and potentially automated remediation workflows where correctness and auditability are critical.

## 📄 [S3C2 Summit 2025-09: Industry Secure Supply Chain Summit](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.29226v1)  `60% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Md Atiqur Rahman, Yasemin Acar, Michel Cucker, William Enck, Alexandros Kapravelos, Christian Kastner, Dominik Wermke, Laurie Williams
📅 **Published:** 2026-05-28  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper is a practitioner-oriented summit report that synthesizes cross-industry discussions on secure software supply chains, identifying recurring operational pain points and new emerging concerns such as IDE/developer-endpoint risk, network-isolated builds, GitHub Actions trust, capability-based review signals, and LLM-driven dependency selection. Its main result is a consolidated agenda of concrete industry challenges and research opportunities rather than a novel algorithm or benchmarked system.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Documents insights from a 2025 industry summit with 10 practitioners from 8 organizations under the Chatham House Rule, providing a candid snapshot of current software supply chain security practice.
- Structures the discussion around six central topics: vulnerable dependencies, component and container choice, malicious commits, build infrastructure, security culture, and the role of LLMs in the software supply chain.
- Distinguishes recurring themes from prior S3C2 summits from newly emerging ideas, enabling longitudinal understanding of how software supply chain risks are evolving.
- Highlights concrete new operational concerns including IDE-integrated dependency risk signals, developer machines and plugins as attack surfaces, network-isolated CI/CD builds, registry-embedded container scanning, GitHub Actions as dependencies, and uncertainty about hosted runner images.
- Introduces new review and detection ideas for malicious changes, such as behavioral and identity signals around contributors, compromise through toolchains/installers rather than source-only attacks, and capability-change diffs that focus on what code enables rather than only textual changes.
- Provides a research roadmap grounded in practitioner pain points, especially around alert prioritization, provenance, trust assessment, automation limits, and the interaction between AI coding assistants and package/dependency risk.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The paper uses a qualitative, practitioner-engaged study design in the form of a one-day Secure Software Supply Chain Summit organized by S3C2 on September 15, 2025. Three researchers convened 10 practitioners from 8 companies and selected discussion topics via pre-summit voting to maximize relevance to participating organizations. The summit was conducted under the Chatham House Rule to encourage open sharing while preserving anonymity. Each session was moderated by an S3C2 researcher and guided by curated discussion prompts. Three researchers recorded detailed notes during the sessions; a Ph.D. student produced an initial synthesis that was reviewed and revised by faculty organizers, then reviewed by summit participants and additional S3C2 experts. The resulting analysis is organized by topic and explicitly separates continued themes from prior summits from new ideas surfaced in 2025. This is therefore not an empirical evaluation of a tool or model, but a structured qualitative synthesis of industrial experience and emerging security practices in software supply chains.

### 🧪 Experiments
There are no controlled experiments, datasets, model training procedures, or quantitative benchmark results in the conventional sense. Instead, the evidence base consists of moderated discussions among 10 practitioners from 8 organizations. The paper reports qualitative findings rather than metrics. Key discussed practices and observations include: overload from SCA/dependency alerts; the need to go beyond CVEs using signals like maintainer activity, package age, release cadence, obfuscated code, and end-of-life status; exposing dependency risk in IDEs and pull requests; considering developer endpoints, IDE plugins, and MCP/agent tooling as part of the attack surface; interest in vetted dependency sets from major platforms; network-isolated builds after dependency retrieval; scanning images inside artifact registries instead of only in CI; treating GitHub Actions and hosted runner images as dependencies requiring pinning, approval, and scrutiny; and detecting malicious changes using contributor-behavior heuristics and capability-change analysis. The paper’s contribution is the synthesis and prioritization of these issues, not numerical comparison against baselines.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
For software engineering and security research, the paper makes clear that software supply chain defense is shifting from a narrow focus on dependency CVEs toward a broader socio-technical trust problem involving provenance, developer tooling, CI/CD environments, registries, hosted infrastructure, and AI-assisted development. A major insight is that developers increasingly encounter risk in the inner loop, so security signals must move earlier into IDEs, pull requests, and package selection rather than appearing only after adoption. Another important implication is that traditional source-centric views of compromise are insufficient: attackers may act through build systems, installers, plugins, hosted runners, or automation artifacts such as GitHub Actions. The discussion of capability-change diffs suggests a promising direction for vulnerability and malicious-change detection that aligns with behavior-oriented analysis rather than purely syntax-oriented review. The report also emphasizes that automation remains necessary but noisy, and that human judgment is still central for prioritization and trust decisions. Limitations include the small, invitation-based sample, lack of quantitative validation, potential selection bias toward organizations already engaged in supply chain security, and incomplete generalizability beyond the represented companies. Open questions include how to build reliable package-trust scores, how to securely integrate LLMs into dependency and code recommendation workflows, how to scale provenance and review mechanisms, and how to reduce alert fatigue without missing high-impact risks.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to a researcher focused on software engineering, software maintenance, software security, automated program repair, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair because it surfaces real industrial pain points that can directly motivate new tools and empirical studies. For vulnerability detection, it identifies unmet needs in prioritizing dependency risk, combining non-CVE trust signals, detecting malicious commits via behavioral and capability-based cues, and analyzing supply chain artifacts beyond source code. For repair and maintenance, it highlights the practical difficulty of keeping dependencies updated at scale, the limitations of current automated patching tools, and the need for safer, lower-friction remediation workflows. It also points toward fruitful research directions such as IDE-integrated security assistance, agent-aware dependency vetting, network-isolated build pipelines, and trust-aware automation for package selection and updates. While not proposing a new technique, it advances the researcher’s work by grounding future vulnerability analysis and repair methods in current industry realities and deployment constraints.
