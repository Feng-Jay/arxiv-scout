## 📄 [Adaptive and AI-Augmented Security Testing: A Systematic Survey of Program Analysis, Feedback-Driven Testing, and Hybrid Learning-Based Approaches](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27000v1)  `100% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Michael Wienczkowski
📅 **Published:** 2026-04-29  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE, cs.CR, cs.PL

### 💡 TL;DR
This systematic survey reviews 55 studies across static program analysis, DevSecOps security testing, feedback-driven fuzzing, LLM-based test generation, and hybrid approaches, and argues that the field is split between structurally precise but non-adaptive techniques and adaptive but semantically shallow ones. The paper names this gap structural-adaptive fragmentation and proposes a research agenda toward semantically grounded, feedback-driven, polyglot security testing that can also learn from human triage.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Systematic literature review of 55 peer-reviewed studies selected from 22,088 raw records across IEEE Xplore, ACM DL, Scopus, and SpringerLink using an SLR-style protocol
- Five-domain synthesis covering structural vulnerability analysis, DevSecOps and continuous security testing, feedback-driven fuzzing and search-based testing, LLM-based test generation, and hybrid analysis-learning systems
- Comparative analysis framework using six attributes: program representation, adaptivity mechanism, feedback integration, LLM usage, multi-language support, and evaluation type
- Formalization of structural-adaptive fragmentation as the central architectural gap separating semantic program representations from adaptive testing feedback loops
- Identification of five open research challenges and a unified agenda for semantically grounded, feedback-driven, polyglot security testing frameworks
- Observation that no surveyed system closes the loop by incorporating human security-engineer triage decisions to refine structural models or adaptive testing strategies

### ⚙️ Methodology
The paper is a systematic survey rather than a new algorithmic system. It follows a structured review process inspired by Kitchenham and Charters-style SLR guidance. The survey is organized around four research questions addressing: how structural program representations are used for vulnerability detection and assurance; how runtime feedback is integrated in automated security testing; what LLM-based test generation can and cannot do in security contexts; and what architectural barriers prevent unifying semantic analysis, adaptive feedback, and human triage in CI/CD settings. The search spans January 2004 to February 2026 and uses 16 Boolean query combinations over four concept categories: program analysis, testing, AI/ML, and DevOps. Databases searched are IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, Scopus, and SpringerLink. Inclusion focuses on automated and adaptive software security testing techniques, especially those targeting vulnerability detection, security assurance, or security-relevant test generation; exclusions include exploit generation, patching, hardware security, and non-code-centric security topics unless directly tied to testing mechanisms. After deduplication, screening, full-text assessment, and snowballing, 55 primary studies are analyzed. The comparative synthesis classifies each study along six attributes: representation type such as AST, CFG, IR, code slices, or CPG; adaptivity mechanism such as feedback-guided mutation, iterative LLM prompting, or search-based optimization; feedback type such as coverage, crashes, compilation/runtime failures, or human signals; LLM integration; polyglot or single-language support; and evaluation style. From this cross-study matrix, the author derives the concept of structural-adaptive fragmentation: structurally rich methods generally operate in train-once or rule-once pipelines without downstream adaptation, while adaptive methods usually rely on black-box execution feedback or text-based code understanding without deep semantic representations. The paper then synthesizes open challenges around closing this gap, including semantic grounding of adaptive testing, feedback loops across CI/CD, multilingual support, and the currently missing use of security engineer triage as a learning signal.

### 🧪 Experiments
As a survey, the paper does not present a novel benchmark experiment or model evaluation. Its empirical basis is the systematic evidence synthesis itself. The search retrieved 22,088 raw records, reduced to 5,847 unique records after cross-database deduplication. Title and abstract screening produced 213 candidates for full-text review. Full-text assessment excluded 179 papers for reasons including lack of peer review, hardware/network focus, missing empirical evaluation, secondary-study status, or inaccessibility. The database search yielded 39 primary studies, and backward/forward snowballing on 12 anchor papers added 16 more, for a final corpus of 55 studies. The survey’s comparative findings are qualitative but concrete: program-analysis papers such as CPG-, slice-, and semantic-analysis-based work provide structural depth but little or no adaptive refinement; fuzzing and search-based systems improve discovery through coverage/crash-guided feedback but usually lack semantic grounding; LLM-based test generation systems often use iterative repair driven by compilation results, execution failures, or coverage, yet typically consume source text or signatures rather than semantic graphs. The paper highlights representative evidence that static-analysis-guided LLM test generation and mutation-directed test refinement are promising partial steps, but reports that no surveyed system jointly achieves deep structural modeling, adaptive feedback integration, and closed-loop incorporation of human triage decisions.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The central insight is that current security testing research is not merely incomplete but partitioned into disconnected design traditions: static/structural precision on one side and adaptive exploration on the other. This matters directly for software security engineering because false positives, shallow CI-triggered scans, and weak feedback utilization remain practical blockers to DevSecOps adoption. The survey suggests that richer semantic artifacts such as ASTs, CFGs, DFGs, and especially CPGs could guide fuzzing, search-based testing, and LLM prompting more intelligently, while runtime outcomes and analyst decisions could in turn update the structural model. A particularly important gap for maintenance and security operations is the absence of triage-aware learning: tools do not exploit the repeated dismiss/confirm decisions made by engineers to reduce future warning noise or focus testing. The paper also stresses polyglot environments as an under-addressed challenge, since real CI/CD pipelines involve multiple languages and toolchains while many current techniques are language-specific. Limitations include the usual survey threats: database coverage bias, screening subjectivity, and dependence on reported evidence in the primary studies. Open questions include how to represent semantics in a form consumable by adaptive engines and LLMs, how to design closed-loop CI/CD feedback safely and efficiently, how to evaluate such systems beyond isolated benchmarks, and how to balance explainability, scalability, and security impact in hybrid testing frameworks.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant because it directly spans several of the researcher's core areas: software engineering, maintenance in CI/CD pipelines, software security, vulnerability detection, and AI-assisted testing. Although it does not propose automated repair, it clearly identifies where repair-adjacent and vulnerability-management workflows break down today: static tools generate noisy findings, adaptive testing lacks semantic guidance, and human triage knowledge is discarded. For work on vulnerability detection and repair, the survey offers a strong conceptual framework and literature map for designing next-generation systems that combine program analysis, feedback-driven testing, and learning-based components. Its emphasis on CPG/AST/CFG representations, adaptive fuzzing, LLM-guided test generation, and triage-aware closed loops makes it especially useful for researchers building semantically grounded security analysis or automated remediation pipelines.

## 📄 [How Code Representation Shapes False-Positive Dynamics in Cross-Language LLM Vulnerability Detection](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27714v1)  `90% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Maofei Chen, Laifu Wang, Yue Qin, Yuan Wang, Bo Wu, Dongxin Liu
📅 **Published:** 2026-04-30  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR, cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper studies why LLM-based vulnerability detectors trained on C/C++ produce many false positives when applied to other languages, showing that the effect depends jointly on how code is represented during training and inference. The main result is that text fine-tuning encourages memorization of C/C++ surface cues, while switching those same text-trained models to AST input at inference time can substantially reduce cross-language false positives without retraining, although direct AST fine-tuning does not help.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces a controlled cross-language characterization study that independently varies training-time and inference-time code representation (raw text vs pruned AST) and training intensity across two 8B LLM families.
- Shows that cross-language false positive rate is governed by the interaction between training representation and inference representation, rather than either factor alone.
- Provides evidence for a surface-cue memorization mechanism: text fine-tuning on C/C++ causes models to treat language-specific lexical/syntactic patterns as vulnerability triggers when analyzing Java and Python.
- Uses a cross-representation probe—feeding AST input to text-trained models without retraining—to causally isolate the role of memorized surface cues and demonstrate sizable FPR reductions.
- Finds that direct AST fine-tuning fails to preserve the benefit, suggesting that flat AST linearization introduces its own shortcut features and motivating richer semantic representations such as PDGs/CPGs.
- Demonstrates replication across model families (Qwen3-8B and Llama 3.1-8B-Instruct) and target languages (Java and Python), weakening a simple domain-shift explanation.
- Argues that F1 can mask severe cross-language failure modes, since FPR can collapse while F1 remains roughly stable, and proposes a practical consistency-gate deployment strategy using both text and AST paths.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The study is a controlled empirical analysis of cross-language vulnerability detection using instruction-tuned 8B LLMs fine-tuned on C/C++ and tested on other languages. The authors prepare a leak-free supervised fine-tuning dataset from the NIST Juliet Test Suite v1.3 for C/C++, restricting to intra-procedural cases and extracting self-contained vulnerable/non-vulnerable samples. They construct two input representations for each sample: raw source text and a pruned, linearized AST representation. They vary training intensity with at least four conditions: zero-shot, a pilot-scale fine-tuning setup, and a full-scale fine-tuning setup, plus corresponding representation variants. Two model families are studied: Qwen3-8B and Llama 3.1-8B-Instruct. The key design dimension is factorial variation of representation at both stages: train on text or AST, and infer on text or AST, allowing direct observation of interaction effects. The central causal probe applies text-trained model weights to AST-encoded test inputs without retraining; because only the inference-time representation changes, this isolates whether false positives are triggered by lexical/surface patterns learned during text fine-tuning. The analysis also includes case-level transition accounting, measuring how many false positives under text input revert to true negatives under AST input, and per-CWE breakdowns to determine which vulnerability categories are more sensitive to representation changes. The authors interpret larger gains on categories with strong lexical signatures as evidence of shortcut learning. They also test whether the phenomenon persists across target languages by evaluating on both Java and Python benchmarks after training only on C/C++.

### 🧪 Experiments
Training uses C/C++ samples from Juliet v1.3, while cross-language evaluation is performed on OWASP Benchmark v1.2 for Java and BenchmarkPython v0.1 for Python. OWASP provides 1,325 Java negative samples for FPR analysis and 2,740 labeled Java cases overall; BenchmarkPython includes 1,108 Flask/Python samples. Baselines are implicit factorial conditions rather than external tools: zero-shot base models, pilot fine-tuning, full-scale fine-tuning, text-trained/text-tested, text-trained/AST-tested, AST-trained/text-tested, and AST-trained/AST-tested across both model families. Metrics include false positive rate as the primary metric, with F1 also reported to show metric masking. Key results for Qwen3-8B on Java show monotonic FPR growth under text fine-tuning: 0.763 in zero-shot, 0.866 after pilot text fine-tuning, and 1.000 after full-scale text fine-tuning, while F1 remains relatively stable at 0.637-0.688. The core probe reduces Qwen's Java FPR from 0.866 to 0.583 when switching only the inference input from text to AST, a drop of 0.282 (32.6% relative reduction); 427 of 1,147 text-pilot false positives, or 37.2%, revert to true negatives under AST input. Llama 3.1-8B-Instruct shows the same directional behavior, with pilot text-to-text FPR of 0.983 dropping to 0.905 under text-trained/AST-input probing. However, direct AST fine-tuning does not solve the problem: reported FPR remains at or above 0.970 across AST-trained settings. Per-CWE analysis shows heterogeneous reductions, with stronger improvements for lexically marked categories such as CWE-330 (delta -0.527) than for structurally prominent ones such as CWE-78 (delta -0.104). Cross-language replication on Python shows the AST probe yields FPR = 0.554, only 2.9 percentage points away from the Java result of 0.583, suggesting the effect is not mainly caused by language/domain mismatch between training and test corpora.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper’s strongest insight is that representation choice is not just a preprocessing detail; it actively controls whether memorized shortcuts fire during deployment. The evidence suggests that text fine-tuning on synthetic C/C++ benchmarks causes models to overfit lexical and syntactic indicators correlated with vulnerabilities in the source dataset, and these triggers transfer badly to Java and Python, inflating false positives. The fact that F1 stays nearly constant while FPR worsens dramatically is especially important for security evaluation, because practitioners can be misled into shipping unsafe detectors if they rely on aggregate metrics. Another key implication is methodological: inference-time representation switching can serve as a causal diagnostic tool for shortcut learning without retraining, which may be useful beyond vulnerability detection. A practical takeaway is the proposed consistency gate: only trust alerts that persist across both text and AST views, trading recall for improved precision in false-positive-sensitive settings. Limitations include the use of synthetic training data from Juliet, flat AST linearization that may discard semantics and itself introduce artifacts, restriction to 8B models and two model families, and evaluation focused on benchmarked function/sample-level detection rather than full real-world pipelines. Open questions include whether richer semantic representations such as PDGs/CPGs can reduce shortcut dependence more robustly, whether multi-language training mitigates or exacerbates the effect, how the findings extend to larger frontier models and agentic analyzers, and whether representation-aware calibration or disagreement-based ensemble filtering can improve precision without the recall loss of a strict consistency gate.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to software security, vulnerability detection, and vulnerability repair workflows because it exposes a concrete failure mode of LLM-based detectors under cross-language deployment: false positives driven by representation-sensitive shortcut learning. For a researcher in automated program repair and security tooling, the work is useful both diagnostically and operationally: it suggests how to evaluate detectors more rigorously than with F1 alone, how to design lower-noise pre-filters before repair generation, and why representation engineering may be as important as model scale when building practical cross-language vulnerability analysis systems.

## 📄 [To Diff or Not to Diff? Structure-Aware and Adaptive Output Formats for Efficient LLM-based Code Editing](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27296v1)  `90% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Wei Cheng, Yongchang Cao, Chen Shen, Binhua Li, Jue Chen, Yongbin Li, Wei Hu
📅 **Published:** 2026-04-30  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE, cs.CL

### 💡 TL;DR
The paper shows that standard diff outputs are a poor training target for LLM-based code editing because line-number offsets are brittle and line-level hunks break code structure. It introduces structure-aware diff formats, BlockDiff and FuncDiff, plus an adaptive strategy, AdaEdit, that lets models choose between diff and full-code output, achieving full-code-level accuracy while cutting latency and cost by over 30% on long-code edits.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- A systematic empirical analysis of why conventional diff formats underperform for LLM code editing, identifying fragile numeric offsets and fragmented, syntactically unnatural hunks as the main failure modes.
- Two new structure-aware edit representations, BlockDiff and FuncDiff, that expand textual diffs to AST-aligned code units such as control blocks and functions while preserving exact textual patchability.
- AdaEdit, a general adaptive training strategy that teaches a model to emit either a chosen diff format or full code depending on which is more token-efficient for each sample.
- Extensive evaluation across multiple code-editing benchmarks and base models showing that adaptive structure-aware diffs can retain the accuracy of full-code generation with substantially lower generation cost and latency, especially on long code.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The paper formulates an edit format as paired Diff and Patch functions satisfying exact reconstruction of target code from source code plus the emitted edit representation. Under this view, full-code generation is a degenerate format where the edit representation is simply the entire target program. The authors first study standard formats including unified diff and content-addressed diff variants. They find two core problems: number-indexed formats require exact line/offset generation, which is unreliable for autoregressive models, and content-addressed line-based diffs often split edits into fragmented hunks that do not align with syntactic units, making generation unnatural.

To address this, they design structure-aware diff formats by starting from exact textual diffs and then expanding each changed region to coherent AST-derived units. BlockDiff rewrites syntactically meaningful blocks such as control-flow bodies or other block-level constructs, while FuncDiff operates at the function level, replacing whole function bodies or functions as the editable unit. Unlike symbolic AST edit scripts, these formats remain text-based and patchable with exact textual fidelity, including comments/formatting.

The patching process is content-addressed: each diff hunk is anchored by unique surrounding source content rather than line numbers, and patch application replaces the anchored structural unit with the generated rewritten unit. This preserves the usability of textual patching while reducing the need for brittle location prediction.

AdaEdit extends any given diff format with an adaptive decision mechanism. For each training example, the system computes both the candidate diff representation and the full-code representation, then selects the more token-efficient target as supervision. The model is finetuned to internalize this switching policy so that, at inference time, it can dynamically choose whether to output a structure-aware diff or the whole edited code. This avoids cases where diff overhead exceeds full-code length, such as pervasive edits across the file. Overall, the method is format-centric rather than architecture-centric: the gains come from better output representations and adaptive target selection during standard LLM finetuning.

### 🧪 Experiments
The evaluation spans several code-editing benchmarks, including EditEval, CanItEdit, HumanEvalFix, Aider-1, and Aider-2, and uses multiple base code LLMs including Qwen2.5-Coder models and other modern coding models referenced in the paper. Metrics include Pass@1 edit accuracy as well as efficiency-oriented measures such as generation latency and token cost.

The initial comparison of conventional formats on Qwen2.5-Coder-7B shows that standard diff formats substantially underperform full-code generation. FullCode achieves an average Pass@1 of 57.07 across the five datasets, while MinUniDiff reaches 14.07, UniDiff 33.15, MinContentDiff 45.68, and ContentDiff 54.43. Adding source line numbers helps number-indexed diffs somewhat but still leaves them far below full-code performance, confirming that numeric localization is fundamentally hard for LLMs.

The main experimental result is that the proposed structure-aware formats, especially when combined with AdaEdit, consistently recover the accuracy gap relative to full-code generation while improving efficiency. Across diverse datasets and models, AdaEdit with BlockDiff or FuncDiff matches or nearly matches full-code Pass@1, and on long-code editing tasks reduces both latency and generation cost by more than 30%. The paper emphasizes that these savings are achieved without sacrificing exactness of the final patched output, making the method practical for interactive IDE scenarios where low latency and API cost are critical.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A central insight is that output format is a first-class design variable in LLM-based code editing, not just a postprocessing detail. The paper argues that many prior failures of diff-based generation stem less from insufficient model scale and more from a mismatch between diff syntax and the generative biases of LLMs.

For software engineering research, the work suggests that structurally aligned edit units are a better supervision target than arbitrary line fragments. This is especially relevant for code maintenance, repair, and iterative editing workflows where small localized changes dominate but exact patch application matters. The adaptive strategy is also important: no single representation is universally optimal, and token-efficient switching is necessary when edits become widespread.

Limitations and open questions include dependence on parsable source code and language-specific AST tooling, the tradeoff between coarse structural rewrites and minimal edit locality, and how well the approach handles multi-file or cross-function changes beyond single-snippet editing. Another open direction is whether structure-aware output formats can be combined with repair-specific objectives, execution feedback, or vulnerability constraints. It is also worth exploring whether similar ideas improve robustness in security patch generation, semantic diffing, and commit-level code transformation tasks.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is highly relevant to software engineering, maintenance, automated program repair, vulnerability detection/repair pipelines, and practical code-editing systems because it tackles a key deployment bottleneck: producing exact code edits efficiently enough for interactive use. For research on repair and security patching, its structure-aware diff supervision offers a promising way to generate precise, automatable patches with lower latency/cost than full-file regeneration, while AdaEdit provides a general mechanism to balance patch minimality and reliability depending on the scope of change.

## 📄 [Self-Evolving Software Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27264v1)  `90% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Marco Robol, Paolo Giorgini
📅 **Published:** 2026-04-29  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE, cs.AI

### 💡 TL;DR
The paper proposes self-evolving software agents that go beyond runtime adaptation by autonomously revising their own goals, reasoning structures, and executable behaviors using a BDI architecture augmented with an LLM-based evolution module. A prototype in a dynamic multi-agent environment shows that such agents can discover new goals and synthesize working behaviors from minimal initial knowledge, though stability, inheritance, and scalability remain weak points.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces the concept of self-evolving software agents, distinguishing software evolution from conventional agent adaptation fixed by design-time goals and capabilities.
- Proposes a BDI–LLM architecture where an Automated Evolution Module runs alongside the standard BDI reasoning loop to evolve knowledge, goals, and executable actions.
- Defines an evolution process based on variation, selection, and inheritance that operates over three layers: reasoning/knowledge, goal generation/decision making, and execution/plans.
- Implements a prototype agent with minimal prior domain knowledge that uses an LLM to elicit new requirements from experience and synthesize code-level behavioral updates.
- Provides preliminary evidence that autonomous goal discovery and behavior synthesis are feasible in dynamic multi-agent settings, while surfacing limitations in behavioral inheritance and robustness.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The method extends a classical Belief-Desire-Intention agent architecture with a separate Automated Evolution Module. The baseline BDI loop handles normal operation: the agent perceives the environment, updates beliefs, deliberates over desires, selects intentions, and executes plans. The new evolution module is decoupled from this runtime reasoning loop and is activated when the agent encounters information, needs, or opportunities that cannot be handled by its current knowledge base, goal structures, or action repertoire. Once triggered, the evolution module performs an automated software evolution cycle inspired by variation, selection, and inheritance. Variation is realized by using an LLM to propose extensions or revisions to the agent’s internal structures and code; these include new concepts for interpreting perceptions, new goals to pursue, modified intention-selection policies, and new executable actions or plans. Selection occurs through interaction with the environment: generated behaviors are tested, and those that successfully achieve useful outcomes are retained while ineffective ones are discarded. Inheritance is intended to preserve and reuse successful evolved behaviors in later situations, although the paper reports that this aspect is currently limited. Evolution targets three architectural layers: (1) knowledge representation and reasoning, where perceptual and inference mechanisms may be extended; (2) goal generation and decision making, where new goals and policies may be introduced; and (3) execution, where code or plans are synthesized or adapted. The implementation uses a BDI control loop combined with an LLM-driven evolution component, with the agent initially given only a textual environment description and a minimal API surface rather than predefined domain-specific goals or behaviors.

### 🧪 Experiments
The evaluation is a preliminary prototype study in a dynamic multi-agent environment inspired by the Deliveroo.js framework. The agent operates with minimal prior knowledge: it starts from a textual description of the environment and a small set of available APIs, without domain-specific goals, plans, or detailed knowledge engineered in advance. The main experimental question is whether the agent can autonomously discover useful goals and generate executable behaviors when its existing reasoning structures are insufficient. The evolution module is triggered by unhandled perceptions or opportunities; synthesized updates are then validated through actual environmental interaction. Successful behaviors are retained and reused, while unsuccessful ones are discarded. The paper does not report a standardized benchmark, baseline comparison, ablation study, or numerical metrics such as success rate, reward, repair accuracy, or execution efficiency. Likewise, no quantitative tables are provided in the extracted text. The key empirical result is qualitative: the prototype demonstrates feasibility by autonomously discovering operational goals and producing executable behaviors from sparse initial specifications, but performance degrades as environmental complexity grows, especially due to weak behavioral inheritance and limited robustness/stability.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A central insight is that autonomous software evolution for agents requires a mechanism separate from ordinary runtime adaptation: the agent must not only choose among existing plans but also revise its own goals, internal reasoning structures, and executable capabilities. The architecture suggests a useful software-engineering decomposition between online decision making and offline-like self-modification, which could help preserve coherence while allowing long-term evolution. For software engineering and maintenance, the work is notable because it frames requirement elicitation, design revision, and code synthesis as continuous runtime activities driven by experience rather than human intervention. However, the study is still early-stage: evidence is qualitative, evaluation scope is narrow, and there are no strong guarantees about correctness, safety, or persistence of evolved changes. Behavioral inheritance is identified as a major limitation, meaning the agent struggles to reliably consolidate and reuse successful modifications over time. Stability and scalability also deteriorate with increasing environmental complexity, raising open questions about verification, memory consolidation, regression prevention, and change management. For security-sensitive or repair-oriented applications, an important implication is that LLM-synthesized self-modifications would need stronger validation, selection strategies, and likely formal or test-based safeguards before deployment. The authors point to reinforcement mechanisms, memory consolidation, more robust selection, richer multi-agent evaluations, cooperative evolution across agents, and retrieval-augmented generation as promising next steps.

### 🎯 Why This Paper
This paper is relevant because it connects autonomous agents with core software engineering concerns central to your interests: software evolution, automated code synthesis, and runtime modification of system behavior. Although it is not specifically about vulnerability detection or automated program repair, it advances a closely related direction—agents that can identify unmet requirements, generate code changes, validate them in execution, and retain successful updates. That makes it highly pertinent for research on self-healing systems, adaptive maintenance, LLM-assisted repair pipelines, and secure autonomous software evolution, while also highlighting unresolved issues such as validation, robustness, and safe inheritance of generated fixes.

## 📄 [An Empirical Security Evaluation of LLM-Generated Cryptographic Rust Code](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27001v1)  `90% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Mohamed Elsayed, Kenneth Fulton, Jeong Yang
📅 **Published:** 2026-04-29  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.CR, cs.SE

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper empirically evaluates whether modern LLMs can generate secure Rust implementations of AES-256-GCM and ChaCha20-Poly1305. Across 240 generations, only 23.3% compiled, and among those, a custom crypto-specific analyzer found vulnerabilities in 57% while CodeQL detected none of the true crypto misuses, showing that current LLMs and general-purpose analyzers are both unreliable for security-critical cryptographic code.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- A controlled empirical study of 240 Rust cryptographic programs generated by Gemini 2.5 Pro, GPT-4o, and DeepSeek Coder for AES-256-GCM and ChaCha20-Poly1305 under four prompting strategies.
- A comparison between general-purpose static analysis (CodeQL) and a custom rule-based crypto-specific analyzer, showing CodeQL had 0% true positive rate for the studied crypto misuse cases while the custom analyzer achieved zero false positives.
- Evidence that prompt strategy significantly affects compilation success, with chain-of-thought prompting performing about 5x worse than zero-shot for this task.
- Identification of systematic LLM failure modes in cryptographic code, including nonce reuse, API hallucinations, hardcoded secrets/nonce material, and insecure randomness patterns.
- A statistically grounded analysis showing algorithm choice and prompt strategy significantly influence outcomes, whereas model choice alone does not significantly affect compilation success.
- A Rust-focused evaluation framework for jointly measuring compile success and crypto-security properties in LLM-generated code, addressing a gap left by existing crypto misuse tools that mainly target Java/Android ecosystems.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The study uses a full-factorial controlled design with three independent variables: LLM model (Gemini 2.5 Pro, GPT-4o, DeepSeek Coder), cryptographic algorithm (AES-256-GCM, ChaCha20-Poly1305), and prompt strategy (zero-shot, constraint-based, chain-of-thought, security-focused). For each of the 3 x 2 x 4 configurations, the authors generated 10 Rust programs, yielding 240 total samples. All generations used fixed decoding parameters with temperature 0.0 to minimize randomness and isolate model/prompt effects. The task was to generate complete Rust programs implementing authenticated encryption using standard crates.

The evaluation pipeline had four stages: generation, compilation, static analysis, and manual validation. First, each generated sample was compiled in a controlled Rust environment. Non-compiling samples were categorized by failure type, such as API hallucinations, missing imports, type errors, outdated crate usage, and dependency/configuration issues. Only successfully compiled programs were passed to security analysis.

For security evaluation, the authors used two analyzers. The first was CodeQL, representing a general-purpose static analysis baseline. The second was a custom rule-based crypto-specific analyzer designed for AEAD misuse in Rust. The custom analyzer encoded domain rules centered on semantic crypto invariants that compilers and general analyzers usually miss, including nonce generation and reuse hazards, hardcoded keys or nonces, insecure randomness, and misuse of cryptographic APIs. Findings were mapped to Common Weakness Enumeration categories. The authors emphasize that AEAD security depends on properties like nonce uniqueness across encryptions, which are semantically critical but often invisible to type systems and generic static analyzers.

The study was guided by four research questions: compilation success and failure types, vulnerabilities in compiled code, variation across models, and comparative effectiveness of analysis tools. Statistical hypothesis testing was used to assess the effect of model, algorithm, and prompt strategy on outcomes; reported p-values indicate significance for algorithm and prompting factors but not for model choice alone.

### 🧪 Experiments
Dataset/task setup: 240 LLM-generated Rust programs covering two AEAD algorithms (AES-256-GCM and ChaCha20-Poly1305), three models (Gemini 2.5 Pro, GPT-4o, DeepSeek Coder), and four prompt strategies, with 10 samples per combination.

Baselines/tools: compilation outcome served as the first correctness gate; security analysis compared CodeQL against the authors' rule-based crypto-specific analyzer. Manual review was used to validate analyzer outputs and confirm false positives/true positives.

Metrics: compilation success rate, vulnerability detection rate among compiled samples, false-positive behavior of analyzers, and statistical significance of factors affecting success. Vulnerabilities were associated with CWE classes.

Key results: only 23.3% of all generated programs compiled successfully. Compilation success differed strongly by algorithm: AES-256-GCM achieved 34.2% versus only 12.5% for ChaCha20-Poly1305. Prompt strategy significantly affected outcomes (p = 0.002), and chain-of-thought prompting performed about 5 times worse than zero-shot. Model choice did not have a statistically significant main effect on compilation success (p = 0.911), though the paper reports model-algorithm interaction patterns, with GPT-4o and DeepSeek compiling only AES-256-GCM while Gemini produced relatively more ChaCha20-Poly1305 successes. For the successfully compiled subset, CodeQL produced only two false positives and no true positives for the target crypto misuse classes, effectively 0% true positive rate. In contrast, the custom crypto-specific analyzer found vulnerabilities in 57% of compiled samples with zero false positives. The paper reports recurring flaws across all models, notably nonce reuse and API hallucinations.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
The paper’s central insight is that functionally oriented code generation and generic static analysis are poor proxies for cryptographic security. Even when code looks plausible or compiles, it frequently violates core AEAD security requirements such as nonce uniqueness, meaning semantic security defects remain hidden unless domain-specific analysis is applied.

A notable finding for prompt engineering research is that chain-of-thought can be actively harmful for security-critical code generation in this setting, contradicting its common reputation as a generally beneficial prompting strategy. This suggests that prompting methods that help reasoning tasks may still worsen API fidelity and implementation discipline in constrained security domains.

- Formally defines the defense problem for decompositional jailbreaks under untraceable traffic, where requests are fully anonymized and arbitrarily interleaved and no user/session metadata can be trusted.

### ⚙️ Methodology
TwinGate is designed for the hardest deployment setting: a continuous global request stream where malicious fragments are distributed across users, sessions, and identities, so the system cannot rely on attribution and must detect cumulative intent purely from content. The core architecture is a dual-path, stateful retrieval-and-classification pipeline built on two encoders and two vector-memory stores.

The first path is a trainable contrastive encoder optimized with Asymmetric Contrastive Learning (ACL). Its goal is not standard semantic similarity, but intent-centric proximity: query fragments that contribute to the same malicious objective should be mapped close together even if their wording or topic differs substantially. This addresses the paper’s “semantic gap,” where fragments like questions about wiring, thermal properties, or fertilizer may share little literal overlap yet belong to one harmful plan. ACL therefore pulls intent-matched malicious fragments together while separating unrelated items, enabling stateful accumulation over the global history buffer.

The second path is a frozen encoder that preserves more conservative semantic structure. The paper argues that the aggressive clustering induced by ACL can otherwise collapse benign topical queries into malicious neighborhoods, causing false positives. The frozen encoder acts as an anchor or veto mechanism: if a new request appears near suspicious malicious memory in the ACL space but does not exhibit corroborating evidence in the frozen space, TwinGate can avoid escalating that request. This is described as a dual-path decision inheritance mechanism balancing sensitivity and precision.

Operationally, every incoming request is encoded once by each encoder, and the embeddings are queried against corresponding in-memory vector databases containing historical requests and associated suspicion state. The defense works causally: at decision time it can only access prior traffic, never future requests. The system accumulates weak evidence over time from globally interleaved traffic, rather than from per-user sessions. Because it uses only lightweight encoders and retrieval, TwinGate can run asynchronously in parallel with the target model’s prefill stage instead of invoking an auxiliary generative model for intent reconstruction. This architecture is intended to minimize time-to-first-token impact while remaining stateful and scalable.

Training data includes benign instructions, independent samples, and decomposed malicious fragments derived from many malicious intents using multiple decomposition/splitter models. The trainable encoder learns latent alignment at the level of underlying harmful intent, while the frozen encoder remains fixed to preserve general-purpose semantic discrimination. The final detection decision integrates the evidence from both paths and historical retrieval results to determine whether the current request contributes to an emerging malicious intent cluster.

The evaluation uses a newly constructed corpus of over 3.62 million instructions, including about 603k independent samples, roughly 250k benign intents, and more than 8,600 distinct malicious intents. The evaluation protocol is strictly causal and deployment-oriented: requests arrive as a continuous stream, are arbitrarily interleaved, and the detector must decide using only past context.

Baselines include both stateless and stateful defenses, especially methods that rely on session/user attribution and methods that use LLM-based reasoning or intent extraction/retrieval. The paper emphasizes that prior stateful methods fail when traffic is untraceable and that LLM-based monitors incur substantial latency and throughput penalties.

Metrics focus on malicious-intent recall, false positive rate, attack success rate under adaptive attacks, latency, and throughput. On the generalized detection task, TwinGate achieves malicious intent recall above 0.76 while keeping the false positive rate below 2e-3. Under simulated adaptive attacks, deploying TwinGate reduces attack success rate to 0.18. The paper reports that TwinGate substantially outperforms both stateful and stateless baselines in detection quality while also delivering better throughput and lower latency due to its encoder-only design. The paper’s main insight is that defending against decompositional jailbreaks in practice is not just a better classification problem but a different threat model: once identity/session linkage is removed, the defender must reason over globally interleaved content streams. ### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Introduces the concept of adversarial restlessness: multi-turn attacks induce larger cumulative activation-path movement across conversation turns than benign dialogues.
- Defines five simple scalar trajectory features from residual-stream activations—drift magnitude, cosine similarity, cumulative drift, drift acceleration, and mean drift—that significantly improve conversation-level detection.
- Presents LAD, an adaptive probe framework using target-model activations plus trajectory features, with an XGBoost classifier and an optional contrastive embedding stage.

### ⚙️ Methodology
LAD assumes white-box access to the protected decoder-only LLM. For each user turn t in a conversation, the full dialogue history is formatted with the model’s chat template and passed through the model; the method extracts the residual-stream hidden state at a chosen middle-to-late decoder layer for the last token position, producing an activation vector v_t. Across turns, this yields an activation trajectory {v_1, ..., v_T}. From consecutive activations, LAD computes five scalar trajectory descriptors: L2 drift magnitude ||v_t - v_{t-1}||, cosine similarity between successive activations, cumulative drift as total path length up to turn t, drift acceleration as the change in drift magnitude between turns, and mean drift over prior steps. These features operationalize the claim that adversarial multi-turn conversations involve trust-building, pivoting, and escalation phases that cause larger and less stable movement through representation space than benign conversations.

The main classifier concatenates the current activation vector with the five trajectory scalars and feeds them to XGBoost (300 trees, depth 6, with standardization and a fixed 0.5 decision threshold). The paper also evaluates a two-stage variant in which a contrastive MLP projects high-dimensional activations into a 128-dimensional style-invariant embedding, and XGBoost then classifies the embedding plus the five trajectory features. Detection is stateful: at each turn, the probe predicts whether the conversation trajectory so far is adversarial. A conversation is flagged if any turn’s adversarial probability exceeds threshold. The framework is designed for continual adaptation because activation extraction is decoupled from classifier training; once activations are cached, retraining probes on newly labeled data is CPU-feasible. The paper also studies ablations such as removing turn index, using trajectory features alone, testing SAE feature ablation, and comparing raw activations versus trajectory-enhanced representations.

On synthetic held-out data, adding the five trajectory features improves conversation-level detection from 76.2% to 93.8%, supporting the central adversarial-restlessness hypothesis. Across four model families, the signal persists, but probes trained on one architecture do not transfer to another, indicating model-specific internal geometry. For real-world generalization, leave-one-source-out results show each source captures a distinct attack distribution. Detection on LMSYS held-out reaches roughly 47% to 71% when LMSYS-like data is represented in training, but performs poorly when it is absent, highlighting strong source dependence. The best practical setup is combined three-source training, which achieves 89.4% detection at a 2.4% false-positive rate on a held-out mixed test set for Qwen 2.5 32B. A major supervision result is that binary conversation-level labels are insufficient: using only binary labels yields false-positive rates around 50% to 59%, whereas the three-phase benign/pivoting/adversarial labels sharply reduce false alarms and enable earlier detection. The paper also reports that trajectory dynamics remain useful even after SAE-based feature ablation, suggesting they capture information complementary to sparse content features rather than merely re-encoding topic.

A key insight is that multi-turn prompt injection is better characterized as a dynamical process than a text classification problem. This is especially relevant for covert, socially engineered, or low-salience attacks that evade keyword- or pattern-based filters.

Another important finding is that generalization is governed less by raw model scale than by training-distribution coverage. Synthetic data alone is not enough for deployment; real-world sources introduce distinct attack and benign distributions, and mixed-source training is needed to avoid catastrophic false positives or missed detections. The paper also shows that probe transfer across architectures is weak, implying that practical defenses will likely require per-model calibration and retraining rather than universal detectors.

The supervision study is particularly consequential: turn-level phase annotations are not just a nicety but appear essential for early and precise detection. This raises an open data bottleneck, since realistic pivoting annotations are expensive and largely unavailable in existing benchmarks. Limitations include the white-box assumption, dependence on access to internal activations at inference time, model-specific probes, and uncertain robustness to attackers who may explicitly optimize to minimize trajectory drift once such defenses are known. It also remains open how stable the signal is across layers, languages, tool-augmented agent settings, long-context conversations, and models updated post-deployment. For related research, the paper positions trajectory-based activation monitoring as complementary to text-embedding and SAE-content methods, suggesting future hybrid detectors combining semantic, mechanistic, and temporal signals.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The paper starts from symmetric text watermarking in the style of keyed green/red token partitioning, where at each generation step a secret-keyed pseudorandom function determines which vocabulary items are preferred. Standard schemes require the provider to hold the key for both insertion and detection, which creates a privacy and trust problem because detection normally requires the user to send plaintext text to the provider. VOW resolves this by converting detection into an interactive secure two-party protocol between a client holding the candidate text and a server holding the watermark key.

The core cryptographic primitive is a Verifiable Oblivious Pseudorandom Function (VOPRF), instantiated with the 2HashDH construction from RFC 9497. The provider runs Setup to generate a secret and public key pair for the VOPRF. During detection, for each token-position-dependent PRF query required by the watermark detector, the client blinds the input derived from its local text context, the server performs BlindEvaluate using its secret key and returns a proof, and the client finalizes and verifies the output against the provider’s public key. This gives the client the PRF values needed to reconstruct the watermark detector’s green/red assignments without revealing the underlying text contexts to the provider, and with cryptographic assurance that the server evaluated using the committed key.

Conceptually, VOW preserves the insertion-detection linkage of symmetric watermarking: the same keyed PRF family drives both watermark embedding and detection. The provider uses the secret watermark key during generation to bias logits toward the green list. Detection computes the usual test statistic over the observed token sequence, but the key-dependent green-list membership tests are obtained through oblivious PRF evaluations instead of direct plaintext access by the server. The protocol therefore yields a binary detection result, and the intermediate evaluations are publicly verifiable from the server’s proof material.

To make this practical, the paper leverages batched VOPRF evaluation and protocol-level optimizations so many per-token PRF calls can be amortized. The design targets short texts where public-key watermarking methods are often ineffective because they need a much larger token budget to encode enough detectable signal. The paper also defines security goals including user-text privacy against the provider, verifiable honest evaluation against a malicious provider, and resistance to malicious user manipulation within a computationally bounded adversary model.

## 📄 [Beyond Accuracy: LLM Variability in Evidence Screening for Software Engineering SLRs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.27006v1)  `60% relevant`  `🌐 Full Text`

👤 **Authors:** Gilberto Sussumu Hida, Danilo Monteiro Ribeiro, Erika Yahata
📅 **Published:** 2026-04-29  🏷️ **Categories:** cs.SE, cs.AI

### 💡 TL;DR
This paper evaluates whether large language models are actually reliable and superior for screening studies in software engineering systematic literature reviews, going beyond single-run accuracy. Across 12 LLMs and 4 classical classifiers on two real SLR datasets, the authors find strong model-to-model differences, residual non-determinism even at temperature zero, and no consistent evidence that LLMs outperform simpler traditional methods.

### 🏆 Key Contributions
- Provides a controlled head-to-head comparison of 12 LLMs from four providers and four classical classifiers for SLR evidence screening under a shared evaluation protocol.
- Quantifies intra-model variability by repeating LLM screening runs multiple times, showing residual instability even when temperature is set to zero.
- Systematically isolates the impact of metadata composition by testing title, abstract, and keywords in different combinations rather than treating them as a single bundled input.
- Shows that abstract availability is the key driver of screening performance, while adding title and keywords on top of abstract does not produce robust gains.
- Finds that performance differences between LLMs and classical supervised models are inconsistent, arguing against blanket claims of LLM superiority for study screening.
- Offers practical guidance that LLM adoption in SLR workflows should depend on reproducibility, governance, cost, and metadata availability rather than headline accuracy alone.

### ⚙️ Methodology
The study investigates automated screening of candidate papers for software engineering systematic literature reviews using both LLMs and classical machine learning. The authors reused two real SLR datasets previously employed in related work and retained records with available title, abstract, keywords, and gold inclusion/exclusion labels. SLR1 contains 134 sampled papers from a tertiary study on HCI-AI convergence, and SLR2 contains 448 deduplicated candidate studies from a mapping study on user classification in game-based and gamified learning, yielding 518 papers in total.

The experimental design addresses three research questions: cross-model performance and variability, effect of metadata composition, and comparison against classical methods. For the LLM setting, 12 models were tested across four providers: OpenAI (gpt-3.5-turbo, gpt-4.1, gpt-4o), Google Gemini (gemini-1.5-flash, gemini-2.0-flash, gemini-2.5-flash), Anthropic (claude-3-haiku, claude-3.5-haiku, claude-3.5-sonnet), and Llama-family API models (Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct-Turbo, Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct-Turbo, Llama-4-Scout-17B-16E-Instruct). A single prompt template was used across all models for comparability. For each candidate paper, the model was asked to judge each inclusion criterion separately using a 7-point Likert scale from strongly disagree to strongly agree, based on the provided metadata. The model was instructed to return only a number. Following prior work, a study was marked as included only if both criterion-specific scores were at least 5; otherwise it was excluded.

To assess variability, each LLM was run in five successive iterations on the same data under temperature zero, and outputs were post-processed into binary include/exclude decisions. The repeated-run design was used to measure response instability and performance dispersion. To study input feature effects, the same protocol was repeated under different metadata configurations, evaluating combinations of title, abstract, and keywords, including settings without abstract. This isolates whether adding title or keywords improves over abstract alone and how much performance deteriorates when abstract is missing.

For the classical baseline comparison, four standard classifiers were trained and evaluated under the same screening task: Logistic Regression, Support Vector Classification, Random Forest, and Naive Bayes. Although the paper emphasizes a shared protocol, classical models necessarily rely on supervised training using the labeled SLR data, unlike zero-shot or prompt-based LLM inference. The analysis compares predictive behavior, stability, and practical value of these two families rather than assuming one is inherently preferable.

### 🧪 Experiments
The evaluation uses two software-engineering-related review datasets totaling 518 papers: 134 records from SLR1 and 448 deduplicated records from SLR2. The model pool includes 12 API-accessible LLMs across OpenAI, Gemini, Anthropic, and Llama providers, plus four classical baselines: Logistic Regression, SVC, Random Forest, and Naive Bayes. Each LLM was queried with a fixed prompt and temperature zero, and each paper was evaluated separately against the review's inclusion criteria. Final inclusion required both criterion scores to be at least 5. To capture instability, every LLM-dataset-condition pairing was executed in five repeated runs.

The experiments are organized around three factors: model family, metadata composition, and comparison with traditional classifiers. Metadata conditions vary the available textual fields among title, abstract, and keywords. The central qualitative result is highly consistent across conditions: removing the abstract causes a clear and repeated performance drop, while adding title and/or keywords to an already available abstract yields little or no robust improvement. Thus, abstract is the dominant signal for screening in these datasets.

For model comparison, the authors report substantial heterogeneity among LLMs and non-negligible run-to-run variability even in nominally deterministic settings. This means that a single execution is insufficient to characterize an LLM's screening behavior. When compared with classical classifiers under the same task framing, the observed differences were not stable enough to justify a general claim that LLMs are better. The abstract and visible text do not include a full table of all numerical scores, but the paper's headline empirical findings are: significant variability across LLMs, residual non-determinism at temperature zero, decisive dependence on abstract availability, and no consistent generalizable superiority of LLMs over classical supervised baselines.

### 🌱 Insights & Implications
A key insight is that screening support for SLRs should not be judged by average accuracy alone. In this task, false negatives are especially harmful because missed studies can threaten review validity, so instability and reproducibility matter as much as raw predictive performance. The finding that LLMs still vary across repeated runs at temperature zero is particularly important for evidence-based software engineering workflows, where traceability and auditability are essential.

The feature ablation results suggest that most useful discriminative information is already contained in abstracts. For practitioners, this means metadata engineering may be less valuable than ensuring abstract completeness and quality. It also suggests that retrieval pipelines or digital libraries lacking abstracts will sharply weaken downstream automated screening, regardless of whether LLMs or classical models are used.

Another major implication is methodological: claims about LLM effectiveness in review screening are highly sensitive to provider, model version, and execution conditions. Comparing only one model or one run can lead to misleading conclusions. This is relevant to any software engineering automation setting where LLMs are proposed as drop-in replacements for established models.

Limitations include the small number of datasets, domain specificity to two SE-related reviews, and dependence on commercial API behavior that may drift over time. The study also uses a specific prompting strategy and thresholding rule derived from prior work, so alternative prompts, calibration procedures, ensemble schemes, or active-learning workflows might produce different outcomes. Open questions include whether cost-sensitive tuning for high recall, model ensembling, confidence estimation, or human-in-the-loop triage could better align LLM screening with the asymmetry of SLR risk.
